**"Did You Know?" Facts**
```markdown

```

**Common Mistakes** (use alongside each concept where relevant)
```markdown
> **Watch Out:** [Describe a common biology error — e.g., "Students often say 'plants breathe CO₂ and release O₂ all the time.' But plants also respire 24/7 — they take in O₂ and release CO₂ just like us. Photosynthesis is separate from respiration."]
```

Common biology watch-outs:
- Photosynthesis happens only during the day (needs sunlight); respiration happens 24/7
- "Cell wall" is present in plant cells, not animal cells — but both have cell membranes
- Mitochondria produce energy (ATP), not "food" — food is broken down elsewhere
- Veins carry blood TO the heart; arteries carry blood AWAY — the names don't match common usage ("arteries = red, thick" vs the actual function)
- Ovule becomes the seed; ovary becomes the fruit — students often swap these

### Formatting Rules
- Classification hierarchies: Use indented lists (Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species)
- Cell diagrams: Describe each part and function ("The nucleus is at the center, surrounded by nuclear membrane. It contains chromosomes which carry genetic information.")
- Processes: Numbered steps with "what happens" and "why it happens"
- Disease tables: Disease | Cause | Symptoms | Prevention

### Target Word Count
6K-9K words

### Common Pain Points
- **Too many named structures — organelle functions get mixed up**: Nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi, lysosomes. Students confuse which does what because the names sound similar. Relate each to a "factory department" — nucleus = management, mitochondria = power plant, ribosomes = assembly line. Happens in The Fundamental Unit of Life chapter.
- **Process steps get memorized in the wrong order**: Photosynthesis, respiration, cell division, digestion — steps are similar but the sequence matters. Always present as a numbered flowchart: "First X happens, then Y because of X, then Z." Happens in Life Processes, Tissues chapters.
- **Plant vs animal — "they look the same"**: Plant tissue vs animal tissue, cell wall vs cell membrane. Students confuse which features belong to which. Always use a side-by-side comparison table: "Has this feature → plant. Doesn't have it → animal." Happens in Tissues, The Fundamental Unit of Life chapters.
- **Classification hierarchy — "why these groups?"**: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. Students memorize levels but don't understand the logic. Explain: "The more characteristics two organisms share, the closer they are in the classification. It's not random — it's based on evolution." Happens in Diversity in Living Organisms chapter.
- **Disease vs infection vs symptom — using terms interchangeably**: "I have a cold" vs "I have a cold virus" vs "I have a fever". Students don't separate cause, condition, and sign. Decision rule: "Disease = the condition. Infection = what caused it. Symptom = what you feel." Happens in Why Do We Fall Ill? chapter.
- **"How does a single cell become a whole organism?"**: Cell division, differentiation, tissue formation — students see these as separate but don't connect the chain. Show: "Division → more cells → cells become different → tissues → organs → systems." Happens in Tissues, The Fundamental Unit of Life chapters.

### Mnemonics Guidance

Biology-specific mnemonic patterns to use in Section 9:

- **Cell organelles — factory analogy**: "N = nucleus = management. M = mitochondria = power plant. R = ribosomes = assembly line. G = Golgi = packaging and shipping. E = ER = transport network. L = lysosomes = waste disposal." — Use the full factory analogy to remember functions.
- **Photosynthesis equation**: "Plants need S W C O² H²O" — Sunlight, Water, Chlorophyll, Carbon dioxide, Water → Glucose + Oxygen
- **Levels of organization**: "C T O S" — Cell → Tissue → Organ → System → Organism
- **Blood components**: "R W P" — Red blood cells (carry O₂), White blood cells (fight infection), Platelets (clotting) — "Red Wagon Platelets"
- **Plant tissue types**: "MPSE" — Meristematic (growth), Permanent (structure), Simple (one cell type), Epidermal (protection)
- **Kingdom classification**: "MPFPA" — Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia — "My Pet Frog Prefers Apples"

For each mnemonic: the mnemonic itself + a 1-sentence explanation of how to recall it.

## Exam Patterns — Science (Biology)

**Paper structure**: Section A (20 MCQs × 1 mark), Section B (6 × 2 marks), Section C (7 × 3 marks), Section D (3 × 5 marks), Section E (3 case-based × 4 marks)
**Total**: 80 marks
**Special question types**: Diagram-based (cell structure, tissue diagrams), activity-based, labeling
**Unit weightage**: Matter (23), Organization in the Living World (20), Motion, Force and Work (27), Food & Food Production (10)

**Sample 1-mark (Biology)**: Which organelle is known as the powerhouse of the cell? → Mitochondria
**Sample 2-mark (Biology)**: What is the difference between osmosis and diffusion? → 1. Diffusion is the movement of particles from high to low concentration in any medium (solid, liquid, gas). 2. Osmosis is the movement of water molecules through a semi-permeable membrane from a region of high water concentration to low water concentration.
**Sample 3-mark (Biology)**: Differentiate between plant cell and animal cell. → 1. Plant cells have a cell wall; animal cells do not. 2. Plant cells have chloroplasts for photosynthesis; animal cells lack them. 3. Plant cells have a large central vacuole; animal cells have small, multiple vacuoles.
**Sample 5-mark (Biology)**: Describe the structure of a plant cell with a diagram. → [Introduction + labeled diagram description + 5 key organelles: cell wall, chloroplast, vacuole, nucleus, cell membrane — function of each]
