# The French Revolution - Detailed NCERT Notes (Version 2)

## 1. Chapter Introduction

### Main Theme
The French Revolution (1789-1799) was a pivotal event that transformed France from an absolute monarchy to a republic based on the principles of Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity. It marked the end of the feudal system and the rise of modern democratic principles that would influence the entire world.

### Historical Importance
The French Revolution is considered one of the most significant events in world history because:
- It ended the absolute monarchy in France
- Established the first modern republic based on democratic principles
- Introduced the concept of popular sovereignty
- Inspired revolutionary movements across Europe and the world
- Created the foundation for modern democratic institutions

### Connection to World History
The French Revolution connected to larger world history through:
- Influence on American Revolution (1776) - both inspired by Enlightenment ideas
- Sparked revolutionary movements in Europe throughout the 19th century
- Inspired anti-colonial movements in Latin America and Asia
- Introduced concepts that shaped modern human rights declarations
- Created a model for democratic revolutions worldwide

## 2. Historical Background

### Political Conditions Before 1789
- France was an absolute monarchy under Louis XVI where the king held all political power
- The monarch could impose taxes without consultation from representative bodies
- The Estates General (representative body) had not met since 1614, giving the king unchecked authority
- No constitutional limits existed on royal authority
- Growing discontent with royal extravagance and mismanagement of state finances

### Social Conditions
- Society divided into three estates with rigid hierarchy:
  - **First Estate**: Clergy (1% of population) - owned 10% of land, paid no taxes
  - **Second Estate**: Nobility (2% of population) - owned 25-30% of land, paid no taxes
  - **Third Estate**: Everyone else (97% of population) - paid all taxes, owned remaining land
- Third estate bore the entire tax burden while having no political representation
- Growing middle class (bourgeoisie) educated but politically excluded from power
- Social position determined by birth rather than merit or achievement

### Economic Conditions
- Empty royal treasury due to:
  - Expensive wars including support for American Revolution (1776-1783)
  - Extravagant court spending at Versailles (costing millions annually)
  - Inefficient tax collection system that failed to reach privileged classes
- Rising food prices and frequent subsistence crises due to population growth
- Growing debt burden - France owed over 2 billion livres with lenders charging 10% interest
- Economic inequality between social classes widened as wages failed to keep pace with prices

## 3. Key Terms and Concepts

### Important Terms Explained

**Old Regime**: The political and social system in France before 1789, characterized by absolute monarchy and feudal privileges where power and wealth were concentrated in the hands of the king, nobility, and clergy

**Estates**: The three social classes in France - Clergy (First), Nobility (Second), and Commoners (Third). Each estate had one vote in the Estates General regardless of population size

**Subsistence Crisis**: Extreme situation where basic means of livelihood are endangered due to food shortages and high prices. Occurred frequently in France when bad harvests, rising population, and speculation combined to make bread unaffordable

**Bourgeoisie**: The middle class including merchants, manufacturers, lawyers, and administrative officials who were educated, wealthy, but denied political rights due to their birth status

**Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen**: Document adopted in August 1789 establishing fundamental rights as natural and inalienable, including liberty, property, security, and resistance to oppression

**National Assembly**: Representative body formed by the Third Estate on June 17, 1789, declaring themselves the true representatives of the French nation

**Constitutional Monarchy**: System where monarch's powers are limited by constitution and shared with elected representatives

**Republic**: Form of government where people elect representatives, including the head of government, with no hereditary monarchy

**Reign of Terror**: Period from 1793-1794 when Robespierre's government executed thousands of suspected enemies of the revolution through revolutionary tribunals

**Jacobins**: Political club of radical revolutionaries led by Maximilian Robespierre who advocated for a republic and supported the execution of Louis XVI

**Sans-culottes**: Radical revolutionaries from working classes who wore long trousers instead of aristocratic knee breeches as a symbol of their opposition to the old order

## 4. Detailed Explanation of Events (Chronological Order)

### Event 1: Financial Crisis and Calling of Estates General (1789)
**What happened**: Louis XVI called the Estates General in May 1789 to approve new taxes due to empty treasury
**Why it happened**: Years of war, court extravagance, and inefficient taxation had bankrupted the state. France had helped the thirteen American colonies gain independence from Britain, adding more than a billion livres to a debt that had already risen to more than 2 billion livres. Lenders who gave the state credit now began to charge 10% interest on loans
**Important dates**: May 5, 1789 - Estates General convened
**Consequences**: Created opportunity for Third Estate to demand political reforms and challenge the existing social order

### Event 2: Formation of National Assembly (June 1789)
**What happened**: Third Estate representatives declared themselves the National Assembly and took Tennis Court Oath
**Why it happened**: King rejected voting by head (one person, one vote) in favor of traditional estate-based voting where each estate had one vote regardless of size. The Third Estate demanded equal representation based on population
**Important dates**: June 17 - Third Estate declares National Assembly; June 20 - Tennis Court Oath
**Consequences**: Marked the beginning of the revolution; challenged royal authority and established the principle of popular sovereignty

### Event 3: Storming of the Bastille (July 14, 1789)
**What happened**: Parisian crowd stormed the Bastille fortress to seize weapons and ammunition
**Why it happened**: Fear of royal troops attacking citizens; symbol of royal despotism and arbitrary power. The Bastille held political prisoners and was seen as representing the king's absolute authority
**Important dates**: July 14, 1789
**Consequences**: Became the symbolic start of the revolution; Bastille demolished; July 14 became France's national day

### Event 4: The Great Fear and Peasant Revolts (July-August 1789)
**What happened**: Peasants attacked chateaux, burned documents, and seized grain
**Why it happened**: Rumors spread that nobles had hired bands of brigands to destroy the ripe crops. Caught in a frenzy of fear, peasants in several districts seized hoes and pitchforks and attacked the homes of their feudal lords
**Important dates**: July-August 11789
**Consequences**: Forced feudal system abolition; accelerated revolutionary changes; nobles fled to neighboring countries

### Event 5: Abolition of Feudalism (August 4, 1789)
**What happened**: National Assembly abolished feudal privileges, tithes, and obligations
**Why it happened**: Pressure from peasant revolts; desire to create equal society; nobles voluntarily gave up their privileges in a night of enthusiastic reform
**Important dates**: August 4, 1789
**Consequences**: Ended feudal system; confiscated church lands; created new social order based on equality

### Event 6: Declaration of Rights of Man and Citizen (August 1789)
**What happened**: National Assembly adopted fundamental rights document
**Why it happened**: To establish principles for new constitution and society; to articulate the philosophical basis for revolutionary change
**Important dates**: August 1789
**Consequences**: Created foundation for modern human rights; influenced global declarations; established equality before law

### Event 7: Women's March to Versailles (October 1789)
**What happened**: Women marched from Paris to Versailles demanding bread and bringing royal family to Paris
**Why it happened**: Bread shortages and high prices; desire to hold king accountable; women were particularly affected by food inflation
**Important dates**: October 1789
**Consequences**: Demonstrated popular power; kept revolution under public pressure; royal family became virtual prisoners in Paris

### Event 8: France Becomes Constitutional Monarchy (1791)
**What happened**: New constitution limited monarch's powers; created representative government
**Why it happened**: To establish stable government based on popular sovereignty; to prevent return to absolute monarchy
**Important dates**: 1791
**Consequences**: Created constitutional framework; established separation of powers; Louis XVI became constitutional monarch

### Event 9: France Becomes Republic (1792)
**What happened**: Monarchy abolished; France declared a republic
**Why it happened**: King's attempted flight to Varennes; war with Austria-Prussia; republican sentiment growing among revolutionaries
**Important dates**: September 21, 1792
**Consequences**: Executed Louis XVI; created first French republic; radicalized the revolution

### Event 10: Reign of Terror (1793-1794)
**What happened**: Robespierre's government executed thousands as enemies of the republic
**Why it happened**: To protect revolution from internal and external threats; to enforce revolutionary virtue and unity
**Important dates**: 1793-1794
**Consequences**: Created fear; eventually led to Robespierre's downfall; demonstrated dangers of revolutionary extremism

### Event 11: Rise of Napoleon (1799)
**What happened**: Napoleon seized power; later crowned himself Emperor
**Why it happened**: Political instability; desire for strong leadership; military success made him popular
**Important dates**: 1799 (coup); 1804 (coronation)
**Consequences**: Ended revolutionary period; spread revolutionary ideas across Europe; created Napoleonic Code

## 5. Causes and Effects

### Political Causes
**Causes**:
- Absolute monarchy with no constitutional limits on royal power
- King's arbitrary power to impose taxes without representative consent
- Estates General not convened for 175 years (last met in 1614)
- No representative government or political participation for common people
- Royal extravagance and mismanagement of state finances

**Effects**:
- Creation of National Assembly as representative body
- Demand for constitutional government and limited monarchy
- Execution of monarchy and establishment of republic
- Creation of democratic institutions and principles

### Economic Causes
**Causes**:
- Empty royal treasury from expensive wars including support for American Revolution
- Court extravagance at Versailles costing millions annually
- Inefficient tax system that exempted clergy and nobility from most taxes
- Rising food prices and frequent subsistence crises due to population growth
- Growing debt burden - France owed over 2 billion livres with lenders charging 10% interest
- Third Estate paid taille (direct tax) and numerous indirect taxes on everyday consumption like salt (gabelle) and tobacco

**Effects**:
- Financial reforms and new taxation system based on equality
- Economic equality measures including price controls during radical phase
- State control of economy during Reign of Terror
- Confiscation and sale of church lands to reduce debt

### Social Causes
**Causes**:
- Rigid social hierarchy based on birth rather than merit (Three Estates system)
- Feudal privileges for nobility and clergy including tax exemptions and feudal dues
- Third Estate's political and economic exploitation despite being productive class
- Growing middle class (bourgeoisie) demanding political rights commensurate with economic status
- Enlightenment ideas spreading among educated classes promoting equality and individual rights

**Effects**:
- Abolition of feudal system and aristocratic privileges
- Equality before law established for all citizens
- Merit-based social mobility replacing birth-based hierarchy
- Women's rights movements inspired by revolutionary ideals
- End of special status for clergy and nobility

### Immediate Effects
- End of absolute monarchy and execution of Louis XVI
- Abolition of feudal privileges and feudal dues
- Declaration of fundamental rights and equality before law
- Church lands confiscated and sold to reduce state debt
- New administrative divisions replacing old provinces
- New calendar and measurement system introduced

### Long-term Effects
- Spread of democratic ideas across Europe and worldwide
- Inspiration for future revolutions in Europe, Latin America, and Asia
- Development of modern nation-states based on citizenship
- Human rights declarations globally influenced by French model
- End of feudal systems across Europe
- Women's suffrage movements inspired by revolutionary ideals
- Creation of modern concepts of citizenship and national identity

## 6. Important Personalities

### Louis XVI
**Role**: King of France from 1774 who ascended the throne with an empty treasury and faced growing discontent within the society of the Old Regime
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Traditional absolute monarchy; believed in divine right of kings; initially well-intentioned but indecisive and ineffective
**Contribution**: His resistance to reforms and attempt to flee Paris in 1791 triggered revolutionary crisis; his execution marked the end of monarchy
**Impact**: Executed on January 21, 1793 for treason; symbolized the failure of absolute monarchy; his fate demonstrated the power of revolutionary justice

### Marie Antoinette
**Role**: Austrian princess and queen of France, wife of Louis XVI
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Royal privilege and extravagance; initially popular but became symbol of royal excess
**Contribution**: Her lavish spending and perceived indifference to people's suffering made her extremely unpopular; represented the disconnect between monarchy and people
**Impact**: Executed publicly on October 16, 1793; became symbol of everything the revolution opposed; her fate showed no one was safe from revolutionary justice

### Maximilien Robespierre
**Role**: Leader of the Jacobin club; head of Committee of Public Safety during the Reign of Terror
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Radical republicanism; virtue through terror; believed terror was necessary to protect the revolution
**Contribution**: Led the Reign of Terror from 1793-1794; defended the republic against internal and external enemies; promoted revolutionary virtue
**Impact**: Guillotined on July 28, 1794 by his own supporters; symbol of revolutionary extremism; demonstrated dangers of unchecked revolutionary power

### Mirabeau
**Role**: Leader of the National Assembly; key figure in creating constitutional monarchy
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Constitutional monarchy; reform from within the system; believed in preserving monarchy with limited powers
**Contribution**: Bridged nobility and Third Estate; powerful orator who defended revolutionary principles; helped create constitutional framework
**Impact**: Died in 1791 before revolution radicalized; represented moderate revolutionary path; his death removed stabilizing influence from revolution

### Abbé Sieyès
**Role**: Political theorist and revolutionary leader; member of National Assembly
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Third Estate as true nation; popular sovereignty; equality before law
**Contribution**: Wrote influential pamphlet "What is the Third Estate?" (January 1789); articulated revolutionary ideology; helped shape constitutional principles
**Impact**: His ideas about national sovereignty and popular representation became fundamental to modern democracy; influenced constitutional development

### Olympe de Gouges
**Role**: Women's rights activist and playwright; advocate for gender equality
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Gender equality; universal rights; extension of revolutionary principles to women
**Contribution**: Wrote Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen (1791); protested against exclusion of women from political rights; advocated for women's suffrage
**Impact**: Executed on November 3, 1793 for her revolutionary activities; inspired future feminist movements; highlighted contradictions in revolutionary principles

### Napoleon Bonaparte
**Role**: Military leader; Emperor of France; ended revolutionary period
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Strong centralized authority; modernization through legal reform; military glory
**Contribution**: Spread revolutionary ideas across Europe through conquests; created Napoleonic Code; established modern administrative system
**Impact**: Ended revolutionary period but preserved many revolutionary achievements; created modern French state; influenced European legal systems

### Marquis de Lafayette
**Role**: Military leader; moderate revolutionary; advocate for constitutional monarchy
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Constitutional monarchy; American-style democracy; individual rights
**Contribution**: Commanded National Guard; helped draft Declaration of Rights of Man and Citizen; fought in American Revolution
**Impact**: Represented moderate revolutionary position; his influence declined as revolution radicalized; later imprisoned by Austrians

### Georges Danton
**Role**: Revolutionary leader; first President of Committee of Public Safety
**Beliefs/Ideology**: Revolutionary pragmatism; believed in using terror when necessary but favored moderation
**Contribution**: Key figure in early revolution; helped organize revolutionary government; later fell out with Robespierre
**Impact**: Guillotined on April 5, 1794; represented revolutionary leadership that became victims of the Terror; showed how even revolutionaries could become enemies

## 7. Social Impact

### Common People
- Gained political rights and representation through new democratic institutions
- Freed from feudal obligations and taxes that had burdened them for centuries
- Access to education and public offices regardless of social birth
- Equal treatment under law replaced arbitrary justice of Old Regime
- Participation in political clubs and discussions allowed expression of grievances
- New opportunities for social mobility based on merit rather than birth

### Women
- Active participants in revolution from storming of Bastille to March on Versailles
- Formed political clubs and newspapers to voice their demands and interests
- Gained some legal rights including divorce (legalized in 1792) and inheritance equality
- Education opportunities expanded with creation of state schools
- Political rights denied despite contributions; reduced to passive citizens in 1791 Constitution
- Olympe de Gouges advocated gender equality through her Declaration of Rights of Woman and Citizen

### Workers
- Better wages during revolutionary period due to economic reforms
- Price controls on essential goods like bread helped maintain living standards
- Right to form associations and participate in political activities
- Equal treatment in workplaces under new laws promoting equality
- Protection from exploitation through maximum wage laws during radical phase
- Participation in political clubs allowed them to influence revolutionary policies

### Peasants
- Freed from feudal dues and obligations that had kept them in poverty
- Confiscation of church lands made available for purchase, allowing land ownership
- Equal tax burden replaced discriminatory system that had burdened Third Estate
- Right to own and inherit land without feudal restrictions
- Participation in local government through new administrative system
- End of seigneurial rights and forced labor obligations

### Different Social Groups
- **Clergy**: Lost privileges and lands; became state employees with salaries; required to swear oath to constitution; church-state separation established
- **Nobility**: Lost titles and feudal rights; some emigrated during Great Fear; those who remained lost special status; new elite based on merit replaced birth-based aristocracy
- **Middle Class**: Gained political power commensurate with economic status; became new administrative and commercial elite; benefited from abolition of feudal restrictions
- **Artisans**: Protected by price controls; organized in political clubs; gained legal protections against exploitation; participated in revolutionary government

## 11. Critical Thinking Section

### Debates and Different Viewpoints

**Historians' Disagreements**:
- **Causes**: Some emphasize economic factors (debt, taxation), others social causes (inequality, privilege), or political causes (absolute monarchy, lack of representation)
- **Nature of revolution**: Was it truly bourgeois revolution benefiting middle class, or did it involve all classes including workers and peasants?
- **Robespierre's legacy**: Hero who saved republic from enemies or tyrant who abused power through terror?
- **Women's role**: How significant was their contribution to revolutionary changes? Why were they excluded from political rights despite active participation?
- **Napoleon's impact**: Modernizer who spread revolution or dictator who betrayed revolutionary principles?

**Contemporary Debates**:
- **Violence justification**: Was the Reign of Terror necessary for revolutionary success or did it betray revolutionary ideals?
- **Universal rights**: Did the Declaration truly apply to all people including women, slaves, and colonial subjects?
- **Slavery contradiction**: How could revolutionaries support freedom while maintaining slavery in colonies?
- **Women's exclusion**: Why were women denied political rights despite their crucial contributions to revolution?
- **Revolutionary goals**: Did the revolution achieve its stated ideals of liberty, equality, fraternity?

**Modern Relevance**:
- Connection to current democratic movements and struggles for representation
- Lessons about balancing liberty and security in times of crisis
- Importance of inclusive political participation and representation
- Role of violence in political change and its consequences
- Relationship between economic inequality and political unrest
- Tension between individual rights and collective security

### 3 Possible 3-Mark Answers

**Q: What were the main causes of the French Revolution?**

**Ans:** The French Revolution had multiple causes:
1. **Political**: Absolute monarchy with no constitutional limits; king's arbitrary power to impose taxes without representative consent; Estates General not convened for 175 years
2. **Economic**: Empty royal treasury from expensive wars including support for American Revolution; court extravagance at Versailles; rising food prices and frequent subsistence crises; growing debt burden with 10% interest rates
3. **Social**: Rigid Three Estates system with Third Estate bearing all tax burden; feudal privileges for nobility and clergy; growing middle class (bourgeoisie) demanding political rights; Enlightenment ideas spreading among educated classes

The combination of these factors created widespread discontent that exploded into revolution when Louis XVI called the Estates General in 1789.

**Q: Explain the importance of the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.**

**Ans:** The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, adopted in August 1789, was crucial because it established fundamental rights as natural and inalienable. It proclaimed that men are born free and equal in rights, and that the aim of political association is to preserve these rights - liberty, property, security, and resistance to oppression. The declaration established popular sovereignty, stating that all sovereignty resides in the nation. It guaranteed freedom of speech, equality before law, and the right to participate in law formation. This document became the foundation for the new constitution and influenced human rights declarations worldwide, making it one of the most important legacies of the French Revolution.

**Q: What were the main features of the Constitution of 1791?**

**Ans:** The Constitution of 1791 established France as a constitutional monarchy with limited royal powers. It vested law-making power in the National Assembly, which was indirectly elected. Only men above 25 years who paid taxes equal to at least 3 days of a laborer's wage were active citizens with voting rights. The constitution separated powers among legislature, executive, and judiciary. It began with the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, establishing rights such as life, freedom of speech, and equality before law as natural and inalienable. The king retained executive powers but could not impose taxes or make laws without Assembly approval. This constitution marked France's transition from absolute monarchy to constitutional government.

### 2 Possible 5-Mark Answers

**Q: Discuss the social causes of the French Revolution and their impact on different groups in society.**

Ans:** The social causes of the French Revolution were deeply rooted in the rigid hierarchical structure of French society under the Old Regime. French society was divided into Three Estates - the clergy (First Estate), nobility (Second Estate), and the common people (Third Estate). The First and Second Estates enjoyed numerous privileges by birth, including exemption from most taxes. They also extracted feudal dues from the peasants and owned about 60% of the land.

The Third Estate, comprising 97% of the population, bore the entire burden of taxation. They paid the taille (direct tax) and numerous indirect taxes on everyday items like salt (gabelle) and tobacco. Despite being the most productive class, they had no political rights and were excluded from decision-making processes.

The growing middle class, or bourgeoisie, within the Third Estate was particularly affected. Educated and wealthy, they were involved in trade, manufacturing, and professions like law and administration. However, their social position was determined by birth rather than merit, which contradicted their beliefs in equality and individual worth.

This social inequality led to widespread discontent. The privileged classes enjoyed all the benefits while the majority suffered economic hardship. The bourgeoisie, influenced by Enlightenment ideas, demanded political rights commensurate with their economic status. Peasants resented feudal obligations and high taxes. The entire Third Estate united against the privileged orders, creating the social foundation for revolutionary change.

The impact was transformative. The revolution abolished feudal privileges, established equality before law, and created a merit-based society. The clergy and nobility lost their special status, while the middle class gained political power. Peasants were freed from feudal dues, and the concept of equal citizenship emerged, fundamentally changing French society.

**Q: Analyze the role of women in the French Revolution and the limitations they faced despite their contributions.**

Ans:** Women played a crucial and active role throughout the French Revolution, participating in all major events from the very beginning. They were involved in the storming of the Bastille, the March on Versailles, political clubs, and revolutionary activities.

Women from the Third Estate had to work for a living as seamstresses, laundresses, market sellers, or domestic servants. They also bore the responsibility of family care - cooking, fetching water, queuing for bread, and looking after children. Their wages were lower than men's, and they had limited access to education and job training.

During the revolution, women organized themselves politically. About sixty women's clubs emerged in different French cities, with the Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women being the most famous. They demanded equal political rights, including the right to vote, to be elected to the Assembly, and to hold political office. Olympe de Gouges wrote the Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen in 1791, demanding gender equality.

The revolutionary government did introduce some improvements in women's lives. State schools were created, and schooling became compulsory for girls. Marriage became a freely entered civil contract, and divorce was legalized for both men and women. Women could now receive job training and start small businesses.

However, despite their significant contributions, women faced severe limitations. The Constitution of 1791 reduced them to passive citizens with no voting rights. During the Reign of Terror, women's clubs were forcibly closed, and many prominent women were arrested and executed. Chaumette and other Jacobins argued that women should confine themselves to domestic duties, claiming that nature had assigned different roles to men and women.

This contradiction - active participation in revolution but denial of political rights - highlighted the limitations of revolutionary ideals. Women's struggle for equality continued for over a century after the revolution. It was only in 1946 that women in France finally won the right to vote, demonstrating how the revolution's promise of universal rights remained incomplete for half of the population.

The women's experience in the French Revolution became an important inspiration for future feminist movements worldwide, showing both the possibilities and limitations of revolutionary change.

- Third Estate pays all taxes
- No political rights for common people

- Equality before law
- All citizens equal (theoretically)
- Political rights for propertied men
