# Key Concepts of the French Revolution

*A simplified guide for Class 9 CBSE Students*

---

## 1. The Old Regime

The term **Old Regime** refers to the social and political system of France before the year 1789. It was characterised by:

- A society divided into three estates
- Absolute monarchy (king had complete power)
- Feudal system where nobles owned land and peasants worked for them
- privileges based on birth rather than merit

The Old Regime was deeply unfair because the clergy and nobility (the first two estates) did not pay taxes, while the common people (third estate) bore the entire burden of funding the government.

---

## 2. The Three Estates System

Before the French Revolution, French society was divided into three groups called **Estates**:

### First Estate — The Clergy

- Consisted of priests, monks, and church officials
- Made up about 0.5% of the population
- Owned about 10% of the land
- **Exempted from paying taxes** to the king
- Collected **tithes** (one-tenth of agricultural produce) from peasants

### Second Estate — The Nobility

- Consisted of kings, nobles, aristocrats, and landlords
- Made up about 1.5% of the population
- Owned about 20% of the land
- **Exempted from paying taxes**
- Collected **feudal dues** from peasants
- Enjoyed special rights and privileges by birth

### Third Estate — The Common People

- Made up about 98% of the population
- Included:
  - **Peasants** — the largest group (90% of population)
  - **Middle Class (Bourgeoisie)** — merchants, lawyers, doctors, traders
  - **Urban Poor** — servants, workers, labourers
- Owned only about 10-20% of the land
- **Paid all taxes** to the state (taille, gabelle, etc.)
- Had no political rights or social status

---

## 3. Feudalism and Feudal Dues

**Feudalism** was a medieval system where land was owned by nobles and worked by peasants. In return, peasants had to:

- Pay **feudal dues** (money or produce) to the lord
- Work on the lord's land without payment
- Serve in the lord's army when called
- Pay **tithes** to the Church (one-tenth of their harvest)
- Use the lord's mill, oven, or wine press (and pay for it)

This system kept peasants poor and trapped in poverty for generations.

---

## 4. Subsistence Crisis

A **subsistence crisis** occurs when people cannot afford basic needs like food. In France during the 1780s, this happened because:

- **Population increased** from 23 million (1715) to 28 million (1789)
- **Food production could not keep pace** with growing demand
- **Bread prices rose sharply** — bread was the staple food of the poor
- **Wages did not increase** to match rising prices
- **Bad harvests** due to drought or hail made things worse

The cycle of subsistence crisis:

```
Bad Harvest → Scarcity of Grain → Rising Food Prices 
→ Poor Cannot Buy Bread → Weak Bodies → Disease Epidemics 
→ Increased Deaths
```

---

## 5. The Middle Class (Bourgeoisie)

The **middle class** (also called bourgeoisie) emerged in the 18th century through:

- Expanding overseas trade
- Manufacturing of goods (wool, silk, textiles)
- Professions like lawyers, doctors, administrators

They were:

- Educated and literate
- Wealthy but not noble
- Wanted political power equal to their economic status
- Believed in equality of opportunity based on merit, not birth
- Were influenced by Enlightenment ideas

---

## 6. Enlightenment Ideas

The **Enlightenment** was an intellectual movement in the 17th-18th century that promoted reason, liberty, and equality. Key philosophers and their ideas:

### John Locke (English Philosopher)

- **Theory:** Government is based on a social contract between people and their rulers.
- **Work:** Two Treatises of Government
- **Idea:** People have natural rights (life, liberty, property). If a government violates these, people can overthrow it.

### Jean Jacques Rousseau (French Philosopher)

- **Theory:** Sovereignty belongs to the people.
- **Work:** The Social Contract
- **Idea:** "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Government should be based on the general will of the people.

### Montesquieu (French Philosopher)

- **Theory:** Separation of powers.
- **Work:** The Spirit of the Laws
- **Idea:** Power should be divided among three branches — Legislative (makes laws), Executive (enforces laws), Judiciary (interprets laws).

These ideas inspired the American and French Revolutions.

---

## 7. The Estates General

The **Estates General** was a political assembly in France representing the three estates. Key facts:

- Called by the King when needed
- Last called in 1614 before 1789
- Each estate had one vote (first two estates always outvoted the third)
- On **5 May 1789**, Louis XVI called it to propose new taxes

In 1789:

- First Estate: 300 representatives
- Second Estate: 300 representatives
- Third Estate: 600 representatives

The Third Estate was denied proper seating and had to stand at the back.

---

## 8. The National Assembly

When Louis XVI tried to continue the old voting system, the Third Estate representatives walked out and formed their own assembly:

- On **20 June 1789**, they met in an indoor tennis court at Versailles
- They declared themselves the **National Assembly**
- They swore the **Tennis Court Oath**: "Not to disperse till we have drafted a constitution"
- Leaders: Mirabeau and Abbé Sieyès

This was the beginning of the revolution.

---

## 9. Storming of the Bastille

On **14 July 1789**, a crucial event took place:

- Paris was in alarm; troops were deployed
- Rumours spread that the king would use force
- About 7,000 people gathered and formed a militia
- They broke into government buildings searching for arms
- Finally, they stormed the **Bastille** (a fortress-prison in eastern Paris)

The Bastille symbolized royal tyranny. Though only 7 prisoners were found, its fall was a powerful symbol of the end of absolute monarchy.

---

## 10. The Great Fear

In summer 1789, fear spread through French villages:

- Rumours said "bands of brigands" were coming to destroy crops
- Peasants panicked and attacked noble estates (chateaux)
- They looted grain and burnt manorial records
- Many nobles fled to neighbouring countries

This spontaneous uprising forced the king to accept the revolution.

---

## 11. Abolition of Feudalism

On the night of **4 August 1789**, the National Assembly:

- Passed a decree abolishing the feudal system
- Ended feudal dues and compulsory services
- Abolished tithes (tax to Church)
- Confiscated Church lands
- The government gained assets worth 2 billion livres

This marked the end of the Old Regime.

---

## 12. The Constitution of 1791

France became a **constitutional monarchy** in 1791:

- **Separation of powers** between legislature, executive, and judiciary
- Power to make laws given to the National Assembly
- King's powers were limited
- Citizens divided into:
  - **Active Citizens:** Men above 25 who paid taxes (could vote)
  - **Passive Citizens:** Poor men and all women (no voting rights)

The Constitution began with the **Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen**.

---

## 13. Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen

Adopted in 1789, this was a landmark document establishing fundamental rights:

- **Right 1:** Men are born and remain free and equal in rights
- **Right 2:** Goal of government: preserve natural rights — Liberty, Property, Security, Resistance to Oppression
- **Right 3:** Source of sovereignty is the nation (the people)
- **Right 4:** Liberty means doing whatever is not harmful to others
- **Right 6:** Law is the expression of general will; all citizens equal before it
- **Right 11:** Freedom of speech and press
- **Right 17:** Property is a sacred right

These rights were "natural and inalienable" — belonging to every human by birth.

---

## 14. France Becomes a Republic

Events moved quickly after 1791:

- **April 1792:** France declared war on Prussia and Austria
- **August 1792:** Jacobins led an uprising; stormed the Tuileries Palace; imprisoned the royal family
- **21 September 1792:** The Convention abolished monarchy and declared France a **republic**
- **21 January 1793:** Louis XVI executed for treason
- **October 1793:** Marie Antoinette executed

A **republic** is a government where people elect their representatives; there is no king or queen.

---

## 15. The Jacobins and Sans-culottes

### The Jacobins

- Most powerful political club during the revolution
- Named after the Jacobin convent in Paris
- Members: small shopkeepers, artisans, servants, daily-wage workers
- Less prosperous sections of society
- Leader: **Maximilien Robespierre**

### Sans-culottes

- Radical supporters of the Jacobins
- Name means "those without knee-breeches"
- Wore long striped trousers (unlike nobles who wore knee breeches)
- Men wore the red Phrygian cap (symbol of liberty)
- Wanted complete equality and end of royal privileges

---

## 16. The Reign of Terror (1793-1794)

From 1793 to 1794, France was ruled by Robespierre through fear:

- All "enemies of the republic" were arrested
- Tried by revolutionary tribunals
- If found "guilty," executed by **guillotine**
- Victims: ex-nobles, clergy, political opponents, even Jacobins who disagreed

### Reforms During the Terror:

- Maximum ceiling on wages and prices
- Meat and bread rationed
- All citizens required to eat "equality bread" (wholewheat)
- Traditional titles (Mr, Mrs) replaced with "Citizen"
- Churches closed and converted to offices

Robespierre was eventually arrested and guillotined in July 1794 by his own supporters.

---

## 17. The Directory (1795-1799)

After Robespierre's fall:

- New constitution gave voting rights only to the wealthy
- Two legislative councils were elected
- They appointed a **Directory** of 5 members as executive
- Meant to prevent one-person rule
- But there was constant conflict between directories and councils
- Political instability led to the rise of a military leader — **Napoleon Bonaparte**

---

## 18. Women in the Revolution

Women played an active role from the beginning:

- Participated in protests and riots
- Demanded equal political rights
- Formed women's clubs and newspapers
- About 60 women's clubs formed in French cities
- Most famous: **Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women**

### Demands:

- Right to vote
- Right to be elected to the Assembly
- Right to hold political office

### Achievements (initially):

- Compulsory schooling for girls
- Right to refuse forced marriage
- Civil marriage and legal divorce
- Women could train for jobs

### Setbacks:

- During the Reign of Terror, women's clubs were closed
- Many women arrested and executed
- Women gained voting rights only in **1946**

---

## 19. Olympe de Gouges (1748-1793)

A pioneering feminist during the revolution:

- Wrote the **Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen** in 1791
- Pointed out that the Declaration of Rights of Man excluded women
- Criticised the Jacobin government for suppressing women's clubs
- Charged with treason and executed in 1793

Her famous words: "Woman is born free and remains equal to man in rights."

---

## 20. The Abolition of Slavery

One of the most progressive acts of the revolution:

### The Triangular Trade:

- French merchants sailed from Bordeaux/Nantes to Africa
- Bought slaves from African chiefs
- Transported them to Caribbean plantations (3-month voyage)
- Sold them to grow sugar, coffee, tobacco, indigo
- Made huge profits

### Revolutionary Action:

- **1794:** Convention abolished slavery in all French colonies
- **1802:** Napoleon reinstated slavery
- **1848:** Slavery finally abolished permanently

French colonies like Martinique, Guadeloupe, and San Domingo were affected.

---

## 21. The Revolution and Everyday Life

The revolution changed daily life in France:

### End of Censorship (1789)

- Freedom of speech and press became a right
- Newspapers, pamphlets, books flooded the country
- People could express opposing views

### Language and Titles

- "Mr/Mrs" replaced with "Citizen/Citoyenne"
- Titles of nobility abolished

### New Laws

- Metric system introduced (decimal system)
- Civil marriage and divorce
- State-controlled schools

---

## 22. Political Symbols

The revolution used symbols to communicate ideas (most people could not read):

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Broken Chain** | Freedom from slavery/ oppression |
| **Bundle of Rods (Fasces)** | Unity and strength |
| **Red Phrygian Cap** | Liberty (worn by freed slaves) |
| **Eye in Triangle** | Knowledge drives away ignorance |
| **Blue-White-Red Flag** | National colours of France |
| **Sceptre** | Royal power (rejected) |
| **Law Tablet** | Equality before the law |

---

## 23. The Napoleonic Era

### Napoleon Bonaparte

- Rose to power after the Directory's fall
- Crowned **Emperor of France in 1804**
- Introduced the **Napoleonic Code** — uniform laws
- Protected private property
- Introduced metric system
- Conquered much of Europe

### Impact:

- Initially seen as a liberator
- Later seen as an invader
- Defeated at **Waterloo in 1815**
- Yet his laws spread revolutionary ideals across Europe

---

## 24. Legacy of the French Revolution

The French Revolution left lasting impacts:

### Political Ideas:

- **Liberty** — Freedom from oppression
- **Equality** — All equal before law
- **Fraternity** — Unity among citizens
- Democratic rights and constitutions
- Republic as a form of government

### Spread of Ideas:

- Inspired liberal movements in Europe
- Influenced anti-colonial movements worldwide
- Indian leaders like Tipu Sultan and Rammohan Roy were inspired by these ideas

### Long-term Effects:

- End of feudalism
- Rise of middle class
- Concept of citizen's rights
- Foundation for modern democracy
