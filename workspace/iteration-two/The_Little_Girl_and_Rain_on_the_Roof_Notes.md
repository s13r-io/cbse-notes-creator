## Section 1: Chapter Introduction

### What This Chapter Is About

This chapter introduces you to two very different literary experiences packed into Beehive Chapter 3. **"The Little Girl"** by Katherine Mansfield is a short story about a young girl named Kezia who is terrified of her strict, unapproachable father — until a nightmare one night reveals his hidden tenderness and changes her understanding forever. **"Rain on the Roof"** by Coates Kinney is a lyric poem in which the poet lies in bed listening to rain falling on his roof, and the gentle patter summons a flood of memories, especially of his mother watching over him as he slept. Together, these pieces explore the powerful emotional bonds between children and their parents — bonds that can be hidden behind sternness or distance, but are always present underneath.

### Why This Matters

- These texts explore **parent-child relationships**, a theme every student can relate to personally and one that appears frequently in CBSE Literature papers.
- "The Little Girl" teaches you how to **analyze character development** — the story traces Kezia's feelings from fear to understanding across a clear emotional arc.
- "Rain on the Roof" develops your skill in **poetry interpretation** — identifying literary devices, understanding figurative language, and connecting personal emotion to natural imagery.
- Both texts frequently appear in **extract-based questions** (Section C of the English paper), which carry 40 marks.
- Understanding how writers use **narrative perspective** and **sensory imagery** will help you in unseen passage comprehension (Section A, 20 marks) and creative writing (Section B, 20 marks).

### What You'll Learn

After studying this chapter, you will be able to:
1. Summarize both the prose piece and the poem in your own words with key details.
2. Analyze Kezia's character development and explain how her perception of her father changes.
3. Identify and explain at least six literary devices used in "Rain on the Roof" with examples from the text.
4. Apply grammar rules for **verbs of reporting** and **synonyms** to answer exam questions correctly.
5. Write a formal letter and a diary entry using the correct CBSE format.

### Before You Start

You should already know:
- Basic knowledge of literary devices: metaphor, simile, personification, alliteration, onomatopoeia (from Class 8 English).
- How to write a paragraph with a clear topic sentence and supporting details.

## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 The Little Girl — Chapter Summary

#### In Brief

Kezia, a young girl, is afraid of her strict and stern father. She stutters in his presence and avoids him. After she accidentally tears up his important speech papers to stuff a birthday pin-cushion, he beats her with a ruler. Her fear deepens. But one night, when she has a nightmare and her mother and grandmother are away, her father comforts her with unexpected tenderness, and she realizes that behind his tough exterior, he has a "big heart."

#### Detailed Summary

Kezia's father is a large, intimidating man with a loud voice and stern manner. Every morning before leaving for work, he gives Kezia a casual kiss that fills her with dread, and she feels a "glad sense of relief" when his carriage grows fainter down the road. In the evenings, she hears him giving orders — demanding tea, newspapers, and slippers — and her grandmother sends her down to pull off his boots. When she enters the drawing-room, her father glares at her over his spectacles and asks if she has been a good girl. Kezia is so nervous that she stutters, and her father threatens to send her to a doctor. She only stutters with him, never with anyone else, because she tries so hard to speak properly that it makes it worse.

⭐ On Sundays, Kezia's grandmother sends her to have "a nice talk" with her parents, but her mother is always reading and her father is always asleep on the sofa, snoring. When he wakes, he tells her not to stare and calls her "a little brown owl." One day, when Kezia has a cold, her grandmother suggests she make her father a birthday pin-cushion from a piece of yellow silk. Kezia laboriously stitches three sides but does not know what to fill it with. She finds sheets of fine paper on her mother's bed-table, tears them into pieces, and stuffs the cushion. That night, the household is in chaos — her father's important speech for the Port Authority has been lost. When her mother discovers what happened, Kezia is dragged downstairs to face her father. She tries to explain it was a birthday surprise but is too terrified to speak clearly. Her father calls for a ruler and beats her little pink palms, telling her she must learn not to touch what does not belong to her.

After the beating, Kezia cries in her grandmother's arms and asks, "What did God make fathers for?" Her grandmother tries to comfort her, but the child never forgets. The next time she sees her father, she hides her hands behind her back and blushes. Then she observes the Macdonald family next door — five children playing tag with their father, who has the baby on his shoulders and laughs as his sons turn the garden hose on him. Kezia realizes there are "different sorts of fathers."

△ One day, Kezia's mother falls ill and goes to the hospital with her grandmother. Kezia is left alone in the house with Alice, the cook. At bedtime, Kezia is afraid of the dark and of having a nightmare. Alice tells her to go to sleep and not to scream. That night, the old nightmare returns — a butcher with a knife and rope approaches her, smiling dreadfully, while she cannot move. She wakes screaming for her grandmother — but it is her father who appears at her bedside with a candle. Instead of being stern, he picks her up, carries her to his bedroom, tucks her in, and lies down beside her. Half-asleep, Kezia snuggles close to him, presses her head against his chest, and feels safe. "What a big heart you've got, Father dear," she whispers. In that moment, she understands that her father is not a giant to be feared — he is a tired, hard-working man who loves her deeply, even if he cannot always show it.

> **THINK Think About It:** Why do you think Kezia's father never played with her or showed affection openly? Was he a bad father, or was there something else going on? Think about how his long work hours might have affected his behaviour.

### 3.2 Character Analysis — "The Little Girl"

| Character | Key Traits | Evidence from Text | Role in Theme |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Kezia** | Innocent, fearful, sensitive, perceptive | "She never stuttered with other people — had quite given it up — but only with Father" | Represents the child's perspective on strict parenting; her journey from fear to understanding is the central emotional arc of the story |
| **Father** | Stern, hard-working, undemonstrative, ultimately loving | "Every day he had to work and was too tired to be a Mr Macdonald" | Represents the busy, emotionally reserved parent; the story reveals that his harshness is not lack of love but exhaustion and inability to express feelings |
| **Grandmother** | Nurturing, understanding, a mediator | "I tried to explain to Father but he was too upset to listen tonight" | Bridges the gap between Kezia and her father; represents the comforting, emotionally available adult figure |
| **Mother** | Submissive, anxious, background figure | Mother rarely challenges Father's behaviour; she "explained" the cushion incident rather than defending Kezia | Represents the traditional domestic role; her absence creates the crucial moment where Kezia and her father bond directly |
| **Mr Macdonald** | Playful, affectionate, warm | "The father with the baby, Mao, on his shoulders... shaking with laughter" | Serves as a foil (contrast) to Kezia's father; shows Kezia that fatherly love can be expressed openly and joyfully |
| **Alice (the cook)** | Practical, unsympathetic | "Don't you scream and wake your poor Pa" | A minor character whose dismissive attitude heightens Kezia's isolation when her mother and grandmother are away |

> **THINK Think About It:** Compare Kezia's father with Mr Macdonald. Both love their children, but they express it very differently. Is one way "better" than the other, or are both valid? Can you think of adults in your life who show love in unexpected ways?

### 3.3 Rain on the Roof — Poem Summary and Stanza-by-Stanza Explanation

#### In Brief

The poet, Coates Kinney, describes the blissful experience of lying in a cozy cottage bed and listening to the gentle patter of rain on the roof. The sound of every raindrop triggers a rush of dreamy memories and fancies in the poet's mind. The single most powerful memory is of his mother, who used to gaze lovingly at him and his siblings before they fell asleep — a memory that the rain brings flooding back with tender sadness.

#### Stanza 1

> *When the humid shadows hover*
> *Over all the starry spheres*
> *And the melancholy darkness*
> *Gently weeps in rainy tears,*
> *What a bliss to press the pillow*
> *Of a cottage-chamber bed*
> *And lie listening to the patter*
> *Of the soft rain overhead!*

**Word Meanings**: humid shadows = dark rain clouds full of moisture; starry spheres = the night sky full of stars; melancholy = a deep, pensive sadness; bliss = extreme happiness or joy; cottage-chamber = a small, cozy room in a cottage; patter = the sound of raindrops falling lightly.

**Explanation**: The poet sets the scene on a dark, rainy night. Dark clouds filled with moisture spread across the sky, hiding the stars. The darkness feels deeply sad, and it seems to weep gentle "rainy tears." In this atmosphere, the poet feels pure happiness lying in his simple cottage bed, pressing his pillow and listening to the soft, rhythmic sound of rain falling on the roof above him.

**Literary Devices**: Personification ("the melancholy darkness gently weeps"), alliteration ("humid shadows hover" — repetition of 'h' sound), onomatopoeia ("patter" imitates the sound of rain), transferred epithet ("melancholy darkness" — the poet is melancholy, not the darkness itself).

#### Stanza 2

> *Every tinkle on the shingles*
> *Has an echo in the heart;*
> *And a thousand dreamy fancies*
> *Into busy being start,*
> *And a thousand recollections*
> *Weave their air-threads into woof,*
> *As I listen to the patter*
> *Of the rain upon the roof.*

**Word Meanings**: tinkle = a light, ringing sound; shingles = rectangular wooden tiles on the roof; echo = a repetition of sound; fancies = imaginary thoughts or daydreams; recollections = memories; woof = the threads woven horizontally across a loom (in weaving).

**Explanation**: Each tiny sound of a raindrop hitting the wooden roof tiles resonates within the poet's heart. The rain triggers hundreds of dreamy thoughts and imaginations that come alive in his mind. At the same time, thousands of memories rise up and weave themselves together like threads in a fabric — the "air-threads" of memory forming the "woof" of his experience. All of this happens simply because he is lying still and listening to the rain.

**Literary Devices**: Metaphor ("air-threads into woof" — memories are compared to threads being woven into fabric), hyperbole ("a thousand dreamy fancies," "a thousand recollections" — exaggerated numbers to suggest abundance), alliteration ("dreamy fancies," "rain upon the roof"), refrain ("As I listen to the patter / Of the rain upon the roof" — repeated from stanza 1, creating a musical echo).

#### Stanza 3

> *Now in memory comes my mother,*
> *As she used in years agone,*
> *To regard the darling dreamers,*
> *Ere she left them till the dawn:*
> *O! I feel her fond look on me*
> *As I list to this refrain*
> *Which is played upon the shingles*
> *By the patter of the rain.*

**Word Meanings**: years agone = years gone by, in the past; regard = to look at with love and attention; darling dreamers = the children who are dreaming; ere = before (old poetic word); fond = loving and affectionate; list = listen (old poetic word); refrain = a recurring sound or melody.

**Explanation**: Of all the memories the rain brings, the most powerful is the memory of the poet's mother. He remembers how, in years past, she would look at her children — the "darling dreamers" — with love and tenderness before leaving them to sleep until dawn. Now, as he listens to the repeating sound of the rain on the roof (the "refrain"), he can actually feel his mother's loving gaze upon him, as though she is right there with him. The rain has become a bridge between the present moment and the past, connecting the poet to the memory of his mother's love.

**Literary Devices**: Metaphor ("the darling dreamers" — sleeping children compared to dreamers), imagery ("fond look on me" — creates a visual image of a mother's loving gaze), symbolism (the rain symbolizes the connection between past and present; the mother's love is felt through nature's music).

⭐ **Central Theme**: The poem celebrates the power of nature to unlock our deepest memories and emotions. The rain acts as a trigger for nostalgia, and the most precious memory is of a mother's unconditional love. The poem also suggests that happiness can be found in simple, quiet moments — lying in bed, listening to rain, and remembering those we love.

> **THINK Think About It:** The poet says his mother is no longer alive ("in years agone"). How does the poem's mood shift from the first stanza to the third? Is it still a happy poem, or has it become sad? Can a poem be both at the same time?

### 3.4 Literary Devices in "Rain on the Roof"

| Device | Example from Poem | Effect |
|--------|------------------|--------|
| **Personification** | "The melancholy darkness / Gently weeps in rainy tears" | Makes the darkness seem alive and emotional; the rain becomes the darkness's tears, creating a sad, beautiful atmosphere |
| **Onomatopoeia** | "Patter" (used three times) | Imitates the actual sound of raindrops, making the reader "hear" the rain while reading |
| **Alliteration** | "humid shadows hover" | The repeated 'h' sound mimics the soft, hushed quality of a rainy night |
| **Transferred epithet** | "Melancholy darkness" | The sadness belongs to the poet, not to the darkness; this technique lets the poet project his feelings onto nature |
| **Metaphor** | "Weave their air-threads into woof" | Memories are compared to threads being woven into fabric — suggesting that memories create the texture of our lives |
| **Hyperbole** | "A thousand dreamy fancies" / "A thousand recollections" | Exaggerated numbers to show that the rain produces an overwhelming flood of thoughts and memories |
| **Symbolism** | The rain on the shingles | Represents the passage of time, the persistence of memory, and the comforting rhythm of nature |
| **Refrain** | "As I listen to the patter / Of the rain upon the roof" | Repeated in stanzas 2 and 3 (with variation), creating a musical quality that mirrors the steady, repetitive sound of rain |

### 3.5 Grammar: Verbs of Reporting

**Rule**: A **verb of reporting** is the word we use to report or quote what someone said, thought, or felt. Every time you write or say what another person said, you use a verb of reporting. Common verbs of reporting include: said, told, asked, replied, shouted, whispered, screamed, ordered, suggested, complained, remarked, explained, wondered, promised, apologised.

**Explanation**: Verbs of reporting appear in two main structures:
1. **Direct speech**: The exact words are placed in quotation marks. The verb of reporting comes before or after the quote.
   - "Sit up," he **ordered**.
   - "What!" **screamed** Mother.
2. **Indirect (reported) speech**: The meaning is reported without exact words. The verb of reporting introduces the reported clause.
   - He **ordered** her to sit up.
   - Mother **explained** that the papers had been torn up.

The choice of reporting verb matters because it tells the reader not just what was said, but **how** it was said. "Whispered" suggests fear or secrecy; "ordered" suggests authority; "suggested" implies a polite recommendation.

**Examples**:
1. Kezia **whispered**, "N-n-no," when her father accused her of tearing the papers. (Shows fear and hesitation)
2. Father **ordered**, "Sit up and hold out your hands." (Shows authority and strictness)
3. Grandmother **promised**, "You'll forget all about it in the morning." (Shows comfort and reassurance)

**Practice**:
1. "I am not afraid," _________ the woman. → Answer: **remarked** (or replied)
2. "Leave me alone," my mother _________. → Answer: **ordered** (or shouted)
3. The children _________ that the roads were crowded and noisy. → Answer: **complained**

### 3.6 Grammar: Synonyms — Words with Similar Meanings

**Rule**: **Synonyms** are words that have the same or nearly the same meaning. However, even though synonyms are similar, they are not always interchangeable. Each synonym carries a slightly different shade of meaning, level of intensity, or formality.

**Explanation**: Consider the synonyms for "happy": glad, pleased, delighted, thrilled, overjoyed. All mean "happy," but they differ in intensity:
- **Glad** = mildly happy, relieved (e.g., "She was glad when the meeting was over.")
- **Pleased** = satisfied, content (e.g., "The teacher was pleased with his performance.")
- **Delighted** = very happy, showing joy (e.g., "She was delighted at the birth of her granddaughter.")
- **Thrilled** = extremely excited (e.g., "He was thrilled to be invited to the party.")
- **Overjoyed** = beyond happy, overflowing with joy (the strongest of the group)

In the chapter, the word "glad" is used in a specific way: "a glad sense of relief" — here, "glad" means happy-about-something-ending, which is different from "pleased" (happy-with-something-achieved) or "thrilled" (excited-about-something-new).

**Examples**:
1. She was **overjoyed** by the news of her brother's wedding. (Extremely happy — the strongest synonym fits the emotional situation of a wedding)
2. I was **thrilled** to be invited to the party. (Excited and eager — fits the social excitement of a party invitation)
3. She was **delighted** at the birth of her granddaughter. (Very happy with warm emotion — fits the joy of a new family member)

**Practice**:
1. The coach was _________ with his performance. (satisfied about) → Answer: **pleased**
2. She was very _________ with her results. (happy about something that has happened) → Answer: **happy** (or glad)
3. Find two synonyms for "sad" in order of increasing intensity. → Answer: **unhappy, devastated** (or gloomy, sorrowful, heartbroken — many valid answers)

### 3.8 Writing Format: Formal Letter

**When to use**: A formal letter is written to a person in authority (a principal, commissioner, editor, or officer) for official purposes — to complain, request information, make a suggestion, or apply for something. It follows a fixed structure and uses a formal, respectful tone.

**Template**:

```
[Sender's full address]
[City, PIN code]
[Date]

The [Designation]
[Receiver's full address]
[City, PIN code]

Subject: [Brief, clear statement of purpose — in 3-6 words]

Respected Sir/Madam,

[BODY — Paragraph 1: Introduce yourself and state the purpose of the letter]

[Paragraph 2: Give details — describe the problem, situation, or request with specific facts]

[Paragraph 3: State what action you expect and express hope for a positive response]

Yours faithfully,
[Your full signature]
[Your printed name]
[Class and Roll Number (if applicable)]
```

**Example**:

```
45, MG Road
Kolkata, 700001
1 April 2026

The Municipal Commissioner
Kolkata Municipal Corporation
Kolkata, 700001

Subject: Request for repair of waterlogged roads in our area

Respected Sir/Madam,

I am a resident of MG Road, Kolkata, and I am writing to bring to your attention the severe waterlogging problem in our locality during the monsoon season.

Every year, the roads near our colony get submerged under knee-deep water for several days after moderate rainfall. This causes immense difficulty for commuters, damages vehicles, and creates unhygienic conditions that lead to the spread of diseases such as dengue and malaria. Senior citizens and school children are particularly affected.

I kindly request you to inspect the drainage system in our area and take necessary measures to ensure proper water flow before the upcoming monsoon season. The residents of this area would be deeply grateful for your prompt action.

Yours faithfully,
Saurabh Karmakar
Class IX, Roll No. 12
```

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- **Using informal language**: Never use contractions like "don't" or "can't" — write "do not" and "cannot." Never use slang or conversational tone.
- **Forgetting the subject line**: The subject line tells the reader what your letter is about. Without it, your letter may not be taken seriously.
- **Not structuring the body into three paragraphs**: Paragraph 1 = introduction + purpose; Paragraph 2 = details; Paragraph 3 = request + hope. Do not write one long block of text.
- **Using "Yours lovingly" or "Yours affectionately"**: These are for informal letters only. In a formal letter, always use "Yours faithfully" (when you do not know the person's name) or "Yours sincerely" (when you know their name).

### 3.9 Writing Format: Diary Entry

**When to use**: A diary entry is a personal record of events, feelings, and reflections. It is written in the first person ("I") and addresses the diary as "Dear Diary." There is no fixed format, but a standard structure helps organise your thoughts.

**Template**:

```
[Day, Date]
[Time (optional)]

Dear Diary,

[Opening: What happened today? State the main event in 1-2 sentences]

[Middle: How did you feel? What did you think about? Describe your emotions and reflections in 2-3 sentences]

[Ending: What did you learn? What do you hope will happen next? 1-2 sentences]

[Sign off — your name or a nickname]
```

**Example** (as Kezia might write):

```
Sunday, 15 March 2026
Night

Dear Diary,

Something wonderful happened tonight. I had the same old nightmare — the butcher with the knife — and I woke up screaming for Grandma. But Grandma and Mother are at the hospital because Mother is ill. It was Father who came to my room with a candle.

He didn't shout. He didn't glare at me over his spectacles. He picked me up in his big arms and carried me to his bedroom. He tucked me in and lay down beside me. I snuggled close to him and put my head on his chest. I could hear his heart beating — what a big heart he has! He even told me to rub my cold feet against his legs to warm them.

I always thought Father was like a giant, scary and unkind. But tonight I understood — he works so hard every day that he is too tired to play. He is not like Mr Macdonald next door, but that does not mean he does not love me. I think I love him too.

Kezia
```

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- **Writing in third person**: A diary is always first person. Use "I," "me," "my" throughout.
- **Making it sound like a news report**: Include feelings and reflections, not just events. The reader of your diary is YOU — so write what you felt, not just what happened.
- **Being too formal**: A diary is personal and private. You can use contractions, short sentences, and emotional language. Do not use "Respected Sir" or formal letter structures.

## Section 4: Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

| # | Misconception / Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|------------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Kezia's father is a cruel man who does not love his daughter" | Students focus only on the beating scene and the stern behaviour, ignoring the tender ending | Kezia's father is **undemonstrative** — he works long hours, is exhausted, and does not know how to show affection openly. His behaviour at the end (comforting her after the nightmare, letting her warm her feet against his legs) proves he deeply loves her. The story's message is that love can exist even when it is not openly expressed. |
| 2 | "The poem 'Rain on the Roof' is just about listening to rain — there is no deeper meaning" | Students take the poem literally and miss the emotional layer | The rain is a **trigger for memory** and a **symbol of the connection between past and present**. The poem is really about the poet's love for his mother and the power of nature to resurrect our deepest emotions. Every raindrop carries an "echo in the heart." |
| 3 | "Any synonym can replace any other synonym in a sentence" | Students think glad = pleased = delighted = thrilled = overjoyed and use them interchangeably | Synonyms have **different levels of intensity and different contexts**. "Glad" implies relief; "pleased" implies satisfaction; "thrilled" implies excitement; "overjoyed" implies overwhelming happiness. You must choose the synonym that matches the emotion and context of the sentence. |
| 4 | "Verbs of reporting are only 'said' and 'told'" | Students have a limited vocabulary for reporting speech | There are dozens of verbs of reporting, each conveying **how** something was said: whispered (fear/softness), screamed (terror/anger), ordered (authority), suggested (politeness), complained (dissatisfaction), promised (assurance). Using the right verb makes your writing vivid and precise. |
| 5 | "A formal letter and an informal letter have the same format" | Students mix up the structures, especially the complimentary close | **Formal letter**: Sender's address, date, receiver's address, subject, salutation (Respected Sir/Madam), body in 3 paragraphs, "Yours faithfully/sincerely." **Informal letter**: Sender's address, date, salutation (Dear Father/ Friend), body in a conversational tone, "Yours lovingly/affectionately." The key difference is formality of tone and structure. |
| 6 | "In a diary entry, I should write about events only, not my feelings" | Students produce dry, factual accounts that read like police reports | A diary is where you record your **inner life** — your emotions, fears, hopes, and reflections. Events are just the starting point. A good diary entry answers: What happened? How did I feel? What did I learn? |

### Within English
- **Ch. 1 "The Fun They Had" (Isaac Asimov)** ← Both chapters explore how human emotions and relationships persist even when circumstances change — the children in "The Fun They Had" discover the value of human teachers, just as Kezia discovers the value of her father's love.

## Section 7: Exam-Oriented Summary

### Top 8 Things to Remember

1. ⭐ **Kezia's emotional journey** is the core of "The Little Girl": fear (of father's sternness) → relief (when he leaves) → terror (the beating) → comparison (Mr Macdonald as contrast) → understanding (the nightmare night). Memorise this arc.
2. ⭐ **"Rain on the Roof" has three stanzas, each with a distinct purpose**: Stanza 1 sets the scene (dark, rainy night, bliss of lying in bed); Stanza 2 describes the effect (rain triggers memories and fancies); Stanza 3 reveals the central memory (the poet's mother).
3. ⭐ **Literary devices in the poem must be memorised with exact examples**: Personification ("melancholy darkness gently weeps"), onomatopoeia ("patter"), alliteration ("humid shadows hover"), transferred epithet ("melancholy darkness"), metaphor ("air-threads into woof"), hyperbole ("a thousand dreamy fancies").
4. **The pin-cushion incident is the turning point** — it deepens Kezia's fear but also sets up the contrast. The father's anger is understandable (his important speech was destroyed), but his response (beating her with a ruler) is disproportionate. CBSE often asks about this incident.
5. **Mr Macdonald is a foil** — he exists in the story specifically to contrast with Kezia's father and make Kezia (and the reader) realise that different fathers express love differently.
6. **"Darling dreamers" refers to the poet and his siblings** sleeping as children, watched over by their mother. This is a frequently tested reference-to-context line.
7. **The verbs of reporting** tell us HOW something was said. In exams, you may be asked to identify or supply the correct verb. Key pairs: screamed/whispered (opposite intensity), ordered/suggested (opposite authority), complained/praised (opposite sentiment).
8. **Formal letter = 3-paragraph body** (intro + purpose, details, request + hope). Diary entry = first person, emotional, reflective. Know the difference in format and tone.

**Q7.** Describe the Macdonald family from Kezia's perspective. How did watching them change her understanding of fathers? ⭐

Watching the Macdonald family was a turning point for Kezia because it showed her a different model of fatherhood.

**Q8.** Explain the significance of the line: "What a big heart you've got, Father dear."
**A:**
This line is the emotional climax of the story and reveals the central theme.
1. Earlier in the story, Kezia saw her father as physically "big" in a terrifying way — "his hands and his neck, especially his mouth when he yawned" — and compared him to a giant.
2. Now, lying with her head on his chest, she can hear his heartbeat. The "big" has been transformed from a source of fear (his large, intimidating body) to a source of comfort (his large, loving heart).
3. The line shows that Kezia has finally understood her father's true nature: behind the stern exterior is a man who works tirelessly and loves his family deeply, even if he struggles to show it during the day.

**Q9.** Identify three literary devices in the following lines from "Rain on the Roof" and explain the effect of each:

> "When the humid shadows hover / Over all the starry spheres / And the melancholy darkness / Gently weeps in rainy tears"

**A:**
1. **Alliteration**: "humid shadows hover" — the repetition of the 'h' sound creates a hushed, atmospheric quality that mimics the quiet, heavy feeling of a night before rain.
2. **Personification**: "the melancholy darkness gently weeps in rainy tears" — darkness is given the human ability to weep, making the rain feel like the tears of a sad, living presence. This creates a mood of gentle sadness.
3. **Transferred epithet**: "melancholy darkness" — the darkness itself cannot feel sadness; it is the poet who feels melancholic. By attaching his emotion to the darkness, the poet blends his inner state with the outer scene, making nature a mirror of his feelings.

**Q11.** "The poem 'Rain on the Roof' uses the simple experience of listening to rain to explore the complex themes of memory, loss, and love." Discuss this statement with reference to the poem. (120-150 words)
**A:**

The poem "Rain on the Roof" by Coates Kinney uses the seemingly simple act of listening to rain to explore deeply emotional themes of memory, the loss of loved ones, and the enduring power of a mother's love.
1. In the first stanza, the poet establishes the physical setting — a dark, rainy night — but immediately transforms it into an emotional experience by calling the darkness "melancholy" and saying it "weeps," blending nature with human feeling.
2. In the second stanza, the rain becomes a catalyst for memory: "a thousand dreamy fancies" and "a thousand recollections" are triggered by the sound of each raindrop, suggesting that our inner world of memories is always waiting to be awakened.
3. The final stanza reveals the poem's emotional core — the memory of the poet's mother, who is no longer alive ("in years agone"). The rain brings back her "fond look" so vividly that he can "feel" it on him.
4. The use of hyperbole ("a thousand"), metaphor ("air-threads into woof"), and refrain ("the patter of the rain") creates a musical, meditative quality that mirrors the rhythmic, comforting, yet bittersweet nature of memory itself.
5. Thus, the poem teaches us that the simplest experiences — lying in bed, listening to rain — can carry the most profound emotional weight when they are connected to the people we love.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills) (1 question)

**Q12.** Both "The Little Girl" and "Rain on the Roof" deal with the theme of parental love, but they present it very differently. Compare how each text shows that a parent's love may not always be visible or openly expressed, and explain which presentation you find more powerful and why. (100-150 words)
**A:**

Both texts reveal that parental love can be hidden behind silence, distance, or even harshness — but they use different literary forms to make this point.

In "The Little Girl," love is hidden behind **strictness and physical intimidation**. Kezia's father appears cold and frightening: he gives orders, glares, and beats her. His love is only revealed in a single, private moment — when he comforts her during a nightmare. The story uses **narrative contrast** (the stern father vs the tender father) to show that actions speak louder than words, and that exhaustion can mask affection.

In "Rain on the Roof," love is hidden behind **time and death**. The poet's mother is no longer alive, and her love can only be accessed through memory, triggered by the rain. The poem uses **sensory imagery and symbolism** (the rain as a bridge between past and present) to show that love persists even after the person is gone.

I find the poem more powerful because it suggests that love transcends even death — a mother's "fond look" can be felt across years simply by listening to rain. While the story shows that love can be hidden by circumstance, the poem shows that love can never truly be lost.
