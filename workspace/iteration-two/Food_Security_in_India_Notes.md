## Core Content

### 3.1 Understanding Food Security: What It Means and Why It Matters

**What it is**: Food security means that all people, at all times, have enough food that is safe, nutritious, and affordable.

**How it works — the three dimensions**:
1. **Availability** — Enough food is produced within the country, imported, or available from previous years' stock stored in government granaries.
2. **Accessibility** — Food is within reach of every person — there are no physical or social barriers to obtaining it.
3. **Affordability** — Each individual has enough money to buy sufficient, safe, and nutritious food to meet their dietary needs.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: When food security exists, families are healthier, children perform better in school, and adults are more productive at work.
- **On the economy/society**: Food-secure populations contribute more to economic growth. Food insecurity, on the other hand, leads to malnutrition, lower productivity, and increased healthcare costs.

> **THINK Think About It:** If a country produces enough food for everyone but some people still go hungry, which dimension of food security is failing — availability, accessibility, or affordability?

⭐ **Why food security matters** — The poorest section of society may be food insecure most of the time. But even people above the poverty line can become food insecure during a natural disaster — an earthquake, drought, flood, tsunami, or a pandemic like COVID-19. During such calamities, food production drops, prices rise, and those with limited incomes can no longer afford to buy food.

**The chain from calamity to famine**:

| Stage | What Happens | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Natural calamity (drought, flood, etc.) | Total production of foodgrains decreases |
| 2 | Shortage of food in affected areas | Food prices rise sharply |
| 3 | People cannot afford food at high prices | Some families skip meals |
| 4 | Calamity is widespread or prolonged | Starvation begins |
| 5 | Massive, prolonged starvation | **Famine** — widespread deaths from starvation and disease |

### Case Study: The Bengal Famine of 1943

**Setting**: Bengal province, British India, 1943 — during World War II.
**Situation**: Bengal experienced a devastating famine that killed approximately 30 lakh (3 million) people. Rice production dropped significantly, but the data reveals a more complex picture.
**Key Economic Concept Illustrated**: Food availability alone does not guarantee food security — distribution and affordability matter equally.

**Production of Rice in Bengal (1938-1943)**

| Year | Production (Lakh tonnes) | Imports (Lakh tonnes) | Exports (Lakh tonnes) | Total Availability (Lakh tonnes) |
|------|--------------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------|----------------------------------|
| 1938 | 85 | — | — | 85 |
| 1939 | 79 | 04 | — | 83 |
| 1940 | 82 | 03 | — | 85 |
| 1941 | 68 | 02 | — | 70 |
| 1942 | 93 | 01 | — | 92 |
| 1943 | 76 | 03 | — | 79 |

**Outcome**: Even though production in 1943 (76 lakh tonnes) was higher than in 1941 (68 lakh tonnes), the famine was far more devastating. This is because food was not distributed properly — war-time policies diverted food away from Bengal, prices skyrocketed, and poor people simply could not afford to buy food.
**Lesson**: A famine is not always caused by a drop in food production. It can result from a failure of distribution and affordability — the other two pillars of food security.

> **THINK Think About It:** Looking at the table above, 1941 shows the lowest total availability (70 lakh tonnes). Why did the famine happen in 1943 and not 1941? What does this tell us about the causes of famine?

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2021), India's food security system was severely tested. The lockdown disrupted supply chains, daily-wage workers lost incomes, and millions of migrant workers walked hundreds of kilometres home. The government responded by distributing extra foodgrains (free of cost) under the Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PM-GKAY) — providing 5 kg of foodgrains per person per month to approximately 80 crore people for over two years.

### 3.2 Who Is Food-Insecure in India?

**What it is**: Despite India becoming self-sufficient in foodgrains, large sections of the population still suffer from food and nutrition insecurity.

**How it works**:
1. **In rural areas**: The worst-affected are landless labourers, traditional artisans, petty self-employed workers, and destitutes (including beggars). Their income is seasonal, low, and unpredictable.
2. **In urban areas**: Workers in ill-paid occupations and casual labour markets are most vulnerable. They lack job security and earn barely enough for survival.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Malnutrition, stunted growth in children, vulnerability to disease, and inability to work productively.
- **On the economy/society**: High food insecurity creates a cycle of poverty — malnourished workers are less productive, which keeps them poor, which keeps them food insecure.

△ **Social composition and food insecurity**: The SCs (Scheduled Castes), STs (Scheduled Tribes), and some sections of the OBCs (Other Backward Classes) who have poor land or low land productivity are particularly vulnerable. Women and children under 5 are also disproportionately affected — the NHFS 1998-99 found that approximately 11 crore women and children suffered from malnutrition.

> **THINK Think About It:** Ahmad, a rickshaw puller in Bangalore, earns an irregular income but survives because he has a yellow card (BPL ration card) that gives him wheat, rice, sugar, and kerosene at half the market price. What would happen to families like Ahmad's if the PDS did not exist?

### 3.3 Chronic Hunger vs Seasonal Hunger

**What it is**: Hunger is not just an expression of poverty — it also brings about poverty. The two main types of hunger operate differently and affect different groups.

**Comparison: Chronic Hunger vs Seasonal Hunger**

| Aspect | Chronic Hunger | Seasonal Hunger |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Nature** | Persistent and constant | Cyclical, linked to agricultural/activity seasons |
| **Cause** | Very low income and inability to buy food | Unemployment during certain periods of the year |
| **Affected groups** | Poor people with irregular or no income | Agricultural labourers, casual construction workers |
| **Duration** | Year-round | A few months each year |
| **Rural or Urban** | Both, but more visible in urban slums | Rural areas (between harvests) and urban areas (e.g., monsoon for construction) |
| **Example** | A beggar who barely eats one meal a day | Ramu, who goes hungry during the 4 months between crop cycles |

**Percentage of Households with Hunger in India**

| Year | Seasonal (Rural) | Chronic (Rural) | Total (Rural) | Seasonal (Urban) | Chronic (Urban) | Total (Urban) |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1983 | 16.2% | 2.3% | 18.5% | 5.6% | 0.8% | 6.4% |
| 1993-94 | 4.2% | 0.9% | 5.1% | 1.1% | 0.5% | 1.6% |
| 1999-2000 | 2.6% | 0.7% | 3.3% | 0.6% | 0.3% | 0.9% |

Source: Sagar (2004)

The data shows a significant decline in both types of hunger over the decades — rural hunger dropped from 18.5% to 3.3% and urban hunger from 6.4% to 0.9% between 1983 and 1999-2000. This improvement is largely attributed to the Green Revolution and expanded food security programmes.

> **THINK Think About It:** Hunger has declined significantly since 1983, but the data stops at 1999-2000. Based on what you know about India's population growth and economic changes since then, do you think this trend has continued? What factors might have helped or hindered it?

### 3.4 India's Food Security System: Buffer Stock and PDS

**What it is**: India has a carefully designed food security system composed of two main components — **buffer stock** and the **Public Distribution System (PDS)**. Together, they form the backbone of India's fight against hunger.

**How it works**:

**Flow: Food Security System**

```
Farmers (surplus states) → FCI procures at MSP → Stored in granaries (Buffer Stock) → Distributed through PDS (Ration Shops) → Consumers (poor households at subsidised Issue Price)
```

Each stage:
1. **Farmers grow foodgrains** — primarily wheat and rice — in surplus states like Punjab, Haryana, and Andhra Pradesh.
2. **FCI procures at MSP** — the Food Corporation of India buys crops at a pre-announced Minimum Support Price, providing income security to farmers.
3. **Stored as buffer stock** — procured foodgrains are stored in government granaries to be used during shortages and for distribution to the poor.
4. **PDS distributes food** — foodgrains are supplied to about 5.5 lakh ration (fair price) shops across the country.
5. **Poor households buy at subsidised rates** — families with ration cards buy foodgrains, sugar, and kerosene at prices lower than market price (issue price).

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Poor families can access food at affordable prices even when market prices rise.
- **On the economy/society**: The system stabilises food prices across the country, prevents famine, and supports farmers by guaranteeing a minimum price for their crops.

⭐ **Minimum Support Price (MSP)** is declared by the government every year before the sowing season. It provides incentives to farmers for growing foodgrains by guaranteeing that the government will buy their crop at that price, regardless of market conditions.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** Farmers' protests in India (2020-2021) were largely centred on the demand for a legal guarantee of MSP for all crops, not just wheat and rice. The protestors argued that without a guaranteed MSP, they are at the mercy of market fluctuations and corporate buyers.

**The Green Revolution and Self-Sufficiency**

India has become self-sufficient in foodgrains over the past few decades, largely due to the **Green Revolution** — a new agricultural strategy that dramatically increased wheat and rice production. Prime Minister Indira Gandhi released a special stamp titled "Wheat Revolution" in July 1968 to mark this achievement.

**Production of Foodgrains in India**

| Year | Production (Million Tonnes) | What It Means |
|------|------------------------------|---------------|
| 2010-11 | 130 | Baseline year in the recent data series |
| 2012-13 | 176 | Significant jump — good monsoon year |
| 2014-15 | 245 | Crossed 240 MT — strong growth phase |

Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh recorded the highest production growth — 58 and 33 million tonnes respectively in 2020-21. UP and West Bengal led rice production (16 and 17 million tonnes), while UP and MP led wheat production (36 and 18 million tonnes).

### 3.5 Evolution of the Public Distribution System

**What it is**: The PDS is India's primary food security instrument. It has evolved significantly since its introduction, becoming more targeted over time.

**How it works — timeline of PDS evolution**:

| Year | Milestone | What Changed |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1940s | **Rationing introduced** | Against the backdrop of the Bengal Famine — first food distribution system |
| 1960s | **PDS revived** | Before the Green Revolution — in response to acute food shortages |
| 1975 | **ICDS launched** | Integrated Child Development Services — focused on children and mothers |
| 1977-78 | **Food-for-Work (FFW)** | Employed people in public works in exchange for foodgrains |
| 1992 | **Revamped PDS (RPDS)** | Extended PDS to 1,700 remote and backward blocks |
| 1997 | **Targeted PDS (TPDS)** | Differentiated pricing for BPL and APL families — poor pay less |
| 2000 | **AAY + Annapurna launched** | Poorest of the poor get 35 kg at Rs 2-3/kg; indigent senior citizens get 10 kg free |
| 2013 | **NFSA enacted** | 75% rural + 50% urban population covered; 5 kg per person per month |

**Comparison of PDS Schemes — Key Features**

| Name of Scheme | Year | Coverage / Target Group | Foodgrain Volume | Issue Price (Rs/kg) |
|----------------|------|------------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| PDS (Universal) | Up to 1992 | Everyone | — | Wheat: 2.34, Rice: 2.89 |
| RPDS | 1992 | Backward blocks | 20 kg/month | Wheat: 2.80, Rice: 3.77 |
| TPDS | 1997/2000 | BPL and APL | 35 kg/month | BPL: W 4.15, R 5.65; APL: W 6.10, R 8.30 |
| AAY | 2002 | Poorest of the poor | 35 kg/month | Wheat: 2.00, Rice: 3.00 |
| APS | 2000 | Indigent senior citizens | 10 kg/month | Free |
| NFSA | 2013 | Priority households | 5 kg/person/month | Wheat: 2.00, Rice: 3.00, Coarse grains: 1.00 |

**Three types of ration cards**:
1. **Antyodaya cards** — for the poorest of the poor (lowest prices, highest subsidy)
2. **BPL cards** — for families below the poverty line (moderate subsidy)
3. **APL cards** — for all others (minimal subsidy, prices close to market rate)

⭐ **The National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013** is a landmark law that provides food and nutritional security at affordable prices, enabling people to live with dignity. It covers 75% of the rural population and 50% of the urban population, entitling them to 5 kg of foodgrains per person per month at subsidised rates.

> **THINK Think About It:** The PDS started as a universal system (same price for everyone). It was later changed to a targeted system (different prices for BPL and APL). Do you think targeting makes the system more efficient, or does it exclude some genuinely needy families who fall just above the BPL line?

### 3.6 Problems with the Food Security System

**What it is**: Despite its achievements, India's food security system has significant problems — including overflowing godowns, wastage, diversion of foodgrains, and environmental damage caused by intensive farming.

**How it works — the problems**:
1. **FCI godowns overflowing**: In 2022, the Central Pool had 159 LMT of wheat and 104 LMT of rice — far above the minimum buffer norms. Some foodgrains rot in storage or are eaten by rats.
2. **High carrying costs**: Storing massive food stocks costs the government enormous amounts in transportation, storage, and maintenance.
3. **PDS malpractices**: Ration shop dealers sometimes divert foodgrains to the open market for better margins, sell poor quality grains, or open shops irregularly.
4. **Regional concentration of procurement**: Most procurement happens in a few prosperous states (Punjab, Haryana, Western UP, Andhra Pradesh). This has led to environmental degradation — excessive water use for rice cultivation has depleted groundwater in these states.
5. **Shift from coarse grains to wheat and rice**: Rising MSP has encouraged farmers to grow wheat and rice instead of coarse grains (jowar, bajra, ragi) — which are the staple food of the poor and more nutritious.
6. **Declining PDS offtake by APL families**: APL families get almost no discount at ration shops — prices are close to market rates — so they have little incentive to buy from PDS.

△ **Per capita consumption of foodgrains is declining**: According to NSSO Report No. 558, per person per month consumption of rice declined from 6.38 kg (2004-05) to 5.98 kg (2011-12) in rural India. However, per capita consumption of PDS rice has doubled in rural areas and increased by 66% in urban areas since 2004-05 — meaning more people are relying on the PDS for their food.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Poor families may receive low-quality or insufficient foodgrains from ration shops. Malnutrition persists despite abundant food stocks.
- **On the economy/society**: Wasted foodgrains represent a massive economic loss. Environmental damage from water-intensive farming threatens long-term agricultural sustainability.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** The debate over agricultural subsidies and MSP continues to shape global trade. India's food subsidy programme (which supports PDS) is one of the largest in the world, costing over Rs 2 lakh crore annually. At the WTO, India has argued that its food security programmes are essential for protecting the poor and should not be restricted by international trade rules.

> **THINK Think About It:** If FCI godowns are overflowing with foodgrains and some are rotting, while people in other parts of the country are going hungry, what does this tell us about the problem — is it one of production, distribution, or both?

### 3.7 Role of Cooperatives in Food Security

**What it is**: Cooperatives are community-owned organisations that play a significant role in food security, especially in southern and western India. They set up shops to sell goods at controlled prices to poor people.

**How it works**:
1. **Cooperatives buy goods in bulk** at lower prices.
2. **They sell directly to consumers** at prices controlled or subsidised by state governments.
3. **Profits are shared among members** or reinvested in the community.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Consumers get essential items at affordable prices, especially in areas where government PDS may be weak.
- **On the economy/society**: Cooperatives reduce dependence on middlemen, create local employment, and often provide better quality goods than private shops.

**Success Stories of Cooperatives**

| Cooperative | Region | Contribution | Impact |
|------------|--------|-------------|--------|
| Tamil Nadu Cooperative Societies | Tamil Nadu | Run about 94% of all fair price shops | Strong PDS — one of the best in the country |
| Mother Dairy | Delhi | Provides milk and vegetables at government-controlled rates | Affordable dairy for urban consumers |
| Amul (GCMMF) | Gujarat | Milk and milk products cooperative | Led India's White Revolution — made India the world's largest milk producer |
| Academy of Development Science (ADS) | Maharashtra | Set up grain banks through a network of NGOs | Innovative food security intervention — acknowledged as successful model |

△ **Grain Banks**: The ADS in Maharashtra has set up grain banks in different regions. These banks store foodgrains collected from the community and redistribute them during lean periods. ADS also trains NGOs on food security management, creating a multiplier effect.

> **THINK Think About It:** Amul is a dairy cooperative that made India the world's largest milk producer. Could a similar cooperative model work for foodgrains in India? What challenges might it face?

## Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

| # | Misconception / Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|------------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Food security only means producing enough food — if India grows 310 million tonnes, everyone is food secure." | Production is only ONE of three dimensions. Food can be abundant but inaccessible or unaffordable for the poor. | Food security = Availability + Accessibility + Affordability. All three must be present simultaneously. |
| 2 | "The Bengal Famine happened because of a shortage of rice production." | The table shows that production in 1943 (76 LMT) was higher than in 1941 (68 LMT). The real cause was a failure of distribution and wartime policies. | Famine is caused by the failure of the entire food system — production, distribution, AND affordability. The 1943 famine was a crisis of access, not just production. |
| 3 | "Green Revolution solved India's food problem completely." | Green Revolution increased production of wheat and rice, but it caused environmental damage (water depletion, soil degradation) and increased inequality — benefiting surplus states more. | Green Revolution had both positives (more food, self-sufficiency) and negatives (environmental damage, neglect of coarse grains, regional inequality). Always present both sides. |
| 4 | "PDS means everyone gets free food from the government." | PDS provides food at subsidised prices, not free (except APS for senior citizens and some emergency distributions). Different card holders get different subsidies. | Only AAY and NFSA beneficiaries get the highest subsidies. APL families pay prices close to market rates. |
| 5 | "Buffer stock is a waste — the food just rots in godowns." | Buffer stock serves a critical purpose — it prevents price spikes during shortages, provides food during disasters, and stabilises supply. The problem is excess, not the concept itself. | Buffer stock is essential for food security. The issue is better management — procuring only what is needed, improving storage, and ensuring timely distribution. |
| 6 | "Hunger and malnutrition are the same thing." | Hunger is about not getting enough food (quantity). Malnutrition is about not getting the right kind of food (quality — proteins, vitamins, minerals). | A person can have enough calories (no hunger) but still be malnourished if their diet lacks essential nutrients. India faces both challenges simultaneously. |

## Exam-Oriented Summary

### Top 8 Things to Remember
1. ⭐ **Food security = Availability + Accessibility + Affordability** — all three dimensions must be present simultaneously. This is the single most tested definition in this chapter.
2. ⭐ **Buffer stock and PDS are the twin pillars** of India's food security system. Buffer stock ensures supply; PDS ensures distribution at affordable prices.
3. ⭐ **FCI procures foodgrains at MSP** from surplus states (Punjab, Haryana, AP) and distributes through 5.5 lakh ration shops across India.
4. **The Bengal Famine (1943)** killed 30 lakh people — it was caused by a failure of distribution and affordability, not just production. This is a frequently cited case study.
5. ⭐ **NFSA 2013** covers 75% rural + 50% urban population, providing 5 kg foodgrains per person per month at subsidised rates (wheat Rs 2/kg, rice Rs 3/kg, coarse grains Rs 1/kg).
6. **Most food-insecure states**: UP (eastern/south-eastern), Bihar, Jharkhand, Odisha, West Bengal, Chhattisgarh, parts of MP and Maharashtra.
7. **Chronic hunger** = persistent, due to low income. **Seasonal hunger** = cyclical, due to seasonal unemployment. Rural hunger declined from 18.5% (1983) to 3.3% (1999-2000).
8. **Problems with the system**: Overflowing godowns, food wastage, PDS malpractices (diversion, poor quality), regional concentration of procurement, and environmental damage from water-intensive farming.

### Model Answers

#### 1-Mark Questions

**Q:** What is food security?
**A:** Food security means availability, accessibility, and affordability of food to all people at all times. It ensures that everyone has sufficient, safe, and nutritious food.

**Q:** What is buffer stock?
**A:** Buffer stock is the stock of foodgrains (wheat and rice) procured by the government through the Food Corporation of India (FCI) and stored in granaries for distribution during shortages and to the poor.

**Q:** Name two food security programmes of the Government of India.
**A:** (1) Antyodaya Anna Yojana (AAY) — provides 35 kg foodgrains/month at Rs 2-3/kg to the poorest of the poor. (2) Mid-Day Meal Scheme — provides free meals to school children.

#### 3-Mark Questions

**Q:** Who are the food-insecure people in India?
**A:**
The food-insecure people in India include:
1. **Landless labourers and poor households in rural areas** — who depend on seasonal agricultural work and have very low or uncertain incomes.
2. **Casual labourers in urban areas** — employed in ill-paid occupations with no job security, whose daily earnings fluctuate.
3. **Socially vulnerable groups** — SCs, STs, women (especially pregnant and nursing mothers), and children under 5 years, who face higher malnutrition rates.

**Q:** Differentiate between chronic hunger and seasonal hunger.
**A:**
1. **Chronic hunger** is a persistent condition where people suffer from inadequate diet in terms of quantity and/or quality throughout the year due to very low income. For example, daily-wage labourers who cannot afford two meals a day.
2. **Seasonal hunger** is related to cycles of food growing and harvesting, where people are unable to get work during certain months. For example, agricultural labourers who face unemployment between harvests.

**Q:** What are the three dimensions of food security?
**A:**
Food security has three essential dimensions:
1. **Availability of food** — food production within the country, food imports, and previous years' stock stored in government granaries.
2. **Accessibility** — food is within reach of every person, with no physical or social barriers.
3. **Affordability** — individuals have enough money to buy sufficient, safe, and nutritious food to meet dietary needs.

#### 5-Mark Questions

**Q:** Describe the role of the Public Distribution System (PDS) in ensuring food security in India. What problems does it face? ⭐
**A:**
The Public Distribution System (PDS) is the most important instrument of government policy for ensuring food security in India.
1. **Distribution mechanism**: PDS distributes foodgrains procured by FCI through about 5.5 lakh ration (fair price) shops across the country at subsidised prices lower than market rates.
2. **Price stabilisation**: PDS has been instrumental in stabilising food prices and making food available to consumers at affordable prices, preventing price spikes during shortages.
3. **Famine prevention**: By supplying food from surplus regions to deficit ones, PDS has averted widespread hunger and famine in India since independence.
4. **Farmer income support**: The MSP-procurement-PDS system has contributed to increased foodgrain production and provided income security to farmers in surplus states.
5. **Problems faced**: PDS suffers from malpractices — dealers divert grains to open market, sell poor quality grains, open shops irregularly. APL families get almost no discount, reducing their incentive to buy from PDS. FCI godowns overflow while people in some regions go hungry, showing distribution gaps.

**Q:** How has the Government of India ensured food security through various programmes and schemes? ⭐
**A:**
The Government of India has designed a comprehensive food security system through multiple programmes:
1. **Buffer stock and PDS**: The twin pillars of food security — FCI procures foodgrains at MSP, stores them as buffer stock, and distributes through 5.5 lakh ration shops at subsidised issue prices.
2. **Evolution of PDS**: From universal PDS (1940s) to RPDS (1992) for backward areas, to TPDS (1997) targeting poor with differential pricing, to AAY (2000) for the poorest of the poor, and finally NFSA (2013) covering 75% rural and 50% urban population.
3. **Nutrition-focused schemes**: ICDS (1975) provides nutrition to children and mothers; Mid-Day Meal Scheme provides free meals to school children, improving both nutrition and school attendance.
4. **Employment-linked schemes**: Food-for-Work (1977-78) and MGNREGA provide employment and wages to rural poor, enhancing their purchasing power and food access.
5. **Cooperative movement**: Cooperatives like Amul, Mother Dairy, and Tamil Nadu cooperative societies supplement government efforts by providing essential items at controlled prices.

### 2-Mark Questions

**Q4.** What is the role of cooperatives in food security? Give one example. ⭐
**A:**
1. Cooperatives set up shops to sell essential goods at controlled, subsidised prices to poor people, supplementing government PDS efforts.
2. **Example**: In Tamil Nadu, cooperatives run approximately 94% of all fair price shops, making it one of the most effective PDS systems in India.

**Q5.** Differentiate between Issue Price and Minimum Support Price.
**A:**
1. **Minimum Support Price (MSP)** is the price at which the government **buys** foodgrains from farmers (before sowing season) — it is the purchase price paid to producers.
2. **Issue Price** is the subsidised price at which the government **sells** foodgrains to poor households through PDS ration shops — it is lower than the market price.

**Q6.** Name the three types of ration cards in India.
**A:**
1. **Antyodaya cards** — for the poorest of the poor.
2. **BPL (Below Poverty Line) cards** — for families below the poverty line.
3. **APL (Above Poverty Line) cards** — for all other families.

### 3-Mark Questions

**Q7.** What is buffer stock? Why is it created by the government? ⭐
**A:**
Buffer stock is the stock of foodgrains (wheat and rice) procured by the government through the Food Corporation of India (FCI) and stored in granaries.
1. **To distribute foodgrains in deficit areas** — surplus food from states like Punjab and Haryana is moved to states with food shortages.
2. **To provide food at subsidised prices to the poor** — the issue price is lower than the market price, making food affordable for low-income families.
3. **To address shortages during calamities** — buffer stock ensures food supply during droughts, floods, and other natural disasters when production drops.

**Q8.** Why is food security needed during natural calamities and pandemics?
**A:**
Food security is crucial during crises because:
1. **Natural calamities reduce food production** — droughts, floods, and cyclones destroy crops, creating food shortages in affected areas.
2. **Prices rise during shortages** — reduced supply pushes up food prices, making it unaffordable for poor families who are most vulnerable.
3. **Pandemics disrupt economic activity** — as seen during COVID-19, lockdowns caused job losses and income reduction, making it difficult for millions to buy food even when food was available.

**Q9.** What problems are associated with the Public Distribution System?
**A:**
The PDS faces several challenges:
1. **Malpractices by dealers** — diversion of foodgrains to open market for higher margins, selling poor quality grains, and irregular opening of shops.
2. **Overflowing godowns and wastage** — FCI godowns often have more foodgrains than needed, leading to rotting, rat damage, and high storage costs.
3. **Exclusion of needy families** — under TPDS, families marginally above the poverty line get almost no subsidy, while some genuinely poor families may be excluded due to classification errors.

### 5-Mark Questions

**Q10.** "India has become self-sufficient in foodgrains, yet food insecurity persists." Explain this paradox with reference to the food security system and its problems. ⭐
**A:**
India produces over 310 million tonnes of foodgrains annually (2021-22), making it self-sufficient, yet food insecurity persists due to gaps in accessibility and affordability.
1. **Production is not distributed evenly** — procurement is concentrated in a few prosperous states (Punjab, Haryana, AP), while food-insecure states (Bihar, Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh) receive less. The food reaches godowns but not all hungry households.
2. **Buffer stock inefficiency** — FCI godowns held 159 LMT of wheat and 104 LMT of rice in 2022, far above buffer norms. Foodgrains rot while people go hungry — this is a distribution failure, not a production failure.
3. **PDS malpractices** — ration shop dealers divert foodgrains to open markets, sell poor quality grains, and open shops irregularly, denying the poor their entitled food.
4. **Poverty and affordability** — even at subsidised PDS prices, the poorest families may not have enough money. Those without ration cards (especially migrant workers) fall outside the system entirely.
5. **Nutrition gap** — India focuses on wheat and rice (calories), but malnutrition persists because diets lack proteins, vitamins, and minerals. Coarse grains (more nutritious) are neglected as MSP policies favour wheat and rice.

**Q11.** Describe the evolution of the Public Distribution System from its origins to the National Food Security Act of 2013. How has the system become more targeted over time?
**A:**
The PDS has evolved significantly from a universal system to a rights-based targeted approach over seven decades.
1. **Origins (1940s)**: Rationing was first introduced in India during the 1940s in response to the Bengal Famine of 1943 — the earliest form of government food distribution.
2. **Revival (1960s)**: The system was revived and strengthened during acute food shortages of the 1960s, before the Green Revolution made India self-sufficient in foodgrains.
3. **Additional programmes (1970s)**: ICDS (1975) for child and maternal nutrition, and Food-for-Work (1977-78) linking employment to food security were introduced.
4. **RPDS (1992)**: The Revamped PDS targeted 1,700 backward and remote blocks that were previously excluded, with 20 kg of foodgrains per month.
5. **TPDS (1997)**: The Targeted PDS introduced differential pricing — BPL families got foodgrains at lower prices than APL families, making the system more focused on the poor.
6. **AAY and APS (2000)**: Special schemes for the "poorest of the poor" (AAY — 35 kg at Rs 2-3/kg) and indigent senior citizens (APS — 10 kg free) further sharpened targeting.
7. **NFSA (2013)**: The landmark Act converted food security into a legal right, covering 75% rural + 50% urban population with 5 kg per person per month — moving from welfare to entitlement.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills)

**Q12.** "Increasing MSP every year may actually harm food security in the long run." Do you agree? Give reasons for your answer.
**A:**

I partially agree with this statement. While MSP is essential for protecting farmers' incomes, indiscriminate increases create problems for food security:

1. **Procurement costs spiral**: Higher MSP means the government pays more for foodgrains, increasing the food subsidy burden. This money could otherwise be spent on irrigation, healthcare, or education — which also contribute to food security.

2. **Environmental damage**: Higher MSP for wheat and rice encourages farmers in Punjab and Haryana to grow water-intensive crops year after year. Groundwater levels have fallen drastically, threatening the long-term sustainability of agriculture in these very states that feed India.

3. **Neglect of coarse grains**: MSP incentivises wheat and rice but not coarse grains (jowar, bajra, ragi). Coarse grains are more nutritious, require less water, and are the staple food of the poor. Shifting to monoculture of wheat and rice makes the poor's diets less diverse and more vulnerable.

4. **Storage crisis**: Higher MSP leads to higher procurement, which leads to overflowing FCI godowns, wastage, and rotting foodgrains — a direct food security failure.

However, removing or freezing MSP entirely would hurt farmers, reduce production incentives, and could itself threaten food availability. The solution lies in balanced MSP — fair to farmers, fiscally sustainable for the government, and environmentally responsible.

### Case-Based / Source-Based Questions

*Ahmad is a rickshaw puller in Bangalore who shifted from Jhumri Taliah along with his 3 brothers, 2 sisters and old parents. He stays in a jhuggi.
