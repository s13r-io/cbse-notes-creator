## Section 1: Chapter Introduction

### What This Chapter Is About

Imagine a country where a queen is nicknamed "Madame Deficit," where the poorest 90% of people pay all the taxes, and where a single loaf of bread can cost a worker an entire day's wages. This was France in 1789 — a powder keg waiting for a spark. That spark came on 14 July 1789, when ordinary men and women stormed a fortress-prison called the Bastille and changed the course of world history. This chapter tells the story of how the people of France overthrew a centuries-old monarchy, declared that all humans are born free and equal, and set in motion ideas that would shape democracies across the globe — including India's own freedom struggle.

### Why This Matters

- **Real life**: The ideas of liberty, equality, and fraternity that emerged from the French Revolution form the basis of democratic governments worldwide. The Preamble to the Indian Constitution itself draws on these very principles.
- **Other subjects**: In Political Science, you will study democratic rights and constitutional design — ideas whose origins lie in this revolution.
- **Exams**: This chapter carries approximately 4-5 marks in the CBSE Social Science paper (out of ~20 marks for History). Source-based questions and map work are frequently asked from this chapter.
- **Broader world**: Every modern revolution, protest movement, and demand for equal rights carries the DNA of 1789. From the Arab Spring to anti-corruption protests in India, the French Revolution's slogan of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" continues to resonate.

### What You'll Learn

After studying this chapter, you will be able to:
1. Describe the social and economic structure of French society under the Old Regime and explain why it led to resentment.
2. Explain the role of the Estates General, the Tennis Court Oath, and the storming of the Bastille in triggering the revolution.
3. Trace the political changes from constitutional monarchy (1791) to republic (1792) to the Reign of Terror (1793-94).
4. Evaluate the role of different groups — the middle class, peasants, women, and the Jacobins — in driving and shaping the revolution.
5. Assess the legacy of the French Revolution for the modern world, including its influence on democratic movements in India and other colonies.

## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 French Society Under the Old Regime

**Background**: In 1774, **Louis XVI** of the Bourbon dynasty became king of France at the age of 20. He inherited a kingdom with an empty treasury, deep social inequality, and a system that placed the entire tax burden on the poorest citizens.

**Causes of Discontent**:

1. **Financial Crisis**: France had spent enormous sums on wars, including supporting the American colonies against Britain. The national debt exceeded 2 billion livres, and lenders charged 10% interest. The cost of maintaining the lavish court at the Palace of Versailles added further strain.
2. **Unfair Tax System**: French society was divided into three estates. The First Estate (clergy) and Second Estate (nobility) enjoyed tax exemptions and feudal privileges. Only the Third Estate — 90% of the population — paid taxes including taille (direct tax), tithes (Church tax), and indirect taxes on salt and tobacco.
3. **Social Inequality**: Peasants, who made up 90% of the population, owned very little land. About 60% of the land was owned by nobles, the Church, and richer members of the Third Estate. Peasants also had to perform forced labour (feudal dues) for their lords.

**What Happened**: The population of France grew from 23 million in 1715 to 28 million in 1789, creating enormous demand for food. Production could not keep pace, and bread prices rose sharply. Wages did not rise with prices, creating a subsistence crisis. Poor harvests in 1788 made things worse, leading to food riots. Meanwhile, a new **middle class** of merchants, lawyers, and educated professionals began questioning the system of privileges.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Widespread anger against the monarchy, rising bread prices, and frequent riots in cities and the countryside.
- **Long-term**: The accumulation of grievances created the conditions for revolution. The middle class, inspired by Enlightenment philosophers, began to imagine a society based on equality and merit rather than birth.

| Estate | Population Share | Tax Burden | Privileges |
|--------|-----------------|------------|------------|
| First Estate (Clergy) | ~0.5% | Exempt from taille | Collected tithes; owned land |
| Second Estate (Nobility) | ~2% | Exempt from taille | Feudal dues; court positions |
| Third Estate (Everyone else) | ~97.5% | Paid ALL taxes — taille, tithes, indirect taxes | None — no political power |

> **THINK Think About It:** If you were a peasant in 1789 France who earned just enough to buy bread, but then the price of bread doubled while your wages stayed the same, what would you do? Would you blame the king, the nobles, or the baker?

⭐ **Enlightenment Thinkers Who Inspired the Revolution**

| Philosopher | Key Work | Core Idea |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **John Locke** | *Two Treatises of Government* | Rejected the divine right of kings; argued that governments exist to protect natural rights (life, liberty, property) |
| **Jean Jacques Rousseau** | *The Social Contract* | Proposed that government should be based on a contract between people and their representatives; sovereignty belongs to the people |
| **Montesquieu** | *The Spirit of the Laws* | Proposed separation of powers — legislature, executive, and judiciary — to prevent tyranny |

Articles 50, 53, and 124 create three independent branches — the executive (President + Council of Ministers), the legislature (Parliament), and the judiciary (Supreme Court + High Courts) — each acting as a check on the others.

### 3.2 The Outbreak of the Revolution (1789)

**Background**: Louis XVI needed to increase taxes but could not do so unilaterally. Under the Old Regime, only the **Estates General** could approve new taxes, and it had not been called since 1614.

**Causes**:

1. **Tax Deadlock**: The king called the Estates General on 5 May 1789 at Versailles. The First and Second Estates sent 300 representatives each; the Third Estate sent 600. But voting followed the old system — each estate got one vote, meaning the clergy and nobility could always outvote the Third Estate 2-to-1.
2. **Demand for Voting Reform**: The Third Estate demanded individual voting (one person, one vote), based on Rousseau's ideas. When the king refused, they walked out of the assembly.
3. **Popular Anger**: Bread prices had soared, and the king had stationed troops in Paris, fuelling fears of a crackdown on civilians.

**What Happened**: On 20 June 1789, the representatives of the Third Estate gathered in a tennis court at Versailles and swore not to disperse until they had drafted a constitution for France. This is called the **Tennis Court Oath**. They declared themselves the **National Assembly**, led by **Mirabeau** (a nobleman who supported reform) and **Abbe Sieyes** (a priest who wrote the pamphlet "What is the Third Estate?").

Meanwhile, the people of Paris took matters into their own hands. On 14 July 1789, a crowd of about 7,000 men and women stormed the **Bastille**, a fortress-prison that symbolised royal despotism. The commander was killed, the prisoners freed, and the fortress demolished. In the countryside, peasants attacked chateaux, burned records of feudal dues, and looted hoarded grain — a wave of panic known as the **Great Fear**.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Louis XVI recognised the National Assembly. On 4 August 1789, the Assembly abolished the feudal system, including tithes and feudal dues. Church lands were confiscated (worth 2 billion livres). These were the first concrete revolutionary reforms.
- **Long-term**: The storming of the Bastille became the defining symbol of the revolution — 14 July is still celebrated as France's national day (Bastille Day). The Tennis Court Oath demonstrated that ordinary people could challenge absolute monarchy and win.

### 3.3 From Constitutional Monarchy to Republic (1791-1793)

**Background**: The National Assembly drafted the **Constitution of 1791**, transforming France into a **constitutional monarchy**. This was a moderate step — the king remained, but his powers were limited and separated into legislature, executive, and judiciary (inspired by Montesquieu).

**Causes of Further Change**:

1. **Limited Political Rights**: The Constitution granted voting rights only to "active citizens" — men above 25 who paid taxes equal to at least 3 days of a labourer's wage. All women and poorer men were classified as "passive citizens" with no vote. This angered the poorer sections.
2. **Royal Betrayal**: Louis XVI secretly negotiated with the King of Prussia, plotting to crush the revolution with foreign help. This confirmed suspicions that the king could not be trusted.
3. **War and Economic Hardship**: France declared war on Prussia and Austria in April 1792. The war brought economic difficulties, food shortages, and rising prices, turning people further against the monarchy.

**What Happened**: Political clubs became centres of opposition. The most influential was the **Jacobin Club**, whose members came from the less prosperous sections — shopkeepers, artisans, servants, and daily-wage workers. They dressed in long striped trousers (sans-culottes) and red caps to distinguish themselves from the nobility. Their leader was **Maximilian Robespierre**.

On 10 August 1792, the Jacobins and Parisians stormed the Palace of the Tuileries, killed the king's guards, and imprisoned the royal family. Elections were held with universal male suffrage (all men aged 21 and above could vote). The newly elected assembly, called the **Convention**, abolished the monarchy on 21 September 1792 and declared France a **republic**. Louis XVI was tried for treason and executed by guillotine on 21 January 1793. Marie Antoinette met the same fate shortly after.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: France was now a republic — no king, no hereditary rule. Power lay with elected representatives. Universal male suffrage was introduced for elections.
- **Long-term**: The execution of the king sent shockwaves across Europe. Other monarchies (Prussia, Austria, Britain, Spain) formed coalitions against France. The revolution entered its most radical and violent phase — the Reign of Terror.

### Key Personalities

**Louis XVI** (1754-1793)
- **Role**: King of France (1774-1792)
- **Key Actions**: Called the Estates General in 1789; accepted the constitutional monarchy reluctantly; secretly negotiated with Prussia against the revolution
- **Significance**: His indecisiveness and secret betrayal of the revolution turned public opinion against the monarchy and led to his own execution

**Marie Antoinette** (1755-1793)
- **Role**: Queen of France, Austrian princess
- **Key Actions**: Known for extravagant spending at a time of national financial crisis; nicknamed "Madame Deficit"
- **Significance**: Her lavish lifestyle became a symbol of royal indifference to the suffering of the common people

**Mirabeau** (1749-1791)
- **Role**: Leader of the early National Assembly; born into nobility but supported the Third Estate
- **Key Actions**: Delivered powerful speeches at Versailles; brought out a journal to spread revolutionary ideas
- **Significance**: Demonstrated that even members of the privileged classes supported reform — he was a bridge between the estates

**Maximilian Robespierre** (1758-1794)
- **Role**: Leader of the Jacobin Club; the most powerful figure during the Reign of Terror
- **Key Actions**: Advocated for democracy, equality, and the execution of enemies of the republic; imposed price controls and rationing; closed churches
- **Significance**: His ruthless pursuit of "virtue through terror" saved the republic from foreign invasion but also made him a tyrant — he was eventually guillotined by his own supporters

**Olympe de Gouges** (1748-1793)
- **Role**: Political activist and playwright; one of the first feminists
- **Key Actions**: Wrote the "Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen" (1791); criticised the Jacobin government for closing women's clubs
- **Significance**: Challenged the exclusion of women from political rights; was executed for treason in 1793

**Napoleon Bonaparte** (1769-1821)
- **Role**: Military general who became Emperor of France (1804)
- **Key Actions**: Conquered large parts of Europe; introduced the Napoleonic Code (protecting private property, uniform laws); reintroduced slavery in French colonies
- **Significance**: Consolidated some revolutionary reforms (legal equality, merit-based administration) while reversing others (slavery, authoritarian rule)

### 3.4 The Reign of Terror (1793-1794)

**Background**: The newly declared republic faced threats from within and outside. Foreign armies were at France's borders, and internal opposition to the revolution remained strong.

**Causes**:

1. **External Threat**: Monarchies across Europe (Prussia, Austria, Britain, the Netherlands, Spain) formed alliances to invade France and restore Louis XVI's monarchy.
2. **Internal Opposition**: Ex-nobles, clergy, and members of rival political parties opposed the new republic.
3. **Economic Crisis**: The war disrupted food supplies and worsened the economic situation.

**What Happened**: ⭐ Robespierre argued that terror was necessary to protect the republic. He stated: "Terror is nothing but justice, swift, severe and inflexible." The government passed laws placing maximum ceilings on wages and prices. Meat and bread were rationed. Peasants were forced to sell grain to cities at government-fixed prices. All citizens were required to eat "equality bread" made of wholewheat. Traditional titles like Monsieur and Madame were replaced with Citoyen and Citoyenne (Citizen). Churches were shut down and converted into barracks or offices.

Anyone suspected of opposing the republic — nobles, clergy, political rivals, even fellow Jacobins — was arrested, tried by a revolutionary tribunal, and executed by guillotine. The **guillotine** became the symbol of the revolution's most violent phase.

Robespierre pursued his policies so relentlessly that his own supporters turned against him. In July 1794, he was arrested, convicted, and sent to the guillotine the very next day.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: The Reign of Terror ended. An estimated 15,000-40,000 people were executed. The violence exhausted the public and discredited extreme radicalism.
- **Long-term**: The Terror showed both the power and the danger of revolutionary idealism taken to extremes. It demonstrated that the pursuit of liberty could itself become tyrannical — a paradox that political thinkers still debate today.

> **THINK Think About It:** Robespierre said, "To establish democracy, we must first finish the war of liberty against tyranny." Do you think using violence and fear is ever justified to achieve freedom? Where would you draw the line?

### 3.5 Women and the Revolution

**Background**: Women of the Third Estate worked as seamstresses, laundresses, flower-sellers, and domestic servants. They had no access to education beyond convent schools (for the wealthy), earned lower wages than men, and bore the double burden of paid work and household duties.

**Causes of Women's Participation**:

1. **Economic Desperation**: Women managed households while men were away at war or at work. Rising bread prices hit them directly — they spent hours in queues at bakeries.
2. **Political Awakening**: The revolutionary ideals of liberty and equality inspired women to demand the same rights for themselves. They began to ask: if all men are born free and equal, why not women?
3. **Organised Activism**: Women formed their own political clubs and newspapers. About 60 women's clubs were established across France, the most famous being the **Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women**.

**What Happened**: On 5 October 1789, a large crowd of Parisian women marched to Versailles and brought the royal family back to Paris — a dramatic display of women's political power. Throughout the revolution, women participated in protests, debates, and political activities.

**Olympe de Gouges** wrote the **Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen** (1791), addressing it to the Queen and the National Assembly. It declared: "Woman is born free and remains equal to man in rights." She demanded the right to vote, to hold political office, and to equal participation in government.

The revolutionary government introduced some reforms: compulsory schooling for girls, marriage as a civil contract (not a religious one), legal divorce available to both men and women, and the right for women to train for jobs and run businesses.

However, during the Reign of Terror, the government closed women's clubs, banned their political activities, and arrested prominent female activists. Olympe de Gouges herself was executed in 1793. Women in France did not win the right to vote until 1946 — more than 150 years after the revolution.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Limited gains (education, divorce, property rights) but political rights denied. Women's clubs were shut down.
- **Long-term**: The French Revolution planted the seed of women's political activism. The example of women like Olympe de Gouges inspired the international suffrage movement of the 19th and 20th centuries.

> **WORLD In Today's World:** In India, women gained voting rights at independence in 1947 — almost a century after the first countries (New Zealand, 1893) granted women's suffrage. The principle that "all citizens are equal before the law," first proclaimed in France in 1789, is now enshrined in Article 14 of the Indian Constitution.

### 3.6 The Abolition of Slavery

**Background**: French colonies in the Caribbean — Martinique, Guadeloupe, and San Domingo (modern Haiti) — were highly profitable. They supplied sugar, coffee, indigo, and tobacco to European markets. But European workers were unwilling to labour on plantations in distant, tropical lands, so a brutal system of African slavery was used.

**Causes**:

1. **Economic Dependence**: French port cities like Bordeaux and Nantes owed their prosperity to the slave trade. Merchants bought enslaved Africans from local chieftains, shipped them across the Atlantic in horrific conditions, and sold them to plantation owners.
2. **Hypocrisy of "Rights of Man"**: The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen proclaimed that "men are born free and equal," but the National Assembly refused to extend these rights to enslaved people in the colonies, fearing opposition from wealthy slave-trading businessmen.
3. **Revolt in San Domingo**: ⭐ Enslaved people in San Domingo, led by **Toussaint L'Ouverture**, launched a major revolution (1791-1804) that eventually created the independent republic of Haiti — the first Black republic in the world.

**What Happened**: The **Convention** (the republican government) passed a law in 1794 abolishing slavery in all French overseas possessions. This was one of the most radical social reforms of the Jacobin regime. However, it was short-lived: Napoleon reintroduced slavery in 1802. Slavery was permanently abolished in French colonies only in **1848**.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Thousands of enslaved people in French colonies were freed in 1794, though many were re-enslaved under Napoleon.
- **Long-term**: The Haitian Revolution (1791-1804), inspired by the French Revolution, became a powerful symbol of anti-colonial resistance. It demonstrated that the ideals of liberty could not be limited by race.

△ The triangular slave trade operated between Europe (manufactured goods), Africa (enslaved people), and the Americas (raw materials like sugar and coffee) — a cycle of exploitation that enriched Europe at the cost of millions of African lives.

> **THINK Think About It:** The Declaration of the Rights of Man said all humans are born free and equal, yet slavery continued for decades. Why do you think it is so difficult for societies to extend rights to everyone at once?

### 3.7 The Directory and Napoleon's Rise (1795-1804)

**Background**: After the fall of Robespierre in July 1794, a power vacuum existed in France. The Jacobins were discredited, and a moderate government needed to be formed.

**Causes of the Directory's Failure**:

1. **Limited Suffrage**: The new constitution (1795) denied voting rights to non-propertied sections, reversing the universal male suffrage of the republic.
2. **Political Instability**: The Directory — a five-member executive — frequently clashed with the legislative councils. Corruption and inefficiency were rampant.
3. **External Wars**: France was still at war with several European coalitions, draining resources and creating demand for a strong military leader.

**What Happened**: ⭐ In 1799, **Napoleon Bonaparte**, a brilliant military general who had won victories across Europe, overthrew the Directory in a coup and seized power. In 1804, he crowned himself Emperor of France.

Napoleon introduced many reforms that carried revolutionary ideas forward: a uniform legal code (the **Napoleonic Code**) that protected private property, established equality before the law, and abolished feudal privileges. He introduced the decimal system of weights and measures. However, he also reintroduced slavery, restricted press freedom, and censored political opposition. He conquered much of Europe, and many people initially saw him as a liberator bringing freedom from feudalism. But his armies eventually came to be seen as an invading force. Napoleon was finally defeated at the **Battle of Waterloo** in 1815.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: France reverted from a republic to an empire under Napoleon. Democratic institutions were weakened, but many legal and administrative reforms survived.
- **Long-term**: Napoleon's conquests spread revolutionary ideas — legal equality, merit-based administration, abolition of feudalism — across Europe. Even after his defeat, these ideas could not be undone. The French Revolution's legacy of liberty, equality, and fraternity became the foundation of modern democracy.

| Phase | Period | Government | Key Feature |
|-------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Old Regime | Before 1789 | Absolute Monarchy | King had unlimited power; estates system |
| Constitutional Monarchy | 1791-1792 | National Assembly + King | Limited monarchy; only wealthy men could vote |
| First Republic | 1792-1795 | Convention | Republic declared; universal male suffrage; Reign of Terror |
| The Directory | 1795-1799 | Five-member executive | Voting restricted to property owners; instability |
| Napoleonic Empire | 1804-1815 | Napoleon as Emperor | Legal reforms; authoritarian rule; conquests |

## Section 5: Concept Connections

### Within History
- **Socialism in Europe and the Russian Revolution** (Chapter 2) — The French Revolution showed that ordinary people could overthrow a monarchy. Later revolutionaries in Russia (1917) explicitly drew on the French example: they toppled the Tsar, formed a republic, and faced similar challenges of civil war and foreign intervention.
- **Nazism and the Rise of Hitler** (Chapter 3) — Napoleon's rise to power through military force and popular support parallels Hitler's rise. Both consolidated power by claiming to restore national greatness while suppressing opposition. Both ultimately overreached through foreign conquest.
- **Forest Society and Colonialism** (Chapter 4) — The French Revolution's anti-slavery movement connects to broader colonial exploitation patterns. The triangular slave trade that enriched French ports is part of the larger story of how European colonialism reshaped societies in Africa, Asia, and the Americas.

### Across Subjects
- **Political Science — Democratic Rights** — The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) is the direct ancestor of the Fundamental Rights guaranteed in Part III of the Indian Constitution (1950). Both documents proclaim the right to life, liberty, equality, and freedom of speech as inalienable.
- **Political Science — Constitutional Design** — The Constitution of 1791, with its separation of powers (inspired by Montesquieu), was a direct model for later constitutions worldwide, including the principle of checks and balances in the Indian Constitution.
- **English — The story of freedom struggles** — The revolutionary slogans of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" influenced literature and political writing across the world, from Indian nationalist newspapers to anti-colonial movements in Africa and Asia.

### In the Real World
- **The Arab Spring (2010-2012)**: Popular uprisings in Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, and Syria against authoritarian rulers echoed the French Revolution's pattern — economic hardship, political repression, and food prices acting as triggers. In each case, people power forced out long-standing rulers.
- **India's Independence Movement**: Leaders like Raja Rammohan Roy were directly inspired by the French Revolution. Roy was so excited by the July Revolution of 1830 in France that he insisted on visiting French warships at Cape Town. The revolution's ideals of liberty and equality influenced the Indian National Congress and the framing of the Indian Constitution.
- **Modern Protests and Democratic Movements**: From the Hong Kong pro-democracy protests to the women's marches worldwide, the idea that ordinary citizens have the right to challenge unjust authority traces its intellectual lineage to the French Revolution.

## Section 7: Exam-Oriented Summary

### Top 8 Things to Remember

1. ⭐ **French society was divided into three estates** — Clergy, Nobility, and everyone else. Only the Third Estate (97.5% of the population) paid taxes, while the privileged estates enjoyed exemptions and feudal dues.
2. ⭐ **The Estates General deadlock triggered the revolution** — The Third Estate demanded individual voting (one person, one vote) at the Estates General (5 May 1789). When the king refused, they declared themselves the National Assembly (Tennis Court Oath, 20 June 1789).
3. ⭐ **The storming of the Bastille (14 July 1789)** symbolised the people's victory over royal despotism. Though only 7 prisoners were freed, the event marked the collapse of royal authority in Paris.
4. ⭐ **The Revolution moved from moderate to radical** — Constitutional Monarchy (1791) → Republic (1792) → Reign of Terror (1793-94). Each stage saw greater participation by common people but also greater violence.
5. ⭐ **The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen** proclaimed that all humans are born free and equal, with rights to liberty, property, security, and resistance to oppression. These ideas influenced constitutions worldwide, including India's.
6. **The Reign of Terror under Robespierre** used the guillotine to execute suspected enemies of the republic. Robespierre himself was guillotined in July 1794 when his own supporters turned against him.
7. **Women played a significant but insufficiently recognised role** — they marched to Versailles, formed political clubs, and demanded equal rights. Olympe de Gouges wrote a Declaration of Women's Rights but was executed. Women did not get the vote in France until 1946.
8. **Napoleon preserved some revolutionary reforms** (legal equality, merit-based administration, abolition of feudalism) but reversed others (slavery, press freedom, democracy). He was finally defeated at Waterloo in 1815, but the ideas of the Revolution had already spread across Europe.

### Model Answers

#### 1-Mark Questions

**Q:** Who proposed the idea of separation of powers?
**A:** Montesquieu, in his book *The Spirit of the Laws*, proposed the division of government into three branches — legislature, executive, and judiciary.

**Q:** What was the "Great Fear"?
**A:** The Great Fear was a wave of panic that swept through the French countryside in July-August 1789, when peasants attacked chateaux, burned feudal records, and looted grain stores fearing attacks by brigands hired by nobles.

**Q:** What does "sans-culottes" mean?
**A:** Sans-culottes literally means "those without knee breeches." It referred to Jacobin supporters — shopkeepers, artisans, and workers — who wore long striped trousers to reject the knee breeches worn by nobles.

#### 3-Mark Questions

**Q:** Describe the causes of the subsistence crisis in France during the Old Regime.
**A:**
The subsistence crisis in France was caused by the following factors:
1. The population of France rose from 23 million (1715) to 28 million (1789), which sharply increased the demand for foodgrains.
2. Production of grains could not keep pace with this rising demand, leading to a rapid increase in the price of bread — the staple diet of the majority.
3. Wages of workers did not increase with rising prices, widening the gap between the poor and the rich. Bad harvests in 1788 worsened the crisis, triggering food riots.

**Q:** What were the results of the French Revolution for the people of France?
**A:**
The French Revolution brought several significant changes:
1. The feudal system was abolished on 4 August 1789 — tithes and feudal dues were ended, and Church lands were confiscated.
2. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen established fundamental rights such as equality before law, freedom of speech, and the right to life as natural and inalienable.
3. Power shifted from the monarchy and privileged estates to elected bodies. The Constitution of 1791 made France a constitutional monarchy, and by 1792 it became a republic with universal male suffrage.

#### 5-Mark Questions

**Q:** Explain the role of women in the French Revolution.
**A:**
Women played a crucial but often overlooked role in the French Revolution:
1. Women from the Third Estate participated actively in revolutionary events. On 5 October 1789, Parisian women marched to Versailles and brought the royal family back to Paris, demonstrating women's collective political power.
2. Women formed their own political clubs and newspapers. About 60 women's clubs were established across France, the most famous being the Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women. They demanded equal political rights, including the right to vote and hold office.
3. **Olympe de Gouges** wrote the "Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen" (1791), directly challenging the male-only Declaration of Rights. She demanded that women be granted all the same rights as men — liberty, property, security, and political participation.
4. The revolutionary government introduced important reforms for women: compulsory schooling for girls, marriage as a civil contract, legal divorce available to both sexes, and the right to train for jobs.
5. However, women's gains were limited and later reversed. During the Reign of Terror, women's clubs were shut down and political activities banned. Olympe de Gouges was executed in 1793. Women in France did not gain the right to vote until 1946 — showing that the revolution's promise of equality remained incomplete.

**Q:** How did the French Revolution affect the rest of Europe and the world?
**A:**
The French Revolution had a profound and lasting impact beyond France's borders:
1. **Spread of Revolutionary Ideas**: The slogans of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" and the principles of the Declaration of the Rights of Man inspired political movements across Europe. Feudal systems were gradually abolished in many European countries over the 19th century.
2. **Napoleonic Wars and Legal Reforms**: Napoleon's conquests spread revolutionary legal reforms — the Napoleonic Code established equality before law, protected private property, and abolished feudal privileges across much of Europe.
3. **Inspiration for Colonised Peoples**: Leaders in colonies and subject nations were inspired by the revolution. Raja Rammohan Roy in India and Toussaint L'Ouverture in Haiti drew directly on French revolutionary ideas to fight for their own freedom.
4. **Abolition of Slavery**: The Convention's decision to abolish slavery in 1794, though reversed by Napoleon, set a precedent. The Haitian Revolution (1791-1804) created the first Black republic, showing that the ideals of liberty could not be confined by race.
5. **Long-term Democratic Legacy**: The revolution demonstrated that ordinary people could overthrow an unjust system. This idea — that sovereignty resides in the people, not in a monarch — became the foundation of modern democracy worldwide.

### 2-Mark Questions

**Q4.** Differentiate between "active citizens" and "passive citizens" under the Constitution of 1791.
**A:**
1. **Active Citizens**: Men above 25 years who paid taxes equal to at least 3 days of a labourer's wage; they had the right to vote. To qualify as an elector or Assembly member, one had to belong to the highest bracket of taxpayers.
2. **Passive Citizens**: All remaining men and all women — regardless of their contribution to society — were denied the right to vote under the Constitution of 1791.

**Q5.** What was the triangular slave trade? Name any two French colonies in the Caribbean.
**A:**
1. The triangular slave trade was a cycle of trade between Europe, Africa, and the Americas: European merchants sailed from ports like Bordeaux to Africa, bought enslaved people, shipped them to the Caribbean to work on plantations, and then carried sugar, coffee, and tobacco back to Europe.
2. Two French colonies in the Caribbean were **Martinique** and **Guadeloupe** (San Domingo, modern Haiti, is also correct).

**Q6.** Why did the National Assembly abolish feudal privileges on 4 August 1789?
**A:**
1. The National Assembly abolished feudal privileges in response to the widespread peasant revolts across the countryside (the Great Fear), where peasants attacked chateaux and burned records of feudal dues.
2. The abolition ended the feudal system of obligations and taxes, including tithes, and led to the confiscation of Church lands worth at least 2 billion livres, which helped fill the empty state treasury.

### 3-Mark Questions

**Q7.** How did the political clubs contribute to the French Revolution? ⭐
**A:**
Political clubs were essential in mobilising public opinion and organising revolutionary action:
1. The **Jacobin Club** was the most influential, with members from poorer sections of society. They demanded universal male suffrage, price controls, and an end to monarchy. Their leader, Robespierre, led the most radical phase of the revolution.
2. Political clubs provided a platform for debating government policies and planning collective action. They spread revolutionary ideas among people who could not read through oral discussions.
3. Women formed approximately 60 clubs, including the Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women. These clubs allowed women to voice their demands for equal rights, education, and political participation, even though their gains were later reversed.

**Q8.** Explain the significance of the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
**A:**
The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) was a foundational document of the revolution:
1. It proclaimed that "men are born and remain free and equal in rights" and established rights to life, liberty, freedom of speech and opinion, equality before law, and resistance to oppression as natural and inalienable.
2. It declared that sovereignty resides in the nation, not in the king — meaning all political authority comes from the people, a revolutionary idea that challenged the divine right of monarchs.
3. The Declaration influenced democratic movements worldwide. Its principles can be seen in the American Bill of Rights, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), and the Fundamental Rights in the Indian Constitution (1950).

**Q9.** What was the Reign of Terror? Why did it end?
**A:**
The Reign of Terror (1793-94) was the period when Robespierre's government used extreme violence to suppress opposition:
1. Robespierre argued that "terror is nothing but justice, swift, severe and inflexible." Anyone suspected of being an enemy of the republic — ex-nobles, clergy, political rivals, even fellow Jacobins — was arrested and guillotined by revolutionary tribunals.
2. The government imposed strict controls: maximum price ceilings on goods, rationing of bread and meat, forced grain sales to cities, closure of churches, and replacement of traditional titles with "Citoyen/Citoyenne."
3. The Terror ended in July 1794 when Robespierre's own supporters turned against him. He was convicted by a court, arrested, and executed the next day. His relentless pursuit of enemies — real and imagined — had alienated even those who initially supported his vision.

### 5-Mark Questions

**Q10.** "The French Revolution was both a success and a failure." Do you agree? Explain with examples. ⭐
**A:**
I agree that the French Revolution was both a success and a failure, depending on which outcomes we examine:
1. **Success — Abolition of feudalism**: The revolution ended the feudal system, eliminated tithes and feudal dues, confiscated Church lands, and dismantled the privileges of the clergy and nobility. The society of estates was replaced by the principle of equality before law.
2. **Success — Spread of democratic ideas**: The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen established fundamental human rights as inalienable. These ideas spread across Europe and the world, inspiring democratic movements for the next two centuries. The revolution proved that ordinary people could overthrow an unjust system.
3. **Success — Legal and administrative reforms**: Even after Napoleon's fall, reforms like the Napoleonic Code (equality before law, protection of private property), the metric system, and merit-based administration remained in place across much of Europe.
4. **Failure — Violence and loss of life**: The Reign of Terror saw the execution of thousands of people — many of them innocent — by the guillotine. The revolution consumed its own leaders: the king was executed, and so was Robespierre. The violence discredited the revolution's ideals of liberty and justice.
5. **Failure — Incomplete equality**: Women were denied political rights and the vote. Slavery was abolished in 1794 but reintroduced by Napoleon in 1802 and not permanently ended until 1848. The Constitution of 1791 restricted voting to wealthy men, excluding the poor majority. The revolution's promise of equality was limited to a narrow definition of "citizen."

**Q11.** Describe the role of Enlightenment philosophers in shaping the French Revolution.
**A:**
The Enlightenment philosophers provided the intellectual foundation for the French Revolution:
1. **John Locke** (1632-1704), in his *Two Treatises of Government*, rejected the doctrine of the divine right of kings. He argued that no person is born with the natural right to rule over others and that governments exist to protect the natural rights of life, liberty, and property. This idea legitimised challenging the monarchy.
2. **Jean Jacques Rousseau** (1712-1778), in *The Social Contract*, proposed that government should be based on a contract between the people and their representatives. He argued that sovereignty resides in the people, not in a monarch. This directly inspired the Third Estate's demand for individual voting and their declaration of the National Assembly.
3. **Montesquieu** (1689-1755), in *The Spirit of the Laws*, proposed the division of governmental power into three branches — legislature, executive, and judiciary — to prevent any one person or institution from becoming tyrannical. This principle was directly implemented in the Constitution of 1791.
4. These ideas were discussed in salons and coffee-houses and spread through books and newspapers. They were read aloud to those who could not read, ensuring that the ideas reached a wide audience beyond the educated elite.
5. The American Revolution (1776), which was itself inspired by Enlightenment ideas, served as a practical example. France had helped the American colonies gain independence, and the American Constitution — with its guarantee of individual rights — became a model for French political thinkers.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills)

**Q12.** The French Revolution proclaimed "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity," yet it denied rights to women and enslaved people. Was the revolution hypocritical, or was it a necessary first step? Justify your answer.
**A:**
The revolution was not entirely hypocritical — it was a product of its time that achieved significant but incomplete progress. Several arguments support this view:
1. The revolution achieved what was considered radical for its era. No government in the world in 1789 extended equal rights to all — not even in Britain or America. The very idea that sovereignty belongs to the people (not the king) was revolutionary. To judge the revolution solely by modern standards is to ignore how transformative it was for its time.
2. The ideas the revolution unleashed could not be contained. Once the principle of "all men are born free and equal" was proclaimed, it became logically impossible to deny rights to women and enslaved people without contradicting the revolution's own foundation. Olympe de Gouges, Toussaint L'Ouverture, and others used the revolution's own language to demand inclusion.
3. However, the revolution did reveal a genuine contradiction. The leaders of the revolution — educated, propertied men — defined "citizen" narrowly to protect their own interests. When women demanded inclusion, they were silenced. When enslaved people in the colonies fought for freedom, Napoleon reversed their emancipation.
4. In conclusion, the French Revolution was a necessary first step — it created the intellectual framework (rights, equality, popular sovereignty) that later movements used to demand fuller inclusion. It was not the final achievement of equality, but the beginning of the journey. Without the revolution's proclamation of universal rights, the later movements for women's suffrage, abolition of slavery, and decolonisation would have lacked their most powerful philosophical foundation.
