Imagine you could zoom into your own body a million times. You would not see bones, blood, or skin anymore — you would see tiny, living building blocks called cells. ## 3. Core Content

### 3.1 Discovery of the Cell and Cell Theory

In 1665, an English scientist named **Robert Hooke** examined a thin slice of cork under a self-designed microscope. He saw small, box-like compartments that reminded him of the small rooms (called "cells") in a monastery. He named them **cells** — from the Latin word for "a little room." Though Hooke was looking at dead cell walls of cork, this was the first time anyone had observed that living things consist of separate units.

Later discoveries built on this:
- **Leeuwenhoek (1674)** — Using an improved microscope, he was the first to observe **free-living cells** (like bacteria) in pond water.
- **Robert Brown (1831)** — Discovered the **nucleus** inside cells.
- **Purkinje (1839)** — Coined the term **"protoplasm"** for the living fluid substance of the cell.
- **Schleiden (1838) and Schwann (1839)** — Proposed the **cell theory**: all plants and animals are composed of cells, and the cell is the basic unit of life.
- **Virchow (1855)** — Extended the theory by stating that **all cells arise from pre-existing cells** ("Omnis cellula e cellula").

With the invention of the **electron microscope** in 1940, scientists could study the ultrastructure of cells in detail, revealing organelles that were invisible under light microscopes.

> **THINK Think About It:** Before the microscope was invented, people believed that living things were made of a single, continuous substance. How might the discovery that life is made of tiny, individual units have changed the way scientists thought about disease?

### 3.2 Unicellular vs Multicellular Organisms

Organisms can be classified based on the number of cells they contain.

**Unicellular organisms** consist of a single cell that performs all life functions — nutrition, respiration, excretion, and reproduction. Examples include **Amoeba**, **Chlamydomonas**, **Paramoecium**, and **bacteria**. Despite being just one cell, these organisms are fully alive and capable of independent survival.

**Multicellular organisms** consist of many cells that group together and specialise to perform different functions. In humans, for instance, muscle cells contract, nerve cells transmit signals, and blood cells carry oxygen. Each multicellular organism begins life as a single cell (the zygote), which divides repeatedly to form the complete body.

> **FACT Did You Know?** The largest single cell in the world is an ostrich egg — it can be up to 15 cm in diameter. On the other end, bacteria are among the smallest cells, typically just 1-10 micrometres long.

The shape and size of cells are related to their function. Amoeba has no fixed shape — it keeps changing. Nerve cells are long and branched to transmit signals over distances. Red blood cells are disc-shaped to maximise surface area for gas exchange.

> **THINK Think About It:** Every multicellular organism (including you) started as a single cell. If all your cells came from one original cell, why don't all your cells look and behave the same?

### 3.3 Cell Organisation: Plasma Membrane, Cell Wall, and Nucleus

If you examine almost any cell under a microscope, you will encounter three basic features: **plasma membrane**, **nucleus**, and **cytoplasm**.

#### 3.3.1 Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane)

⭐ The **plasma membrane** is the outermost covering of the cell. It separates the cell's contents from the external environment. It is **flexible** and made up of **lipids and proteins**.

Its most important property is **selective permeability** — it allows some substances to pass through while blocking others. This control over what enters and exits the cell is essential for maintaining the cell's internal conditions.

**Diffusion** is the movement of particles from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration. Gases like CO₂ (waste) and O₂ (needed for respiration) move in and out of cells by diffusion. No energy is required — it is a passive process.

**Osmosis** is a special type of diffusion — the movement of **water molecules** through a selectively permeable membrane. Water moves from a region where its concentration is high (low solute) to a region where its concentration is low (high solute).

**Three types of solutions based on water concentration:**

| Solution Type | Water Concentration vs Cell | Effect on Cell |
|--------------|----------------------------|----------------|
| **Hypotonic** | Higher outside | Cell **gains water** → swells |
| **Isotonic** | Same inside and outside | **No net movement** → cell stays same size |
| **Hypertonic** | Lower outside | Cell **loses water** → shrinks |

> **Experiment: De-shelled Egg in Different Solutions**
>
> **Aim**: To observe osmosis in a living cell.
> **Materials**: Eggs, dilute hydrochloric acid, pure water, concentrated salt solution.
> **Procedure**:
> 1. Dissolve the shell of an egg in dilute HCl (the shell is calcium carbonate).
> 2. Place the de-shelled egg in pure water. Observe after 5 minutes.
> 3. Place a similar de-shelled egg in concentrated salt solution. Observe after 5 minutes.
> **Observation**: The egg in pure water **swells** (water enters by osmosis). The egg in salt solution **shrinks** (water leaves by osmosis).
> **Conclusion**: Water moves from hypotonic (pure water) into the egg and from the egg into hypertonic (salt solution).

The plasma membrane can also actively engulf food particles from outside — a process called **endocytosis**. Amoeba acquires its food this way. Endocytosis requires energy, unlike diffusion and osmosis.

> **Watch Out:** Students often say "diffusion and osmosis are the same thing." They are not. Diffusion applies to any substance (solid, liquid, gas) moving from high to low concentration. Osmosis is specifically the movement of water through a semi-permeable membrane. Every osmosis is diffusion, but not every diffusion is osmosis.

> **THINK Think About It:** When you sprinkle salt on a raw cucumber slice, water droplets appear on the surface. Can you explain why?

#### 3.3.2 Cell Wall

Plant cells have an additional rigid outer covering called the **cell wall**, located outside the plasma membrane. It is mainly composed of **cellulose**, a complex carbohydrate that provides structural strength. The cell wall is **non-living** and **fully permeable** — it allows all substances to pass through.

The cell wall allows plant cells, fungi, and bacteria to withstand very dilute (hypotonic) external media without bursting. When a plant cell takes up water, it swells and pushes against the rigid wall. The wall pushes back with equal pressure, preventing the cell from bursting. This pressure is called **turgor pressure**.

When a living plant cell is placed in a **hypertonic solution**, water moves out by osmosis and the cell contents shrink away from the cell wall. This phenomenon is called **plasmolysis**. Importantly, plasmolysis occurs only in **living cells** — dead cells cannot perform osmosis.

> **FACT Did You Know?** Cellulose, the main component of the plant cell wall, is the most abundant organic polymer on Earth. Paper, cotton, and some fabrics are all made primarily of cellulose.

> **Watch Out:** "Cell wall" is present in plant cells, NOT in animal cells — but BOTH have a cell membrane. Students often confuse these two terms. The cell wall is outside the cell membrane and is dead and rigid; the cell membrane is inside the cell wall and is alive and flexible.

#### 3.3.3 Nucleus

The **nucleus** is a darkly stained, spherical or oval structure located near the centre of the cell. It has a double-layered covering called the **nuclear membrane**, which has **pores** that allow material to pass between the nucleus and the cytoplasm.

Inside the nucleus are **chromosomes** — thread-like structures that carry genetic information in the form of **DNA** (Deoxyribo Nucleic Acid) molecules. Chromosomes are visible as rod-shaped structures only when the cell is about to divide. In a non-dividing cell, the DNA is present as an entangled mass called **chromatin material**.

Functional segments of DNA are called **genes** — they determine specific traits inherited from parents.

The nucleus has two critical roles:
1. It controls cellular reproduction (cell division).
2. It directs the chemical activities of the cell, determining how the cell develops and functions.

> **DIAGRAM: Generalised Eukaryotic Cell**
> Imagine a labelled diagram showing:
> - A rectangular **cell wall** (outermost, thick, in plant cells) — provides rigidity and structural support
> - A thin **cell membrane** just inside the cell wall — selectively permeable, regulates movement of substances
> - A large, central **vacuole** filled with cell sap — provides turgidity, stores nutrients and waste
> - A dark, spherical **nucleus** near the centre with a double membrane and pores — contains DNA and controls cell activities
> - **Chromatin threads** inside the nucleus — carry genetic information
> - Scattered **mitochondria** (oval with folded inner membrane) — produce ATP energy
> - A network of **endoplasmic reticulum** (tube-like structures near the nucleus) — transports materials and synthesises proteins/lipids
> - **Ribosomes** (tiny dots on rough ER and scattered in cytoplasm) — sites of protein synthesis
> - **Golgi apparatus** (stacked flattened sacs) — packages and dispatches cellular products
> - **Lysosomes** (small round sacs) — contain digestive enzymes for waste breakdown
> - **Chloroplasts** (green, disc-shaped, in plant cells only) — contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis
>
> The overall shape is rectangular for a plant cell (due to the rigid cell wall) and irregular/rounded for an animal cell (no cell wall).

#### 3.3.4 Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes

Not all cells have a well-defined nucleus. This fundamental difference divides organisms into two groups:

| Feature | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Size** | Generally small (1-10 um) | Generally large (5-100 um) |
| **Nuclear region** | Poorly defined, no nuclear membrane; called **nucleoid** | Well-defined nucleus with nuclear membrane |
| **Chromosomes** | Single, made of nucleic acid only | Multiple, made of DNA and protein |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | Absent | Present (ER, Golgi, lysosomes, mitochondria, etc.) |
| **Examples** | Bacteria, blue-green algae (cyanobacteria) | Humans, plants, fungi, Amoeba, Paramoecium |

△ The term **prokaryote** comes from "pro" (primitive) and "karyon" (nucleus). Prokaryotes lack a true nucleus and most organelles, but their cytoplasm performs many of the same functions in a less organised way.

> **THINK Think About It:** Bacteria are prokaryotic and lack mitochondria, yet they still produce energy. How do you think they manage this without a "powerhouse"?

### 3.4 Cell Organelles: The "Organs" of the Cell

Just as your body has organs (heart, lungs, stomach) that perform specific functions, a cell has **organelles** — tiny structures that carry out specialised tasks. Each organelle is enclosed by its own membrane, keeping its chemical reactions separate from the rest of the cell.

#### 3.4.1 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The **endoplasmic reticulum (ER)** is a large network of membrane-bound tubes and sheets. It looks like a system of interconnected tubules and vesicles (small sacs). There are two types:

**Rough ER (RER)** — Has **ribosomes** attached to its surface, giving it a rough appearance. Ribosomes are the sites of **protein synthesis**. The proteins manufactured on ribosomes are transported through the ER to various parts of the cell.

**Smooth ER (SER)** — Lacks ribosomes and has a smooth surface. SER helps in the manufacture of **lipids (fats)**, which are important for cell function and for building the cell membrane itself (a process called **membrane biogenesis**). In liver cells of vertebrates, SER plays a crucial role in **detoxifying** poisons and drugs.

Functions of ER:
- Serves as a transport channel for materials (especially proteins) between cytoplasm and nucleus.
- Provides a surface for biochemical activities.
- Plays a role in membrane biogenesis (building new cell membrane).
- SER detoxifies harmful substances in liver cells.

> **DIAGRAM: Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)**
> Imagine a labelled diagram showing:
> - A **nuclear membrane** at the centre with pores — encloses the nucleus
> - **Rough ER** connected to the nuclear membrane — has tiny dots (ribosomes) on its surface, appears bumpy
> - **Smooth ER** extending from the rough ER — smooth, no dots, tube-like
> - **Ribosomes** scattered on the rough ER surface — small, round, the "protein factories"
> - Small **vesicles** budding off from the ER — carrying proteins to the Golgi apparatus

#### 3.4.2 Golgi Apparatus

The **Golgi apparatus** consists of stacks of membrane-bound, flattened sacs called **cisterns**. It was first described by **Camillo Golgi**. These stacks are often connected to the membranes of the ER.

Functions of the Golgi apparatus:
- **Packages** materials synthesised near the ER into vesicles for transport.
- **Modifies** proteins and lipids before dispatching them.
- **Stores** cellular products.
- **Makes complex sugars** from simple sugars.
- Involved in the **formation of lysosomes**.

Think of the Golgi apparatus as the cell's **packaging and dispatch centre** — it receives raw materials from the ER, processes them, and ships them to the correct destination inside or outside the cell.

> **FACT Did You Know?** Camillo Golgi developed a revolutionary staining technique called the "black reaction" using a weak silver nitrate solution. This allowed scientists for the first time to see individual nerve cells clearly under a microscope. He won the Nobel Prize in 1906 for this work.

#### 3.4.3 Lysosomes

⭐ **Lysosomes** are membrane-bound sacs filled with powerful **digestive enzymes**. These enzymes are made by the rough ER. Lysosomes serve as the cell's **waste disposal system**:

- They digest **foreign material** entering the cell (such as bacteria or food particles).
- They break down **worn-out cell organelles**, keeping the cell clean.
- They can break down virtually all organic material — proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids.

If a cell is damaged or its metabolism is disturbed, lysosomes may **burst open** and release their enzymes, which then digest the cell's own contents. For this reason, lysosomes are called **"suicide bags"** of the cell.

> **Watch Out:** Students often say "mitochondria produce energy." While that's partially true, mitochondria don't produce "energy" in a general sense — they specifically produce **ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)** molecules, which are the cell's "energy currency." The cell then spends ATP to do actual work (making proteins, moving muscles, etc.).

#### 3.4.4 Mitochondria

⭐ **Mitochondria** are known as the **"powerhouse of the cell"** because they generate most of the energy the cell needs. They have a unique structure:
- **Two membrane coverings**: the outer membrane is porous; the inner membrane is deeply **folded** into finger-like projections called **cristae**.
- These folds greatly **increase the surface area** for the chemical reactions that produce ATP.
- The energy released by mitochondria is stored in **ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)** molecules — the universal "energy currency" of cells.

A remarkable feature: mitochondria have their **own DNA and ribosomes**, meaning they can make some of their own proteins. This is unusual for an organelle and supports the scientific theory that mitochondria were once free-living bacteria that were absorbed by larger cells billions of years ago.

#### 3.4.5 Plastids

**Plastids** are found **only in plant cells**. There are two main types:

**Chromoplasts** — Coloured plastids. Those that contain the green pigment **chlorophyll** are specifically called **chloroplasts**. Chloroplasts are the site of **photosynthesis** — the process by which plants make their own food using sunlight, CO₂, and water. Chloroplasts also contain yellow and orange pigments.

**Leucoplasts** — White or colourless plastids. They primarily **store** materials such as starch, oils, and protein granules.

Like mitochondria, plastids have their **own DNA and ribosomes**. The internal structure of chloroplasts consists of numerous membrane layers embedded in a material called the **stroma**.

> **DIAGRAM: Chloroplast**
> Imagine a labelled diagram showing:
> - An **outer membrane** and an **inner membrane** — double covering (similar to mitochondria)
> - **Thylakoid membranes** stacked into coin-like piles called **grana** (singular: granum) — the sites where light reactions of photosynthesis occur
> - **Stroma** — the fluid filling the inside of the chloroplast, where the dark reactions of photosynthesis occur
> - **Chlorophyll** molecules embedded in the thylakoid membranes — the green pigment that captures light energy

#### 3.4.6 Vacuoles

**Vacuoles** are storage sacs for solid or liquid contents. Their size varies greatly between cell types:

- **In animal cells**: Vacuoles are small and numerous.
- **In plant cells**: There is usually a single, large **central vacuole** that can occupy **50-90%** of the cell volume.

Functions of vacuoles:
- Store **amino acids, sugars, organic acids**, and some **proteins**.
- In plant cells, they are full of **cell sap** and provide **turgidity and rigidity** to the cell.
- In **Amoeba**, the **food vacuole** stores consumed food items.
- In some unicellular organisms, specialised **contractile vacuoles** expel excess water and waste from the cell.

> **DIAGRAM: Plant Cell vs Animal Cell Comparison**
> Imagine a side-by-side labelled diagram showing:
>
> **Plant Cell** (rectangular):
> - Thick, rigid **cell wall** on the outside
> - **Cell membrane** just inside the cell wall
> - Large, central **vacuole** (pushes cytoplasm to the edges)
> - **Chloroplasts** scattered in the cytoplasm (green disc shapes)
> - **Nucleus** pushed to one side by the large vacuole
> - All other organelles in the thin layer of cytoplasm
>
> **Animal Cell** (irregular/rounded):
> - No cell wall — only a flexible **cell membrane**
> - Small, scattered **vacuoles** (if any)
> - No chloroplasts
> - **Nucleus** typically near the centre
> - Organelles spread throughout the cytoplasm

> **THINK Think About It:** If plant cells have large central vacuoles that push everything to the edges, how do organelles like mitochondria and ribosomes function efficiently in such a cramped space?

### 3.5 Cell Division

⭐ New cells are formed for three reasons: **growth**, **replacement of old/dead cells**, and **reproduction**. The process of forming new cells is called **cell division**. There are two main types:

#### 3.5.1 Mitosis

**Mitosis** is the process by which most body cells divide for **growth and repair**. In mitosis:
- One **mother cell** divides to form **two identical daughter cells**.
- The daughter cells have the **same number of chromosomes** as the mother cell.
- This ensures that new cells are genetically identical to the original.

**Process of Mitosis:**

**What it is**: Cell division that produces two identical daughter cells with the same chromosome number as the mother cell.
**Why it happens**: To enable organisms to grow, repair damaged tissues, and replace dead cells.

**Steps**:
1. **DNA replication**: The cell copies all its DNA so that each new cell gets a complete set → This ensures genetic information is not lost.
2. **Chromosome alignment**: The copied chromosomes line up at the centre of the cell → This ensures equal distribution.
3. **Chromosome separation**: The chromosomes are pulled apart to opposite ends of the cell → Each half now has a complete set.
4. **Cell splitting**: The cell divides into two, with each new cell receiving an identical set of chromosomes → Two identical daughter cells are formed.

**Result**: Two genetically identical daughter cells, each with the same number of chromosomes as the original cell.

Examples: A cut on your skin heals by mitosis. A growing plant extends its roots and shoots by mitosis.

#### 3.5.2 Meiosis

**Meiosis** is a specialised type of cell division that occurs in reproductive organs to produce **gametes** (sperm and egg cells). In meiosis:
- The mother cell divides **twice consecutively**, producing **four daughter cells**.
- Each daughter cell has **half the number of chromosomes** of the mother cell.
- This halving is essential because when sperm and egg fuse during fertilisation, the resulting zygote has the correct (full) chromosome number.

**Why halving is necessary**: If gametes had the full number of chromosomes, fertilisation would double the chromosome count in every generation, which is not sustainable.

> **FACT Did You Know?** Human body cells have 46 chromosomes, but human sperm and egg cells have only 23 each. When they fuse during fertilisation, the resulting zygote has the correct 46 chromosomes — 23 from each parent.

**Comparison of Mitosis and Meiosis:**

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Purpose** | Growth and repair | Production of gametes |
| **Number of divisions** | One | Two consecutive |
| **Daughter cells produced** | Two | Four |
| **Chromosome number** | Same as mother cell | Half of mother cell |
| **Genetic identity** | Identical to mother cell | Different from mother cell and each other |
| **Where it occurs** | Body (somatic) cells | Reproductive organs |

> **THINK Think About It:** Why is it important that the four cells produced by meiosis are genetically different from each other, rather than being identical?

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

| # | Misconception / Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|------------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | "All cells look the same — they're just tiny boxes." | Cells vary enormously in shape, size, and internal structure depending on their function. Nerve cells are long and branched; red blood cells are disc-shaped; muscle cells are spindle-shaped. | Cell shape and size are related to the specific function they perform. A nerve cell is long to transmit signals over distances. |
| 2 | "Plant cells and animal cells are the same thing." | Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole — features absent in animal cells. Animal cells are generally smaller and more variable in shape. | Always use a comparison table to remember the differences: cell wall (plant only), chloroplasts (plant only), large vacuole (plant), centrioles (animal only). |
| 3 | "Mitochondria produce energy/food for the cell." | Mitochondria do not produce "energy" or "food." They break down nutrients to produce **ATP molecules**, which the cell then uses as energy currency to do work. | Mitochondria = power plant that generates ATP. ATP = energy currency the cell spends. Food is broken down in the cytoplasm and mitochondria, not created there. |
| 4 | "Diffusion and osmosis are the same thing." | Diffusion is the general movement of any substance from high to low concentration. Osmosis is specifically the movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane. | Osmosis is a special case of diffusion — it always involves water and always requires a semi-permeable membrane. |
| 5 | "Lysosomes are only for digesting food." | While lysosomes do digest food particles (especially in Amoeba), their broader function is waste disposal — they also break down old organelles and can destroy the entire cell if it is damaged. | Lysosomes = waste disposal AND suicide bags. They clean up foreign material, recycle worn-out organelles, and can trigger cell death in damaged cells. |
| 6 | "The cell wall controls what enters and exits the cell." | The cell wall is fully permeable — it lets everything through. It is the **cell membrane** (inside the wall) that is selectively permeable and controls movement of substances. | Cell wall = rigid support structure (fully permeable). Cell membrane = flexible gatekeeper (selectively permeable). |

## 7. Exam-Oriented Summary

### Top 8 Things to Remember

1. ⭐ **Cell theory** — All living organisms are made of cells; the cell is the basic unit of life; all cells arise from pre-existing cells (Schleiden, Schwann, Virchow).
2. ⭐ **Plasma membrane is selectively permeable** — It controls what enters and exits the cell. Diffusion moves gases; osmosis moves water through a semi-permeable membrane.
3. ⭐ **Plant vs Animal cell differences** — Plant cells have cell wall, chloroplasts, and large central vacuole. Animal cells lack all three.
4. ⭐ **Mitochondria = powerhouse** — Produces ATP (energy currency). Has its own DNA and ribosomes. Double membrane with folded inner membrane (cristae).
5. ⭐ **Lysosomes = suicide bags** — Contain digestive enzymes made by RER. Digest foreign material, worn-out organelles, and can destroy the cell itself.
6. ⭐ **ER and Golgi apparatus** — RER (with ribosomes) makes proteins; SER makes lipids. Golgi apparatus packages, modifies, and dispatches these products.
7. **Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes** — Prokaryotes lack nuclear membrane and membrane-bound organelles (e.g., bacteria). Eukaryotes have both (e.g., humans, plants).
8. **Mitosis vs Meiosis** — Mitosis produces 2 identical cells (growth/repair). Meiosis produces 4 cells with half chromosomes (gametes for reproduction).

### Model Answers

#### 1-Mark Questions

**Q:** Which organelle is known as the powerhouse of the cell?
**A:** Mitochondria. It produces energy in the form of ATP molecules, which are used by the cell for various chemical activities.

**Q:** What is the function of the cell wall in plant cells?
**A:** The cell wall provides structural strength and rigidity to the plant cell. It is made of cellulose and allows the cell to withstand hypotonic media without bursting.

**Q:** Name the process by which an Amoeba takes in food.
**A:** Endocytosis. The flexible plasma membrane engulfs food particles from the external environment.

#### 3-Mark Questions

**Q:** Differentiate between plant cell and animal cell. (Any three differences)
**A:**
Plant cells and animal cells differ in several key features:
1. **Cell wall**: Plant cells have a rigid cell wall made of cellulose outside the cell membrane; animal cells lack a cell wall.
2. **Chloroplasts**: Plant cells contain chloroplasts for photosynthesis; animal cells do not have chloroplasts.
3. **Vacuoles**: Plant cells have a large central vacuole occupying 50-90% of cell volume; animal cells have small, multiple vacuoles or none at all.

**Q:** Describe the structure and function of mitochondria.
**A:**
Mitochondria are double-membrane organelles known as the powerhouse of the cell:
1. **Structure**: They have an outer porous membrane and an inner membrane that is deeply folded into projections called cristae. These folds increase the surface area for chemical reactions.
2. **Function**: Mitochondria produce ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate), which is the energy currency of the cell. The cell uses ATP for making new chemical compounds and for mechanical work.
3. **Unique feature**: Mitochondria have their own DNA and ribosomes, allowing them to make some of their own proteins.

#### 5-Mark Questions

**Q:** Describe the structure of a plant cell with a diagram.
**A:**
A plant cell has several distinct structural features:
1. **Cell wall**: The outermost rigid covering made of cellulose. It provides structural strength and allows the cell to withstand hypotonic media without bursting.
2. **Plasma membrane**: Located inside the cell wall, it is a flexible, selectively permeable membrane made of lipids and proteins that controls the movement of substances in and out of the cell.
3. **Nucleus**: A spherical structure enclosed by a double-layered nuclear membrane with pores. It contains chromosomes (DNA and protein) that carry genetic information, and it controls all cellular activities.
4. **Chloroplasts**: Green, disc-shaped organelles containing chlorophyll. They are the sites of photosynthesis, where plants make food using sunlight, CO₂, and water.
5. **Central vacuole**: A large storage sac filled with cell sap that occupies 50-90% of the cell volume. It stores amino acids, sugars, organic acids, and provides turgidity and rigidity to the cell.
6. Other organelles include mitochondria (powerhouse), ER (transport and synthesis), Golgi apparatus (packaging), ribosomes (protein synthesis), and lysosomes (waste disposal).

**Q:** Explain the processes of diffusion and osmosis. How are they important for cells?
**A:**
Diffusion and osmosis are two passive transport processes essential for cell function:
1. **Diffusion**: It is the spontaneous movement of particles (solids, liquids, or gases) from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration. For example, CO₂ (cellular waste) accumulates inside the cell at high concentration and diffuses out, while O₂ diffuses in when its level inside the cell decreases. No energy is required for diffusion.
2. **Osmosis**: It is the movement of water molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of high water concentration (low solute) to a region of low water concentration (high solute). For example, plant roots absorb water from the soil by osmosis.
3. **Types of solutions**: In a hypotonic solution (higher water concentration outside), a cell gains water and swells. In an isotonic solution, there is no net movement. In a hypertonic solution, the cell loses water and shrinks.
4. **Plasmolysis**: When a plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution, water moves out and the cell contents shrink away from the cell wall.
5. **Importance**: These processes enable gaseous exchange, water absorption, nutrient uptake, and waste removal — all essential for cell survival without expending energy.

### Examiner's Tips for This Chapter

- **Diagram questions are very common**: Be prepared to draw and label either a plant cell, an animal cell, or a comparison of both. Draw large, use pencil, and label all parts clearly.
- **Know your comparisons**: "Differentiate between plant cell and animal cell" and "Differentiate between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell" are frequently asked 3-5 mark questions. Use a table or point format.
- **Use the factory analogy**: If asked about organelle functions, use the analogy — nucleus = management, mitochondria = power plant, ribosomes = assembly line, Golgi = packaging, ER = transport, lysosomes = waste disposal. Examiners appreciate clear, structured answers.
- **State the key definition first**: For 1-mark questions ("What is osmosis?"), start with the definition. Do not add unnecessary explanation.
- **Underline key terms**: In longer answers, underline terms like "selectively permeable," "cellulose," "ATP," "chromosomes," and "prokaryotic/eukaryotic."

## 10. Extra Practice Questions with Answers

### 1-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q1.** What is the energy currency of the cell? ⭐
**A:** ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). It is produced by mitochondria and used by the cell for all energy-requiring activities.

**Q2.** What is a nucleoid?
**A:** A nucleoid is an undefined nuclear region found in prokaryotic cells that contains nucleic acids (DNA) but is not enclosed by a nuclear membrane.

**Q3.** Name two organelles that have their own DNA and ribosomes.
**A:** Mitochondria and plastids (chloroplasts). Both are semi-autonomous organelles capable of making some of their own proteins.

### 2-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q4.** What is plasmolysis? Under what condition does it occur?
**A:**
1. Plasmolysis is the shrinkage of the cell contents (cytoplasm and vacuole) away from the cell wall when a plant cell loses water.
2. It occurs when a living plant cell is placed in a **hypertonic solution** (a solution with lower water concentration than the cell). Water moves out by osmosis, causing the cell contents to contract.

**Q5.** Differentiate between diffusion and osmosis. ⭐
**A:**
1. **Diffusion** is the movement of any substance (solid, liquid, or gas) from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration, without requiring a semi-permeable membrane.
2. **Osmosis** is the specific movement of **water molecules** through a **selectively permeable membrane** from a region of high water concentration to a region of low water concentration.

**Q6.** Why is the Golgi apparatus called the "packaging and dispatch centre" of the cell?
**A:**
1. The Golgi apparatus receives materials (proteins and lipids) synthesised by the ER, modifies them, and packages them into vesicles for transport.
2. It then dispatches these packaged products to their correct destinations — either to other organelles within the cell or to the cell membrane for secretion outside the cell.

### 3-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q7.** Describe the structure and function of the plasma membrane. ⭐
**A:**
The plasma membrane is the outermost flexible covering of the cell that separates its contents from the external environment.
1. **Structure**: It is made up of organic molecules called **lipids and proteins**. Its structure can only be observed through an electron microscope. It is flexible, which allows the cell to change shape and perform endocytosis.
2. **Function — Selective permeability**: The membrane is **selectively permeable**, meaning it allows certain substances to pass through while blocking others. Gases like CO₂ and O₂ move by diffusion; water moves by osmosis; and larger molecules require energy-dependent transport or endocytosis.
3. **Function — Protection**: It protects the cell's internal environment and helps maintain the specific conditions needed for biochemical reactions to occur efficiently.

**Q8.** Explain three reasons why cell division is important for living organisms.
**A:**
Cell division is the process by which new cells are formed, and it is important for three main reasons:
1. **Growth**: Multicellular organisms grow by increasing the number of cells through repeated cell division. A single fertilised egg divides billions of times to form a complete organism.
2. **Repair and replacement**: Old, dead, and injured cells are continuously replaced by new cells formed through division. For example, skin cells are constantly replaced to heal cuts and wounds.
3. **Reproduction**: In reproductive organs, specialised cell division (meiosis) produces gametes (sperm and egg cells) that are essential for sexual reproduction and maintaining chromosome number across generations.

**Q9.** Compare prokaryotic cells and eukaryotic cells on the basis of: (i) nucleus, (ii) membrane-bound organelles, and (iii) size.
**A:**
1. **Nucleus**: Prokaryotic cells have a poorly defined nuclear region called the **nucleoid**, which lacks a nuclear membrane. Eukaryotic cells have a well-defined nucleus enclosed by a **double-layered nuclear membrane** with pores.
2. **Membrane-bound organelles**: Prokaryotic cells **lack** membrane-bound organelles such as ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, and mitochondria. Eukaryotic cells **possess** all of these organelles, each enclosed by its own membrane.
3. **Size**: Prokaryotic cells are generally **small** (1-10 micrometres), while eukaryotic cells are generally **large** (5-100 micrometres).

### 5-Mark Questions (2 questions)

**Q10.** Describe the various cell organelles found in a eukaryotic cell and their functions. ⭐
**A:**
Eukaryotic cells contain several membrane-bound organelles, each performing a specialised function:
1. **Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)**: A network of membrane-bound tubes and sheets. **Rough ER** (with ribosomes) is the site of **protein synthesis**. **Smooth ER** synthesises **lipids** and helps in detoxifying poisons and drugs (especially in liver cells). ER also serves as a transport channel for materials within the cell.
2. **Golgi Apparatus**: Consists of stacked membrane-bound sacs (cisterns). It **packages, modifies, and dispatches** proteins and lipids produced by the ER to their destinations inside or outside the cell. It is also involved in forming lysosomes.
3. **Lysosomes**: Membrane-bound sacs filled with powerful **digestive enzymes** made by the RER. They digest foreign material (bacteria, food particles) and worn-out cell organelles, keeping the cell clean. If the cell is damaged, lysosomes can burst and digest the entire cell — hence they are called **"suicide bags."**
4. **Mitochondria**: Double-membrane organelles called the **"powerhouse of the cell."** The inner membrane is deeply folded (cristae) to increase surface area. Mitochondria produce **ATP (energy currency)** through chemical reactions. They have their own DNA and ribosomes.
5. **Plastids** (plant cells only): **Chloroplasts** (a type of chromoplast) contain chlorophyll and are the sites of **photosynthesis**. **Leucoplasts** store starch, oils, and proteins. Like mitochondria, plastids have their own DNA and ribosomes.
6. **Vacuoles**: Storage sacs — small in animal cells, large and central in plant cells. They store nutrients, provide **turgidity** to plant cells, and in unicellular organisms like Amoeba, the food vacuole stores ingested food.

**Q11.** (a) What is osmosis? (b) Describe what happens when a plant cell is placed in (i) a hypotonic solution, and (ii) a hypertonic solution. (c) Give one daily-life application of osmosis.
**A:**
(a) **Osmosis** is the net movement of water molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of high water concentration to a region of low water concentration.

(b)
1. **Hypotonic solution**: When a plant cell is placed in a hypotonic solution (higher water concentration outside), water enters the cell by osmosis. The cell swells but does **not burst** because the rigid **cell wall** exerts pressure against the swollen cell. This pressure is called **turgor pressure**, and it keeps the plant upright and firm.
2. **Hypertonic solution**: When a plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution (lower water concentration outside), water moves out of the cell by osmosis. The cell contents shrink away from the cell wall in a phenomenon called **plasmolysis**. The plant wilts and loses its rigidity.

(c) **Daily-life application**: Sprinkling salt on raw cucumber slices causes water to be drawn out of the cucumber cells by osmosis (since the salt creates a hypertonic environment). This is why water droplets appear on the surface of salted vegetables. Similarly, **food preservation** using salt or sugar works on the same principle — microbes in hypertonic conditions lose water and die.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills) (1 question)

**Q12.** If a scientist discovers a new unicellular organism and observes that it has a cell wall but no nucleus, no mitochondria, and its genetic material is a single circular chromosome floating freely in the cytoplasm, how should this organism be classified? Justify your answer with at least three reasons.
**A:**
This organism should be classified as a **prokaryote**.

1. **Absence of a nuclear membrane**: The genetic material is not enclosed in a well-defined nucleus but floats freely in the cytoplasm as a nucleoid. This is the defining feature of prokaryotic cells — they have a poorly defined nuclear region without a nuclear membrane.
2. **Absence of membrane-bound organelles**: The organism lacks mitochondria and other membrane-bound organelles, which is characteristic of prokaryotes. Eukaryotic cells always possess membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria, ER, and Golgi apparatus.
3. **Single circular chromosome**: Prokaryotic cells typically contain a single chromosome made of only nucleic acid (DNA), unlike eukaryotic cells which have multiple chromosomes composed of both DNA and protein.

The presence of a cell wall does not contradict this classification, as many prokaryotes (bacteria) and also eukaryotes (plants, fungi) have cell walls. It is the absence of a nuclear membrane and membrane-bound organelles that is decisive.

### Case-Based / Source-Based Questions (1 question, 4 marks)

**Q13.** A biology student performs an experiment with three potato cups (hollowed-out potato halves). Cup A is kept empty, Cup B contains a teaspoon of sugar, and Cup C contains a teaspoon of salt. All three cups are placed in a trough containing water and left for two hours. After two hours, the student observes that water has gathered in the hollowed portion of Cups B and C, but not in Cup A. When a fourth cup (Cup D, made from a boiled potato) is prepared with sugar and placed in water, no water gathers in it.

**13a.** Name the process responsible for water entering Cups B and C. (1 mark)
**13b.** Why did water NOT gather in Cup A? (1 mark)
**13c.** Explain why no water gathered in Cup D (the boiled potato cup). What does this tell us about the conditions required for this process? (2 marks)

**A13a:** The process is **osmosis** — the movement of water molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of high water concentration (the trough water) to a region of low water concentration (the sugar/salt solution inside the cup).

**A13b:** Water did not gather in Cup A because it was empty. The concentration of water inside Cup A was the same as in the trough (both were pure water), so there was no difference in water concentration to drive osmosis. Cup A serves as the **control** for the experiment.

**A13c:**
1. No water gathered in Cup D because the potato was **boiled**, which killed the cells and destroyed their selectively permeable membranes. Without a selectively permeable membrane, osmosis cannot occur.
2. This tells us that osmosis requires a **living, intact cell membrane** that is selectively permeable. Dead cells cannot perform osmosis, which is why only Cups B and C (with living potato cells) showed water movement. This principle is also seen in plasmolysis experiments with Rhoeo leaves, where only living cells shrink away from the cell wall in hypertonic solutions.
