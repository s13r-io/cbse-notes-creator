## Section 1: Chapter Introduction

### What This Chapter Is About

Every living thing — a tree, a bacterium, a whale, or you — is made of **cells**. The cell is the basic unit of life. This chapter answers three big questions: What is a cell? What is it made of? How does it work?

This story begins in 1665 when an English scientist named **Robert Hooke** looked at a thin slice of cork through a microscope he had built himself. He saw tiny box-like compartments that reminded him of small rooms — he called them **cells** (from the Latin word *cella*, meaning "a little room"). He had no idea he was looking at the walls of dead plant cells, but the name stuck.

### Why This Chapter Matters

Understanding the cell is like understanding the atom in chemistry — once you know this foundational unit, everything else in biology makes sense. The cell explains:
- Why you grow (cells divide)
- Why cuts heal (new cells replace damaged ones)
- How medicines work (they target specific cell processes)
- Why plants are green (chloroplasts in plant cells)

## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 Discovery of the Cell and Cell Theory

#### Robert Hooke (1665) — The First Glimpse

In 1665, **Robert Hooke** examined a thin slice of cork under his self-designed microscope. He saw a structure resembling a honeycomb — many small box-like compartments. He called these boxes **cells**, from the Latin word for "little room." What he was actually seeing were the dead walls of plant cells (cork comes from bark), not the living contents. But the word "cell" entered biology forever.

#### The Timeline of Discovery

| Year | Scientist | Discovery |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| 1665 | Robert Hooke | Observed cells in cork using primitive microscope |
| 1674 | Antonie van Leeuwenhoek | Discovered free-living cells in pond water using improved microscope |
| 1831 | Robert Brown | Discovered the nucleus in the cell |
| 1839 | Purkinje | Coined the term "protoplasm" for the fluid substance of the cell |
| 1838 | Schleiden | Proposed that all plants are made of cells |
| 1839 | Schwann | Proposed that all animals are made of cells |
| 1855 | Virchow | Extended cell theory: all cells arise from pre-existing cells |
| 1940 | — | Electron microscope invented, revealing organelle details |

#### Cell Theory (Complete Version)

The cell theory was developed by **Schleiden (1838)** and **Schwann (1839)**, later expanded by **Virchow (1855)**:

1. All living organisms are composed of cells.
2. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.
3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells (Virchow's addition).

> **Think About It:** Why was Virchow's addition so important? Before it, people believed life could arise spontaneously. Virchow's principle means every cell — and therefore every living thing — can be traced back to a parent cell.

### 3.2 Unicellular and Multicellular Organisms

#### Unicellular Organisms

Some organisms consist of **just one cell** that performs all life functions on its own. This single cell breathes, digests food, responds to its environment, and reproduces.

Examples: *Amoeba*, *Chlamydomonas*, *Paramecium*, bacteria

#### Multicellular Organisms

Most organisms we see — fungi, plants, animals — are **multicellular**. Their cells are specialised: heart cells pump blood, nerve cells carry signals, leaf cells perform photosynthesis. This division of labour makes complex life possible.

#### Cell Size and Shape

- Cells vary enormously in size. Most are microscopic (1–100 micrometres).
- Shape relates to function: nerve cells are long and branched (to carry signals), red blood cells are disc-shaped (to carry oxygen efficiently), *Amoeba* cells change shape constantly (to engulf food).
- Despite diversity, all cells share three basic features: **plasma membrane**, **nucleus** (or nucleoid), and **cytoplasm**.

---

### 3.3 Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane)

#### What It Is

The **plasma membrane** is the outermost covering of the cell in animals (in plant cells, it lies beneath the cell wall). It is made of **organic molecules called lipids and proteins**.

#### Why It Matters: Selective Permeability

The plasma membrane is called a **selectively permeable membrane** because it allows some substances to pass through freely, restricts others, and blocks some entirely.

- **Freely passing substances**: Carbon dioxide (CO₂), oxygen (O₂), water
- **Controlled passage**: Nutrients (glucose, amino acids), ions
- **Blocked substances**: Large molecules, unwanted chemicals

#### How Substances Move: Diffusion

**Diffusion** is the movement of a substance from an area of **high concentration** to an area of **low concentration** — without using energy.

- When CO₂ builds up inside a cell (cellular waste), it diffuses out through the membrane.
- When O₂ levels drop inside (used up in respiration), O₂ diffuses in from the surroundings.

#### How Water Moves: Osmosis

**Osmosis** is a special case of diffusion — it is the movement of **water molecules** through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of **high water concentration** (dilute solution) to a region of **low water concentration** (concentrated solution).

Three scenarios:

| Solution Type | Condition | Effect on Cell |
|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Hypotonic** | More water outside than inside | Water enters → cell swells (may burst in animal cells) |
| **Isotonic** | Same water concentration inside and outside | No net water movement → cell stays the same size |
| **Hypertonic** | Less water outside than inside | Water exits → cell shrinks (crenation in animal cells; plasmolysis in plant cells) |

> **Real-World Connection:** This is why cuts sting when you apply salt — the hypertonic salt draws water out of your cells. It's also why you should not drink seawater when dehydrated — it would pull water out of your blood cells by osmosis.

#### Endocytosis

The **flexibility** of the plasma membrane allows the cell to engulf food and other materials by folding around them. This process is called **endocytosis**. *Amoeba* acquires food this way.

### 3.4 Cell Wall

#### What It Is

Plant cells, unlike animal cells, have a **cell wall** — a rigid outer covering that lies outside the plasma membrane. It is mainly composed of **cellulose**, a complex carbohydrate that provides structural strength.

Fungi and bacteria also have cell walls, but of different compositions.

#### What It Does

1. **Protection**: The cell wall shields the plant cell from physical damage.
2. **Shape**: It gives the cell a definite shape.
3. **Withstands osmotic pressure**: When a plant cell absorbs water by osmosis and swells, the cell wall provides resistance (called **wall pressure**), preventing the cell from bursting. This is why plant cells can survive in hypotonic solutions that would burst animal cells.

#### Plasmolysis

When a plant cell is placed in a **hypertonic solution**, it loses water by osmosis. The plasma membrane and cytoplasm shrink away from the cell wall. This shrinkage is called **plasmolysis**.

> **Lab Observation**: Rheoeo leaf cells placed in a concentrated sugar/salt solution — the green chloroplasts (attached to plasma membrane) can be seen pulling away from the cell wall under the microscope.

### 3.5 Nucleus

#### Structure of the Nucleus

The nucleus is the **control centre** of the cell. It has several components:

| Component | Structure | Function |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Nuclear membrane** | Double-layered membrane with pores | Separates nucleus from cytoplasm; pores allow material transfer |
| **Chromosomes** | Rod-shaped, visible only during cell division | Carry genetic information in DNA form |
| **Chromatin** | Tangled thread-like DNA-protein material | Present in non-dividing cells; condenses into chromosomes during division |
| **Genes** | Functional segments of DNA | Code for proteins; control cell characteristics |

#### What the Nucleus Does

1. **Controls all cell activities** — it "instructs" the cell on what proteins to make.
2. **Determines cell development** — directs how the cell will develop and what form it takes.
3. **Reproduction** — plays a central role in cell division.

#### Nucleoid vs Nucleus

In **bacteria** (prokaryotes), there is no nuclear membrane. The DNA is concentrated in an undefined region called the **nucleoid**. It is not a true nucleus.

> **Think About It:** Viruses have no membranes at all. They cannot show any characteristics of life until they enter a living cell and use the cell's machinery. This suggests that membranes are essential for life functions.

---

### 3.6 Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

This is one of the most important comparisons in this chapter — examiners love asking it.

| Feature | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Size** | Generally small (1–10 μm) | Generally large (5–100 μm) |
| **Nuclear membrane** | Absent | Present (double-layered) |
| **Nuclear region** | Poorly defined; called nucleoid | Well-defined nucleus |
| **Chromosomes** | Single chromosome; no proteins | Multiple chromosomes; associated with histone proteins |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | Absent | Present (ER, Golgi, lysosomes, mitochondria, etc.) |
| **Ribosomes** | Present (smaller, 70S type) | Present (larger, 80S type) |
| **Cell wall** | Present (non-cellulose composition) | Present in plants/fungi (cellulose); absent in animals |
| **Examples** | Bacteria, Blue-green algae | Plants, animals, fungi, protists |

### 3.7 Cytoplasm and Cell Organelles

#### Cytoplasm

The **cytoplasm** is the fluid content inside the plasma membrane (but outside the nucleus). It is the "workspace" of the cell — all metabolic reactions happen here, and it contains all the **cell organelles**.

Organelles are to the cell what organs are to the body — each one does a specific job.

---

### 3.8 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The **Endoplasmic Reticulum** is a large network of **membrane-bound tubes and sheets** that runs throughout the cytoplasm.

#### Two Types

| Feature | Rough ER (RER) | Smooth ER (SER) |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Ribosomes** | Present on surface (makes it look rough) | Absent |
| **Main function** | Protein synthesis | Lipid/fat synthesis |
| **Also does** | Membrane biogenesis | Detoxification of drugs and poisons (in liver cells) |

#### Functions of ER

1. **Transport channel**: Carries materials from nucleus to cytoplasm and between different cytoplasm regions.
2. **Protein production** (RER): The ribosomes on RER manufacture proteins.
3. **Lipid production** (SER): Makes fat molecules essential for cell function.
4. **Membrane biogenesis**: The proteins and lipids made here help build the plasma membrane.
5. **Cytoplasmic framework**: Provides a surface for biochemical activities.

---

### 3.9 Golgi Apparatus

The **Golgi apparatus** was first described by Italian scientist **Camillo Golgi** (1843–1926). It consists of a system of **flattened, membrane-bound vesicles (sacs)** arranged in stacks. These stacks are called **cisternae**.

#### Functions

1. **Storage**: Stores materials synthesised near the ER.
2. **Modification**: Modifies proteins and lipids before they are sent out.
3. **Packaging**: Packages materials into vesicles for delivery.
4. **Dispatch**: Sends materials to various destinations inside and outside the cell.
5. **Lysosome formation**: Involved in forming lysosomes.

> **Analogy**: Think of the Golgi apparatus as the "post office" of the cell — it receives packages (proteins from ER), sorts and labels them, then dispatches them to the correct address.

---

### 3.10 Lysosomes

**Lysosomes** are small, membrane-bound sacs filled with **digestive enzymes**. These enzymes are made by the **RER**.

#### Functions

1. **Waste disposal**: Digest and break down foreign material (bacteria, food particles) entering the cell.
2. **Clean-up**: Digest worn-out cell organelles and cellular debris.
3. **Autolysis**: During cell disturbance or damage, lysosomes may rupture and digest the cell's own contents — this is why they are called the **"suicide bags" of the cell**.

> ⭐ **Exam Favourite**: "Why are lysosomes called suicide bags?" — Always include that they contain powerful digestive enzymes and can digest the cell itself when released.

---

### 3.11 Mitochondria

**Mitochondria** are double-membrane organelles known as the **"powerhouses of the cell"** because they produce ATP (energy).

#### Structure

- **Outer membrane**: Porous, smooth
- **Inner membrane**: Deeply folded into structures called **cristae** — these folds increase surface area for ATP-producing reactions

#### Functions

1. **ATP production**: Break down food molecules through cellular respiration to produce ATP.
2. **Energy for life**: ATP powers all cellular activities — movement, growth, synthesis.
3. **Self-replication**: Mitochondria have their own **DNA and ribosomes** — they can make some of their own proteins and replicate themselves.

> **Why do mitochondria have their own DNA?** Evolutionary theory suggests mitochondria were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by ancient cells and became permanent residents — keeping their own DNA is evidence of this ancient partnership.

△ **Common Confusion**: Mitochondria are involved in **respiration** (energy release), NOT photosynthesis. Photosynthesis happens in chloroplasts.

---

### 3.12 Plastids (Plant Cells Only)

**Plastids** are large organelles found **exclusively in plant cells**. There are two main types:

| Type | Colour | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Chromoplasts** | Coloured (green, yellow, orange) | Photosynthesis (if green = chloroplasts); attract pollinators |
| **Leucoplasts** | Colourless/white | Storage of starch, oils, proteins |

#### Chloroplasts (Most Important Plastid)

**Chloroplasts** are the green plastids containing the pigment **chlorophyll**.

- Site of **photosynthesis**: Convert sunlight + CO₂ + water → glucose + oxygen
- Internal structure: contains membrane layers embedded in a material called **stroma**
- Like mitochondria, chloroplasts have their **own DNA and ribosomes**

> **Why are leaves green?** Because chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, which absorbs red and blue light for photosynthesis but reflects green light — hence the green colour.

---

### 3.13 Vacuoles

**Vacuoles** are membrane-bound storage sacs in the cell.

| Feature | Animal Cells | Plant Cells |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Size** | Small, multiple vacuoles | One large central vacuole |
| **Volume** | Small fraction of cell | Up to 50–90% of cell volume |
| **Functions** | Temporary storage | Turgidity, storage of cell sap, waste storage |

#### Functions in Plant Cells

1. **Turgidity**: The large vacuole filled with cell sap exerts pressure on the cell wall, keeping the cell firm (turgid). This is why plants stand upright.
2. **Storage**: Stores amino acids, sugars, organic acids, proteins.
3. **Waste storage**: Stores waste products of metabolism.

In *Amoeba*, the **food vacuole** contains food the cell has engulfed, and specialised vacuoles help expel excess water and wastes.

### 3.14 Plant Cell vs Animal Cell — Complete Comparison

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cell wall** | Present (cellulose) | Absent |
| **Plasma membrane** | Present (inside cell wall) | Present (outermost boundary) |
| **Chloroplasts** | Present | Absent |
| **Vacuole** | One large central vacuole | Small, multiple vacuoles |
| **Nucleus** | Present (to the side due to vacuole) | Present (usually central) |
| **Centrioles** | Absent (in most plants) | Present |
| **Shape** | Fixed, rectangular | Variable, irregular |
| **Lysosomes** | Rarely present | Present |

### 3.15 Cell Division

New cells are produced for three reasons:
1. **Growth** — adding new cells
2. **Repair** — replacing dead or damaged cells
3. **Reproduction** — forming gametes (sex cells)

#### Mitosis — Growth and Repair

In **mitosis**, one mother cell divides to form **two daughter cells** with the **same number of chromosomes** as the mother cell.

- Result: 1 cell → 2 identical cells
- Chromosome number: remains the same (e.g., human cells have 46 chromosomes; after mitosis, both daughter cells have 46)
- Purpose: growth and repair of body tissues

#### Meiosis — Gamete Formation

In **meiosis**, one mother cell undergoes **two consecutive divisions** to form **four daughter cells** with **half the chromosome number**.

- Result: 1 cell → 4 cells
- Chromosome number: halved (46 → 23 in humans)
- Purpose: produces gametes (sperm and egg cells)
- Why half? So that when sperm and egg fuse during fertilisation, the resulting cell has the correct number (23 + 23 = 46)

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Number of divisions** | 1 | 2 |
| **Daughter cells produced** | 2 | 4 |
| **Chromosome number** | Same as mother cell | Half of mother cell |
| **Purpose** | Growth, repair | Gamete formation |
| **Where it occurs** | Body (somatic) cells | Reproductive organs |

## Section 4: Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

△ **Misconception 1: "Cell wall and plasma membrane are the same thing"**
They are different structures. **All cells** have a plasma membrane. Only **plant cells, fungi, and bacteria** have a cell wall (outside the plasma membrane). The cell wall is rigid; the plasma membrane is flexible.

△ **Misconception 2: "Osmosis and diffusion are the same"**
Diffusion is the movement of **any substance** from high to low concentration. Osmosis is specifically the movement of **water** across a **selectively permeable membrane**. Osmosis is a special type of diffusion.

△ **Misconception 3: "All cells look the same"**
Cells are highly variable in shape and size depending on their function. A nerve cell can be over 1 metre long; a red blood cell is only 8 micrometres. A fat cell is spherical; a muscle cell is elongated.

△ **Misconception 4: "Prokaryotes have no organelles at all"**
Prokaryotes do have **ribosomes** (though smaller than in eukaryotes). What they lack are **membrane-bound organelles** like mitochondria, ER, and Golgi apparatus.

△ **Misconception 5: "Lysosomes only destroy foreign things"**
Lysosomes also digest the **cell's own worn-out organelles** (a process called autophagy). And in extreme cases (cellular damage), they can digest the entire cell — hence "suicide bags."

△ **Misconception 6: "Mitosis produces gametes"**
Gametes (sperm and egg) are produced by **meiosis**, not mitosis. Mitosis is for growth and repair. This is a very common error in exams.

△ **Misconception 7: "Only plants perform photosynthesis because they have chloroplasts"**
True, but it is important to say that plants perform photosynthesis **because** they have chloroplasts with chlorophyll. The chloroplast is what makes this possible — not just being a plant.

△ **Misconception 8: "The nucleus controls everything in the cell directly"**
The nucleus contains DNA, which codes for proteins. These proteins do the actual work. The nucleus gives instructions; the ribosomes, ER, and other organelles carry them out.

## Section 5: Concept Connections

### 5.1 Links Within This Chapter

- **Plasma membrane → Osmosis → Turgidity**: The selectively permeable plasma membrane allows osmosis. In plant cells, water entering by osmosis fills the vacuole, creating turgidity. This connects membrane function to plant firmness.

- **Nucleus → Chromosomes → DNA → Genes → Protein synthesis**: The nucleus contains chromatin (DNA + protein). When the cell divides, chromatin condenses into chromosomes. Genes on the DNA code for proteins. The ER and ribosomes then make these proteins.

- **Mitochondria + Chloroplasts → Own DNA**: Both organelles have their own DNA and ribosomes, hinting at an ancient evolutionary origin as free-living bacteria. This is the "endosymbiont theory."

- **RER → Golgi → Lysosomes**: Ribosomes on RER make digestive enzymes → shipped to Golgi → Golgi packages them into lysosomes.

### 5.2 Links to Other Chapters

- **Class 9 Science Chapter 7 (Diversity in Living Organisms)**: Prokaryotes (bacteria) vs Eukaryotes relates directly to the five-kingdom classification — Monera are prokaryotes; all others are eukaryotes.

- **Class 9 Science Chapter 6 (Tissues)**: Multiple cells with similar structure and function group together to form tissues — directly extends from the cell concept.

- **Class 10 Science (Heredity and Evolution)**: DNA and chromosomes introduced here are the basis of heredity in Class 10.

- **Class 10 Science (How Do Organisms Reproduce?)**: Mitosis and meiosis introduced here form the foundation for understanding reproduction in Class 10.

### 5.3 Real-World Applications

- **Medicines and antibiotics**: Many antibiotics target the cell wall of bacteria (prokaryotes). Since human cells don't have cell walls, antibiotics can destroy bacteria without harming us.

- **Cancer**: Cancer is uncontrolled **mitosis** — cells keep dividing without stopping. Understanding cell division helps explain cancer biology.

- **Food preservation**: Salt (hypertonic) draws water out of bacteria cells by osmosis, killing them — this is the principle behind using salt to preserve food.

- **Kidney dialysis**: Works on the principle of diffusion across a selectively permeable membrane — just like the plasma membrane.

**1.** Who first observed and named cells?
**Answer**: Robert Hooke, in 1665, observing cork under a microscope.

**2.** What is a selectively permeable membrane?
**Answer**: A membrane that allows some substances to pass through freely but blocks or restricts others.

**3.** What happens to a cell placed in a hypotonic solution?

**Q: Explain the structure and function of the plasma membrane.**

The plasma membrane is the outermost covering of animal cells, made of lipids and proteins. It is selectively permeable — allowing some substances through while blocking others.

1. **Structural role**: Separates the cell's contents from its external environment, maintaining the cell's internal conditions. (1 mark)
2. **Transport role**: Allows CO₂ and O₂ to move by diffusion, and water by osmosis, without energy. Controls nutrient entry. (1 mark)

**Q: What are the differences between RER and SER?**

The Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) is a network of membrane-bound tubes in the cytoplasm. It exists in two forms:

1. **Rough ER (RER)** has ribosomes on its surface, which makes it look rough. Its main function is protein synthesis. (1 mark)
2. **Smooth ER (SER)** lacks ribosomes. It synthesises fat/lipid molecules important for cell function. In liver cells, SER detoxifies poisons and drugs. (1 mark)
3. Both types are involved in **membrane biogenesis** — the proteins and lipids they make help build and repair the plasma membrane. (1 mark)

1. **Outer membrane**: Smooth and porous — allows passage of materials. (1 mark)
2. **Inner membrane**: Deeply folded into structures called **cristae**, which increase surface area for ATP-generating reactions. (1 mark)

### 7.3 Model 5-Mark Answers

**Q: Compare plant cells and animal cells. Give five points of difference.**

Plant and animal cells are both eukaryotic cells, but they differ in several important ways.

1. **Cell wall**: Plant cells have a rigid cell wall made of cellulose outside the plasma membrane. Animal cells have no cell wall — only a plasma membrane. (1 mark)
2. **Chloroplasts**: Plant cells contain chloroplasts for photosynthesis. Animal cells have no chloroplasts and cannot photosynthesise. (1 mark)
3. **Vacuoles**: Plant cells have one large central vacuole (50–90% of cell volume) that maintains turgidity. Animal cells have small, scattered vacuoles. (1 mark)
4. **Centrioles**: Animal cells have centrioles involved in cell division. Most plant cells lack centrioles. (1 mark)
5. **Shape**: Plant cells are fixed and rectangular due to the rigid cell wall. Animal cells have variable, irregular shapes. (1 mark)

**Q: Describe the structure and function of the nucleus.**

The nucleus is the control centre of the cell. It directs all cellular activities and plays a central role in cell reproduction.

**Structure:**
1. **Nuclear membrane**: A double-layered membrane with pores that surround the nucleus. The pores allow transfer of materials between the nucleus and cytoplasm. (1 mark)
2. **Chromosomes/Chromatin**: In non-dividing cells, genetic material exists as loose threads called chromatin. Before cell division, chromatin condenses into rod-shaped chromosomes. Chromosomes contain DNA. (1 mark)
3. **Genes**: Functional segments of DNA called genes carry instructions for building and organising cells. DNA molecules contain the information for constructing proteins. (1 mark)

**Functions:**
4. **Control**: The nucleus directs all metabolic activities of the cell by regulating protein synthesis. (1 mark)
5. **Reproduction**: The nucleus plays a central role in cell division — chromosomes are distributed to daughter cells, ensuring genetic continuity. (1 mark)

## Section 10: Extra Practice Questions with Answers

### 1-Mark Questions

**Q1.** What does the term "cell" come from and who coined it?

**A**: The undefined nuclear region in prokaryotic cells that contains nucleic acids but has no nuclear membrane.

**Q5.** What pigment is present in chloroplasts?
**A**: Chlorophyll.

**Q6.** In which organisms are plastids found?
**A**: Plastids are found only in plant cells.

**Q7.** What are cisternae?
**A**: The flattened, membrane-bound sacs arranged in stacks that make up the Golgi apparatus.

**Q8.** Which type of ER is rough under a microscope and why?
**A**: Rough ER (RER) — it has ribosomes attached to its surface, giving it a rough appearance.

### 2-Mark Questions

**Q9.** Define osmosis and give one example from daily life.
**A**: Osmosis is the net diffusion of water molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of high water concentration (dilute solution) to a region of low water concentration (concentrated solution).
Example: When raisins are placed in water, they swell because water enters the raisin cells by osmosis (surrounding solution is hypotonic relative to the raisin cells).

**Q10.** What is endocytosis? How does Amoeba use it?
**A**: Endocytosis is the process by which the flexible plasma membrane folds around and engulfs food or other material from outside the cell, bringing it inside.
*Amoeba* uses endocytosis to capture food particles — it extends its membrane around a particle, engulfs it into a food vacuole, and then digests it with enzymes.

**Q11.** Distinguish between chromoplasts and leucoplasts.
**A**:
- **Chromoplasts**: Coloured plastids. Contain pigments like chlorophyll (green), carotenoids (yellow/orange). Function in photosynthesis (chloroplasts) or attracting pollinators.
- **Leucoplasts**: Colourless plastids. Function primarily as storage organelles — store starch, oils, and protein granules.

### 3-Mark Questions

**Q12.** Explain what happens to a plant cell when placed in (a) hypotonic solution, (b) isotonic solution, (c) hypertonic solution.

**A**:
1. **Hypotonic solution** (more dilute than cell): Water enters the cell by osmosis. The cell swells. The cell wall provides resistance and prevents bursting — the cell becomes **turgid**. (1 mark)
2. **Isotonic solution** (same concentration as cell): No net water movement. The cell remains the same size — **normal state**. (1 mark)
3. **Hypertonic solution** (more concentrated than cell): Water leaves the cell by osmosis. The cell membrane shrinks away from the cell wall — this is **plasmolysis**. The cell becomes flaccid. (1 mark)

**Q13.** Describe the structure of mitochondria with a labeled diagram description.

**A**:
Mitochondria are double-membrane organelles found in eukaryotic cells.

1. **Outer membrane**: Smooth, porous outer boundary. Allows passage of small molecules and ions. (1 mark)
2. **Inner membrane**: Deeply folded into finger-like projections called **cristae**. These folds greatly increase the surface area available for ATP-generating biochemical reactions. (1 mark)
3. **Own genetic material**: Mitochondria contain their own circular DNA and ribosomes. This allows them to synthesise some of their own proteins independently of the nucleus. (1 mark)

*Diagram labels*: Outer membrane, inner membrane, cristae (folds), matrix (space inside inner membrane).

---

**Q14.** Why are lysosomes important for cell survival? What happens if they malfunction?

**A**:
Lysosomes are membrane-bound sacs containing powerful digestive enzymes produced by the RER.

1. **Normal function**: They digest foreign material (bacteria, food particles) and worn-out organelles, keeping the cell clean and functional. (1 mark)
2. **Autophagy**: When organelles become old or damaged, lysosomes digest them so their components can be recycled. (1 mark)
3. **Malfunction consequence**: If lysosomes malfunction, undigested material accumulates in the cell, disrupting normal function. If lysosomes burst due to cellular damage, the released enzymes digest the cell's own proteins and structures — causing cell death. This is why they are called "suicide bags." (1 mark)

### 5-Mark Questions

**Q15.** How is a prokaryotic cell different from a eukaryotic cell? Give five differences. Include examples of each.

**A**:
Prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells represent the two fundamental types of cellular organisation.

1. **Nuclear membrane**: Prokaryotic cells lack a nuclear membrane — their genetic material exists in a region called the nucleoid. Eukaryotic cells have a well-defined nucleus surrounded by a double-layered nuclear membrane. (1 mark)
2. **Size**: Prokaryotic cells are generally smaller (1–10 μm). Eukaryotic cells are generally larger (5–100 μm). (1 mark)
3. **Membrane-bound organelles**: Prokaryotes have no membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria or ER. Eukaryotes have complex membrane-bound organelles. (1 mark)
4. **Chromosomes**: Prokaryotes have a single chromosome composed only of nucleic acid. Eukaryotes have multiple chromosomes associated with histone proteins. (1 mark)
5. **Examples**: Prokaryotes — bacteria, blue-green algae (cyanobacteria). Eukaryotes — plants, animals, fungi, protists. (1 mark)

**Q16.** Describe all the functions of the Golgi apparatus with a diagram description. Why is it important to the cell?

**A**:
The Golgi apparatus was first described by Camillo Golgi. It consists of flattened, membrane-bound sacs (cisternae) arranged in parallel stacks.

1. **Storage**: Stores proteins and lipids synthesised near the Endoplasmic Reticulum. Acts as a temporary holding area for cellular products. (1 mark)
2. **Modification**: Chemically modifies the proteins and lipids it receives — for example, adding sugar chains to proteins (glycosylation). (1 mark)
3. **Packaging**: Packages the modified materials into membrane-bound vesicles for transport. (1 mark)
4. **Dispatch**: Sends vesicles to destinations inside the cell (e.g., lysosomes) or outside the cell (secreted proteins). This is why it is called the "post office" of the cell. (1 mark)
5. **Lysosome formation**: The Golgi apparatus is directly involved in forming lysosomes — it packages digestive enzymes (made by RER) into membrane-bound sacs that become lysosomes. (1 mark)

*Diagram labels*: Flattened sacs (cisternae), vesicles budding off, ER connection on one side, plasma membrane connection on other side.

**Q17.** Explain cell division. Why is it important? Distinguish between mitosis and meiosis.

**A**:
Cell division is the process by which new cells are produced from existing cells. It is essential for life — without it, organisms could not grow, repair themselves, or reproduce.

**Importance:**
1. **Growth**: New cells are added during the growth of an organism — every time you grow taller, bone cells are undergoing mitosis. (1 mark)
2. **Repair**: Damaged or dead cells are replaced. When you get a cut, cells around the wound undergo mitosis to replace lost cells. (1 mark)

**Mitosis vs Meiosis:**
3. **Mitosis**: One mother cell divides once to produce two daughter cells. The daughter cells are genetically identical to the mother cell and have the same number of chromosomes. Occurs in all body (somatic) cells for growth and repair. (1 mark)
4. **Meiosis**: One mother cell undergoes two consecutive divisions to produce four daughter cells. Each daughter cell has half the number of chromosomes of the mother cell. Occurs in reproductive organs to produce gametes (sperm and eggs). (1 mark)
5. **Why half chromosomes in meiosis?**: When a sperm (23 chromosomes) fertilises an egg (23 chromosomes), the resulting cell (zygote) has the correct full set of 46 chromosomes. If gametes had 46 chromosomes, the zygote would have 92 — the chromosome number would double every generation. (1 mark)

### Case-Based Questions (4 Marks)

**Q18.** A student placed three pieces of potato in three different solutions: (A) pure water, (B) a 5% salt solution, and (C) a 15% salt solution. After 30 minutes, piece A had swollen, piece B remained unchanged, and piece C had shrivelled.

(i) Name the process responsible for these changes. (1 mark)
(ii) What type of solution is each solution with respect to the potato cells? (1 mark)
(iii) Explain why piece A swelled. (1 mark)
(iv) What would happen to a red blood cell placed in pure water? Would the result differ from the potato cell? Why? (1 mark)

**Answers**:
(i) Osmosis — movement of water across the selectively permeable cell membrane.
(ii) Solution A (pure water) = hypotonic. Solution B (5% salt) = isotonic. Solution C (15% salt) = hypertonic.
(iii) Pure water has higher water concentration than the potato cell's interior. Water moved from outside (high water concentration) into the cell (lower water concentration) by osmosis, causing the cell to swell.
(iv) The red blood cell would also absorb water and swell. Unlike the potato cell, it would eventually **burst** (lyse) because animal cells have no cell wall to resist the swelling pressure. Plant cells do not burst because the cell wall provides resistance and limits swelling.
