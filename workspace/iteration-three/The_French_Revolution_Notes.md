## Section 1: Chapter Introduction

### What This Chapter Is About

Imagine a country where 90% of the population pays all the taxes, but has zero political power. Where bread costs more than a day's wages. Where the king lives in staggering luxury while peasants starve. That was France in 1789 — and the explosion that followed changed the world forever. This chapter tells the story of how the French people overthrew one of the oldest monarchies in Europe, established a republic, executed their king, and then descended into a bloody Reign of Terror before a young general named Napoleon seized power.

### Why This Matters

- **In your daily life**: The rights you take for granted — equality before law, freedom of speech, the right to vote — were first codified during the French Revolution in the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
- **In other subjects**: The French Revolution deeply influenced Political Science (ideas of democracy and rights), Economics (the rise of capitalism and end of feudal dues), and English Literature (Romantic poets like Wordsworth and Shelley wrote extensively about it).
- **In exams**: This chapter carries approximately 4-5 marks in the CBSE Social Science paper. Source-based questions, timeline questions, and cause-effect chain questions are the most common types.
- **In the broader world**: The slogans "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" that originated in 1789 remain the national motto of France and have inspired democratic movements across the globe — from the Indian freedom struggle to the Arab Spring.

### What You'll Learn

After studying this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Describe the social structure of France under the Old Regime and explain why it was unfair.
2. Explain the causes — economic, social, and intellectual — that led to the French Revolution.
3. Trace the major events of the Revolution chronologically, from the Estates General to the fall of Napoleon.
4. Analyze the role of different groups — the middle class, peasants, women, and slaves — in shaping the Revolution.
5. Evaluate the legacy of the French Revolution and connect it to modern democratic principles.

## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 French Society During the Old Regime

**Background**: In 1774, **Louis XVI** of the Bourbon family became king of France at the age of 20. He inherited an empty treasury, a feudal social system, and a population growing faster than the food supply.

**Causes of the Financial Crisis**:

1. **War debts**: France had fought long, expensive wars, including supporting the American colonies against Britain. The American War alone added over a billion livres to a debt that already exceeded 2 billion livres.
2. **Extravagant court expenses**: The immense palace of Versailles and the lavish lifestyle of the royal court drained state resources continuously.
3. **Unfair tax system**: Only the Third Estate paid taxes — the clergy and nobility were exempt. This meant the state could never raise enough revenue to cover its expenses.

**What Happened**: French society was divided into three estates. The **First Estate** was the clergy (priests, bishops, and others who held church offices). The **Second Estate** was the nobility, who inherited titles, land, and feudal privileges by birth. The **Third Estate** included everyone else — from wealthy merchants and lawyers to landless peasants and servants. This estate made up roughly 90% of the population but carried the entire tax burden.

Peasants, who formed the vast majority of the Third Estate, owned very little land. About 60% of all land was held by nobles, the Church, and richer members of the Third Estate. Peasants paid **taille** (direct tax to the state), **tithes** (one-tenth of produce to the Church), and **feudal dues** (labour and payments to the lord). The result was crushing poverty. Lenders now charged 10% interest on state loans, forcing the government to spend a growing share of its budget just on interest payments.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: The treasury was effectively bankrupt; the king could not meet routine expenses like maintaining the army, running government offices, or funding universities.
- **Long-term**: The injustice of this system created deep resentment that fuelled revolutionary sentiment among all classes of the Third Estate.

> **THINK Think About It:** If you were a peasant in 1789 who paid taxes to three different authorities (state, Church, and lord) while your noble neighbour paid nothing, how would you feel about the system? Would you try to fix it from within, or would you want to overthrow it entirely?

### 3.2 The Subsistence Crisis and the Growing Middle Class

**Background**: The economic hardship of the 1780s was not just about unfair taxes. A combination of population growth, poor harvests, and stagnant wages created a crisis that threatened the survival of the poorest French citizens.

**Causes of the Subsistence Crisis**:

1. **Population explosion**: France's population rose from about 23 million in 1715 to 28 million in 1789 — a 22% increase that dramatically increased demand for food.
2. **Slow agricultural growth**: Grain production could not keep pace with the rising population, leading to food shortages.
3. **Rising bread prices**: Bread was the staple diet of the majority. As demand outstripped supply, prices soared beyond what workers could afford.
4. **Stagnant wages**: Workshop owners fixed wages, and these did not rise with prices. The gap between the poor and the rich widened year after year.
5. **Natural disasters**: Drought and hail frequently destroyed harvests, turning scarcity into famine.

**What Happened**: The subsistence crisis followed a predictable chain of destruction. A bad harvest meant the poorest could no longer buy bread. Malnutrition weakened bodies, making people vulnerable to disease epidemics. Scarcity drove food prices even higher, leading to more deaths and eventually to food riots. This cycle repeated frequently under the Old Regime.

At the same time, a new **middle class** was emerging within the Third Estate. Merchants who profited from overseas trade, manufacturers of woollen and silk textiles, lawyers, and administrative officials — all were educated and prosperous. Unlike peasants, they had the education and resources to articulate demands for change. They believed that social position should depend on merit, not birth. This idea came from philosophers like **John Locke** (who argued against the divine right of kings in his *Two Treatises of Government*), **Jean Jacques Rousseau** (who proposed government based on a social contract in *The Social Contract*), and **Montesquieu** (who proposed separation of powers into legislative, executive, and judiciary in *The Spirit of the Laws*). These ideas spread through salons, coffee-houses, books, and newspapers, often read aloud to those who could not read themselves.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Anger and protest against the system of privileges intensified, especially when news spread that Louis XVI planned to impose further taxes.
- **Long-term**: The middle class provided the intellectual leadership that turned scattered peasant revolts into a coherent revolutionary movement with clear political goals.

| Aspect | Peasant Revolts (Before 1789) | Middle-Class-Led Revolution (1789 Onwards) |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Leadership** | Spontaneous, local leaders | Educated professionals with political programmes |
| **Goals** | Lower taxes, cheaper bread | End of privileges, constitution, equal rights |
| **Methods** | Riots, refusal to pay dues | Political clubs, pamphlets, assemblies |
| **Reach** | Local villages | Entire nation |
| **Outcome** | Suppressed by force | Overthrew the monarchy |

> **THINK Think About It:** Georges Danton, a future revolutionary leader, wrote that he was well-educated but could find no job because he was not a noble. How does this connect to the idea that the middle class wanted a society based on "merit" rather than "birth"? Can you think of a similar situation today where education does not guarantee opportunity?

### 3.3 The Outbreak of the Revolution (1789)

**Background**: Louis XVI needed new taxes to save the bankrupt state. Under the Old Regime, he could not impose taxes unilaterally — he had to call the **Estates General**, a body representing all three estates that had last met in 1614.

**Causes**:

1. **Financial bankruptcy**: The king had no choice but to call the Estates General on 5 May 1789 to approve new taxes.
2. **Voting dispute**: The Third Estate demanded voting by head (one person, one vote), but the king insisted on the old system where each estate got one vote — meaning the clergy and nobility could always outvote the Third Estate two-to-one.
3. **Exclusion of common people**: The Third Estate's 600 representatives were prosperous, educated men. Peasants, artisans, and women were denied entry, even though 40,000 letters carrying their grievances had been brought to the assembly.
4. ** bread crisis and troop movements**: While the Estates General debated, a severe winter ruined the harvest. Bread prices soared, and the king ordered troops into Paris, raising fears of a violent crackdown.

**What Happened**: When the king rejected the Third Estate's demand for individual voting, its representatives walked out in protest. On 20 June 1789, they assembled in a tennis court at Versailles and declared themselves the **National Assembly**, swearing not to disperse until they had drafted a constitution for France. This was the **Tennis Court Oath**. They were led by **Mirabeau** (a nobleman who opposed feudal privilege) and **Abbe Sieyes** (a priest who wrote the influential pamphlet *What is the Third Estate?*).

While the Assembly debated at Versailles, the rest of France erupted. On 14 July 1789, an angry crowd of about 7,000 men and women stormed the **Bastille**, a fortress-prison that symbolised the king's despotic power. The commander was killed, and the prisoners were released. In the countryside, panic spread through what became known as the **Great Fear** — rumours that lords had hired brigands to destroy crops drove peasants to attack chateaux, burn records of manorial dues, and loot hoarded grain.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Faced with this mass uprising, Louis XVI recognised the National Assembly. On the night of 4 August 1789, feudal dues and taxes were abolished. Church tithes were abolished and Church lands were confiscated, giving the government assets worth at least 2 billion livres.
- **Long-term**: The storming of the Bastille became the defining symbol of the Revolution — 14 July is still celebrated as France's national day. The destruction of feudal records meant the old social order could never be restored.

### 3.4 France Becomes a Constitutional Monarchy (1791)

**Background**: After the abolition of feudalism, the National Assembly set about drafting a constitution that would restructure French government and society.

**Key Features of the Constitution of 1791**:

1. **Separation of powers**: Following Montesquieu's model, power was divided among the legislature (which made laws), the executive (which enforced them), and the judiciary (which interpreted them). No single body or person held absolute power.
2. **Indirect elections**: Citizens voted for electors, who then chose the National Assembly. This was not direct democracy.
3. **Limited franchise**: Only men above 25 who paid taxes equal to at least 3 days of a labourer's wage were **active citizens** with voting rights. All women and poorer men were classified as **passive citizens** with no vote. To be an elector or Assembly member, a man had to be in the highest tax bracket.

**What Happened**: The Constitution began with the **Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen**, which established that rights such as the right to life, freedom of speech, freedom of opinion, and equality before law were "natural, inalienable" — meaning they belonged to every human being by birth and could not be taken away. Key articles stated that men are born free and equal (Article 1), that sovereignty resides in the nation (Article 3), that liberty means doing anything that does not harm others (Article 4), and that all citizens have the right to participate in forming laws (Article 6).

However, there was a deep contradiction. The Declaration proclaimed equality and freedom for all, yet the Constitution denied the vote to women and poorer men. Revolutionary journalists like **Jean-Paul Marat** criticised this: "The task of representing the people has been given to the rich." The Constitution favoured the wealthier middle class, not the common people.

Political symbols became important in a society where most people could not read. The **broken chain** stood for freedom from slavery. The **bundle of rods (fasces)** symbolised strength through unity. The **red Phrygian cap** was worn by freed slaves in ancient Rome and became the symbol of liberty. The colours blue, white, and red became the French national colours. The **law tablet** signified that the law was the same for all.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: The Constitution gave France a framework for government that did not depend on the king's whim. But it also created deep dissatisfaction among those excluded from political participation.
- **Long-term**: The Declaration of the Rights of Man became one of the most influential documents in modern history, inspiring the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and the constitutions of many democratic nations including India.

> **THINK Think About It:** The Declaration of the Rights of Man said "Men are born free and equal in rights." But the Constitution of 1791 denied voting rights to women and the poor. Can a document that proclaims equality while practising inequality really be called revolutionary? How would Olympe de Gouges have answered this question?

### 3.5 The Abolition of Monarchy and the Republic (1792-1793)

**Background**: The constitutional monarchy did not last long. Louis XVI secretly negotiated with the King of Prussia, and European monarchies made plans to crush the Revolution by force.

**Causes of the Radicalisation**:

1. **The king's treason**: Louis XVI entered secret negotiations with Prussia and Austria to restore his absolute power, betraying the Constitution he had signed.
2. **War with Prussia and Austria**: In April 1792, the National Assembly declared war. Thousands of volunteers joined, singing the **Marseillaise** (composed by Roget de L'Isle), which became France's national anthem.
3. **Economic hardship**: War brought losses and food shortages. Women struggled to earn a living while men fought at the front.
4. **Political exclusion**: The Constitution of 1791 gave political rights only to the wealthy. The poor felt betrayed by the revolution they had fought for.

**What Happened**: Political clubs became rallying points for those who wanted to push the revolution further. The most important was the **Jacobin Club**, named after the convent of St Jacob in Paris. Its members were from the less prosperous sections — small shopkeepers, artisans like shoemakers and watch-makers, servants, and daily-wage workers. Their leader was **Maximilian Robespierre**. Many Jacobins wore long striped trousers (the **sans-culottes**) to distinguish themselves from nobles who wore knee breeches.

On 10 August 1792, Jacobins and Parisians stormed the Tuileries Palace, massacred the king's guards, and imprisoned the royal family. Elections were held with universal male suffrage — all men aged 21 and above could vote, regardless of wealth. The new assembly, called the **Convention**, abolished the monarchy on 21 September 1792 and declared France a **republic**. Louis XVI was tried for treason, found guilty, and executed by guillotine on 21 January 1793 at the Place de la Concorde. Marie Antoinette met the same fate shortly after.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: The most powerful monarchy in Europe was destroyed. A republic — a government elected by the people — was established for the first time in French history.
- **Long-term**: The execution of the king sent shockwaves across Europe. Other monarchies feared similar revolutions in their own countries and prepared for war against France.

### 3.6 The Reign of Terror (1793-1794)

**Background**: The newly declared republic faced threats from within (royalists, dissenters) and from without (European armies). Robespierre argued that extreme measures were necessary to protect the revolution.

**Causes**:

1. **External threats**: Armies from Prussia, Austria, and other monarchies were advancing on France, threatening to restore the monarchy.
2. **Internal enemies**: Former nobles, clergy who had lost privileges, and members of rival political factions were suspected of plotting against the republic.
3. **Economic crisis**: War and disruption had caused severe food shortages and inflation.
4. **Robespierre's ideology**: He believed that "terror is nothing but justice, swift, severe and inflexible" and that it was necessary to "annihilate the enemies of the republic."

**What Happened**: From 1793 to 1794, France lived under the **Reign of Terror**. Anyone suspected of being an enemy of the republic — ex-nobles, clergy, political rivals, even members of Robespierre's own party who disagreed with him — could be arrested, tried by a **revolutionary tribunal**, and executed by guillotine. Robespierre's government imposed strict controls: wages and prices were capped, meat and bread were rationed, peasants were forced to sell grain at government-fixed prices, and all citizens had to eat the pain d'egalite (equality bread) made of wholewheat. Churches were shut down and converted into barracks and offices. The traditional forms of address — Monsieur and Madame — were replaced with Citoyen and Citoyenne (Citizen).

Robespierre pursued these policies so relentlessly that even his own supporters turned against him. In July 1794, he was convicted by a court, arrested, and sent to the guillotine the next day.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: The Reign of Terror ended with Robespierre's death. An estimated 16,000-40,000 people were executed during this period.
- **Long-term**: The Terror showed the dark side of revolutionary idealism — the contradiction between fighting for liberty while denying it to others. It became a cautionary tale about how revolutionary governments can become tyrannical.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** The debate over whether the state should use extreme measures for "security" is still alive. After the 9/11 attacks, governments around the world — including India — passed laws that restricted civil liberties in the name of fighting terrorism. The tension between liberty and security that Robespierre and Desmoulins argued about in 1793 is the same tension democracies face today.

### 3.7 The Directory and the Rise of Napoleon (1795-1804)

**Background**: After Robespierre's fall, a power vacuum emerged. The wealthy middle classes seized the opportunity to reshape the government in their favour.

**Causes of Napoleon's Rise**:

1. **Political instability**: The new government, called the **Directory**, consisted of two elected legislative councils that appointed a five-member executive. The Directors clashed constantly with the councils, creating instability.
2. **Exclusion of the poor**: A new constitution denied the vote to non-propertied sections of society, alienating the masses who had driven the revolution.
3. **Continued economic problems**: Inflation, food shortages, and the costs of war continued to burden the population.
4. **Military success**: Napoleon Bonaparte was a brilliant military commander who won a series of victories for France, making him enormously popular.

**What Happened**: The Directory proved to be a weak and corrupt government that could not address France's problems. In 1799, Napoleon staged a coup and seized power. By 1804, he crowned himself Emperor of France. He conquered large parts of Europe, dispossessed old dynasties, and placed his family members on thrones across the continent.

Napoleon saw himself as a "moderniser." He introduced the **Napoleonic Code**, which protected private property, established a uniform system of weights and measures (the decimal system), and reformed administration. Initially, many Europeans saw him as a liberator who would bring the Revolution's ideals — liberty, equality, modern laws — to their countries. But his armies soon came to be seen as an invading force, and a coalition of European powers finally defeated him at **Waterloo in 1815**.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: France lost its republican government and became an empire under Napoleon. However, the feudal systems Napoleon abolished across Europe could not be easily restored after his defeat.
- **Long-term**: Napoleon spread revolutionary ideals — legal equality, property rights, modern administration — far beyond France's borders. Many of his reforms outlasted his empire. The French Revolution's true legacy was not the republic or the empire, but the ideas that survived them both.

> **THINK Think About It:** Napoleon destroyed the French Republic and made himself Emperor — the very thing the Revolution fought against. Yet he also spread the ideas of the Revolution across Europe. Was he a betrayer of the Revolution or its greatest champion? Can someone do both?

### 3.8 The Role of Women in the French Revolution

**Background**: Women were active participants in the Revolution from the very beginning, yet the new governments consistently denied them equal political rights.

**Causes of Women's Participation**:

1. **Economic necessity**: Most women of the Third Estate worked for a living — as seamstresses, laundresses, flower-sellers, or domestic servants. They had to feed their families while men were away at war or at political meetings.
2. **Political awakening**: The revolutionary ideals of liberty and equality inspired women to demand the same rights as men. They started their own political clubs and newspapers.
3. **Concrete grievances**: Women's wages were lower than men's, they had no access to education or job training, and they could not vote or hold political office.

**What Happened**: About sixty women's political clubs were formed across France. The most famous was the **Society of Revolutionary and Republican Women**. On 5 October 1789, a large crowd of Parisian women marched to Versailles and brought the king back to Paris — a powerful demonstration of women's political force. **Olympe de Gouges** wrote the *Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen* in 1791, directly challenging the original Declaration for excluding women.

The revolutionary government did pass some laws that improved women's lives: state schools were created with compulsory education for girls, fathers could no longer force daughters into marriage, marriage became a civil contract, divorce was legalised for both men and women, and women could train for jobs and run small businesses.

However, the gains were limited and temporary. During the Reign of Terror, the Jacobin government banned women's political clubs and activities. Chaumette, a Jacobin politician, argued that politics was "the kingdom of Man" and that women should stick to "domestic duties." Olympe de Gouges was executed in 1793 for criticising the government. French women did not win the right to vote until 1946 — more than 150 years after the Revolution.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Women gained some legal rights (education, divorce, marriage reform) but lost political rights when their clubs were shut down.
- **Long-term**: The French Revolution showed that women could organise, protest, and influence politics — but it also showed that even revolutionary governments could betray the principle of equality when it came to gender. The struggle for women's rights continued for over a century.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** France granted women the right to vote in 1946 — almost 157 years after the Revolution proclaimed equality. India granted women the right to vote at independence in 1947. Saudi Arabia allowed women to vote only in 2015. The gap between proclaiming equality and actually practising it remains a global challenge.

### 3.9 The Abolition of Slavery

**Background**: France's Caribbean colonies — Martinique, Guadeloupe, and San Domingo — produced sugar, coffee, tobacco, and indigo using slave labour. The slave trade was a major source of wealth for French port cities.

**Causes of the Slave Trade**:

1. **Labour shortage**: Europeans were reluctant to work in distant, unfamiliar tropical colonies. Plantation owners turned to Africa for forced labour.
2. **Profit motive**: The triangular slave trade (Europe → Africa → Americas → Europe) generated enormous profits for merchants in ports like Bordeaux and Nantes.
3. **Racist ideology**: Slavery was justified by the belief that Africans were inferior — an ideology that the Declaration of the Rights of Man should have demolished, but did not.

**What Happened**: The slave trade had operated since the seventeenth century. French merchants bought slaves from African chieftains, branded and shackled them, packed them into ships for a three-month voyage across the Atlantic, and sold them to plantation owners in the Caribbean. The National Assembly debated extending the "rights of man" to all French subjects, including colonial slaves, but did not act, fearing opposition from slave-dependent businessmen.

It was the **Convention** (the republican government) that finally legislated to free all slaves in French overseas possessions in 1794. This was one of the most radical acts of the Revolution. But it was short-lived: **Napoleon reintroduced slavery in 1802**, bowing to pressure from plantation owners who saw their "freedom" as including the right to enslave others. Slavery was finally and permanently abolished in French colonies in **1848**.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: Tens of thousands of enslaved people were freed in 1794, only to be re-enslaved under Napoleon eight years later.
- **Long-term**: The contradiction between proclaiming universal rights while maintaining slavery exposed the hypocrisy at the heart of the Revolution. It took another 54 years after Napoleon's reversal for slavery to be permanently abolished in French territories.

| Aspect | Declaration of Rights of Man (1789) | Reality for Slaves in French Colonies |
|--------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| "Men are born free and equal" | Proclaimed as a universal principle | Slaves were bought, branded, and shackled |
| "Liberty is a natural right" | Inalienable, cannot be taken away | Entire lives spent in forced labour |
| "No one may be deprived of property" | Property is a sacred right | Human beings were treated as property |
| "Resistance to oppression" | Listed as a natural right | Slaves who resisted were brutally punished |

> **THINK Think About It:** The Declaration of the Rights of Man said that "men are born free and equal in rights" and that "liberty" is a natural right. Yet the National Assembly refused to abolish slavery. The Convention abolished it in 1794, but Napoleon brought it back. What does this tell us about the limits of revolutionary ideals? Were the revolutionaries hypocrites, or were they constrained by economic realities?

### 3.10 The Revolution and Everyday Life

**Background**: The Revolution was not just about governments and constitutions. It changed how ordinary French people lived — what they wore, what they read, and even how they addressed each other.

**Key Changes**:

1. **Abolition of censorship**: Under the Old Regime, all books, newspapers, and plays had to be approved by the king's censors. After 1789, the Declaration of Rights proclaimed freedom of speech and expression as a natural right. Newspapers, pamphlets, books, and printed pictures flooded France. Opposing viewpoints could be published and debated openly.
2. **Political symbols**: Since most people could not read, symbols became a powerful way to communicate revolutionary ideas. The broken chain (freedom), the bundle of rods (unity), the Phrygian cap (liberty), and the tricolour (the nation) appeared everywhere — on buildings, in paintings, on flags.
3. **Changes in language and dress**: The traditional Monsieur and Madame were replaced with Citoyen and Citoyenne (Citizen). The sans-culottes rejected aristocratic knee breeches in favour of long striped trousers. The pain d'egalite (equality bread) replaced white flour bread.
4. **Festivals and public art**: The revolutionary government staged large public festivals using symbols from ancient Greece and Rome to give the Revolution an air of historical importance. Plays, songs, and processions made political ideas accessible to ordinary people.

**Effects**:
- **Immediate**: French people experienced a dramatic expansion in their freedom of expression and their ability to participate in public life, even if many formal political rights were still restricted.
- **Long-term**: The abolition of censorship and the explosion of print culture established principles of press freedom that became cornerstones of modern democracies.

## Section 10: Extra Practice Questions with Answers

### 1-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q1.** Name the political body that was convened by Louis XVI on 5 May 1789. ⭐
**A:** The Estates General was convened by Louis XVI on 5 May 1789 to pass proposals for new taxes. It was the first time it had been called since 1614.

**Q2.** Who composed the Marseillaise?
**A:** The Marseillaise was composed by the poet Roget de L'Isle. It was sung for the first time by volunteers from Marseilles as they marched into Paris and later became the national anthem of France.

**Q3.** What was the "Great Fear"?
**A:** The Great Fear was a wave of panic that spread through the French countryside in July 1789. Rumours that lords had hired brigands to destroy crops led peasants to attack chateaux, burn records of manorial dues, and loot hoarded grain.

### 2-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q4.** Differentiate between "active citizens" and "passive citizens" under the Constitution of 1791.
**A:**
1. **Active citizens** were men above 25 years of age who paid taxes equal to at least 3 days of a labourer's wage, giving them the right to vote.
2. **Passive citizens** were all remaining men and all women who did not meet the tax qualification, and they had no voting rights.

**Q5.** Why was the Bastille hated by the people of France?
**A:**
1. The Bastille was a fortress-prison that symbolised the despotic and arbitrary power of the king — people were imprisoned there without trial by the king's order.
2. It was stormed on 14 July 1789 because the crowd hoped to find hoarded ammunition, and its destruction became the defining symbol of the Revolution.

**Q6.** What was the triangular slave trade? Name two French ports involved in it.
**A:**
1. The triangular slave trade was a three-continent trade route: French merchants sailed from ports like Bordeaux and Nantes to Africa to buy slaves, transported them across the Atlantic to the Caribbean, and then carried sugar and coffee back to Europe.
2. Two French ports involved were **Bordeaux** and **Nantes**, which owed their economic prosperity to this trade.

### 3-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q7.** How did the political system work under the Constitution of 1791? ⭐
**A:**
The Constitution of 1791 made France a constitutional monarchy with the following structure:
1. **Separation of powers**: Power was divided among the legislature (National Assembly, which made laws), the executive (which enforced laws), and the judiciary (which interpreted laws), following Montesquieu's model.
2. **Indirect elections**: Citizens voted for electors, who then chose the National Assembly members. Only men above 25 paying at least 3 days' wages in taxes could vote (active citizens).
3. **Limited monarchy**: The king's powers were restricted by the constitution, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen guaranteed fundamental rights like liberty, equality, and freedom of speech.

**Q8.** Describe the role of philosophers in spreading revolutionary ideas in France.
**A:**
Philosophers played a crucial role in shaping the intellectual foundations of the French Revolution:
1. **John Locke** argued against the divine and absolute right of the monarch in his *Two Treatises of Government*, asserting that government exists to protect people's rights.
2. **Jean Jacques Rousseau** proposed a form of government based on a social contract between the people and their representatives in *The Social Contract*, arguing that sovereignty resides in the people.
3. **Montesquieu** proposed the separation of government into legislative, executive, and judiciary branches in *The Spirit of the Laws*, a model later implemented in the USA and in France's 1791 Constitution.

**Q9.** Explain the significance of the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
**A:**
The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen was a foundational document of the French Revolution:
1. It proclaimed that rights such as the right to life, freedom of speech, equality before law, and resistance to oppression were "natural and inalienable" — meaning they belonged to every human being by birth and could not be taken away.
2. It established that sovereignty resides in the nation and that all citizens have the right to participate in forming laws, either personally or through representatives.
3. It became one of the most influential political documents in modern history, inspiring the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and the fundamental rights provisions of democratic constitutions worldwide, including India's.

### 5-Mark Questions (2 questions)

**Q10.** Describe the events that led to the abolition of the monarchy and the establishment of the Republic in France. ⭐
**A:**
The abolition of the French monarchy was the result of a series of escalating events between 1789 and 1792:

1. **Louis XVI's betrayal**: After signing the Constitution of 1791, the king entered secret negotiations with the King of Prussia to restore his absolute power, which amounted to treason against the new constitutional order.

2. **War with Prussia and Austria**: In April 1792, the National Assembly declared war against these monarchies, which were planning to crush the Revolution. The war brought economic hardship and intensified political divisions within France.

3. **Rise of the Jacobins**: Political clubs, especially the Jacobin Club led by Robespierre, mobilised the poorer sections of society who were angry about food shortages, high prices, and their exclusion from political rights under the 1791 Constitution.

4. **Storming of the Tuileries**: On 10 August 1792, Jacobins and Parisians stormed the Tuileries Palace, massacred the king's guards, and imprisoned the royal family, effectively ending the monarchy's power.

5. **Establishment of the Republic**: Elections were held with universal male suffrage (all men 21+ could vote). The newly elected Convention abolished the monarchy on 21 September 1792 and declared France a Republic. Louis XVI was executed on 21 January 1793 for treason.

**Q11.** "The French Revolution was both a success and a failure for women." Evaluate this statement.
**A:**
The French Revolution brought both gains and disappointments for women, making it both a success and a failure for women's rights:

1. **Political participation**: Women were active from the beginning — they marched to Versailles on 5 October 1789, formed about 60 political clubs, and started their own newspapers, showing that women could be powerful political actors.

2. **Legal reforms**: The revolutionary government introduced significant improvements — compulsory education for girls, an end to forced marriage, marriage as a civil contract, legalised divorce for both men and women, and access to job training and small businesses.

3. **Intellectual contribution**: Olympe de Gouges wrote the *Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen* (1791), challenging the original Declaration for excluding women and demanding equal political rights — one of the earliest feminist manifestos in history.

4. **Political exclusion**: Despite their contributions, the Constitution of 1791 classified all women as "passive citizens" with no voting rights. The revolutionary ideals of equality did not extend to gender.

5. **Suppression and reversal**: During the Reign of Terror, women's political clubs were banned, their newspapers shut down, and activists like Olympe de Gouges were executed. French women did not win the vote until 1946 — 157 years after the Revolution. The Revolution was a partial success: it improved women's private lives but failed to grant them public equality.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills) (1 question)

**Q12.** Arthur Young, an Englishman who travelled through France in 1787-1789, wrote: "He who decides to be served and waited upon by slaves, ill-treated slaves at that, must be fully aware that by doing so he is placing his property and his life in a situation which is very different from that he would be in, had he chosen the services of free and well-treated men." To what extent did the French Revolution address the concern that Young is expressing here? Did the Revolution ultimately prove him right or wrong? Support your answer with examples.
**A:**

Arthur Young is warning that systems based on exploitation (slavery, serfdom) are inherently unstable — they create resentment that eventually leads to violence against the oppressors. He is essentially predicting the Revolution.

The Revolution partially addressed Young's concern in the following ways:

The abolition of feudal privileges on 4 August 1789 ended the system of forced labour, dues, and obligations that peasants owed to lords. The Declaration of the Rights of Man proclaimed that all men are born free and equal. The abolition of slavery in 1794 directly addressed the exploitation of enslaved people in the colonies.

However, the Revolution also proved Young right in several ways. The violence of the Revolution — the storming of the Bastille, the massacre of the king's guards at the Tuileries, the Reign of Terror's mass executions — was precisely the kind of backlash that Young warned about. When people are oppressed long enough, their response is not gradual reform but violent overthrow.

Furthermore, Napoleon's reintroduction of slavery in 1802 showed that the concern was not fully resolved. The Revolution removed one form of exploitation (feudalism) but could not fully overcome the economic interests that sustained another (slavery). In this sense, Young's insight was profound: systems of exploitation contain the seeds of their own destruction, but removing those systems requires more than declarations — it requires sustained commitment to equality, which the Revolution could not fully deliver.
