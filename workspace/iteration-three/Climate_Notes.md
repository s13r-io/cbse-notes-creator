### What This Chapter Is About

Why does it pour rain in Mumbai during June while Chennai stays relatively dry at the same time? Why can you wear a T-shirt in Thiruvananthapuram in December but need heavy woollens in Drass? The answer lies in one word: **monsoon**. This chapter explores India's climate system — the long-term pattern of temperature, rainfall, and winds that shapes everything from the crops we grow to the festivals we celebrate. ## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 Understanding Climate vs. Weather

Before we study India's climate in detail, it is essential to understand the difference between **climate** and **weather**, because these two terms are frequently confused in exams. Weather refers to the atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time — it is what you experience when you step outside. If it is raining in your city right now, that is weather. Climate, on the other hand, is the average pattern of weather over a long period — typically thirty years or more. When we say "India has a monsoon climate," we are describing a pattern that has been consistent over decades.

The elements of both weather and climate are the same: **temperature, atmospheric pressure, wind, humidity, and precipitation**. The key difference is the time scale. Weather can change within hours, but climate changes slowly. For example, a single hot day in May is weather; the fact that May is always hot in northwestern India is climate. On the basis of the generalised monthly atmospheric conditions, the year is divided into four seasons in India: winter, summer, the rainy season (advancing monsoon), and the retreating monsoon.

**Why this matters for your exam**: A common question asks you to distinguish between climate and weather. Remember — weather is short-term and local; climate is long-term and regional. Another exam favourite is asking about the elements of weather and climate. The answer is always: temperature, atmospheric pressure, wind, humidity, and precipitation.

### 3.2 Factors Affecting India's Climate

Six major controls determine the climate of any place on Earth. For India, each of these factors plays a distinctive role in creating the remarkable diversity of climatic conditions across the country — from the snowfall of Ladakh to the tropical warmth of Thiruvananthapuram.

**1. Latitude**

The **Tropic of Cancer** (23 degrees 30' N) passes through the middle of India, from the Rann of Kuchchh in the west to Mizoram in the east. This is the single most important latitudinal reference for India's climate. Almost half the country, lying south of the Tropic of Cancer, falls in the **tropical zone**, where temperatures remain high throughout the year. The remaining area, north of the Tropic, lies in the **sub-tropical zone**, where temperatures show greater seasonal variation. Because India spans both zones, its climate has characteristics of both tropical and subtropical climates.

For example, compare Thiruvananthapuram (8 degrees 29' N, near the equator) with Delhi (29 degrees N, in the subtropics). Thiruvananthapuram's annual temperature range is only about 2.1 degrees C (from 26.2 degrees C to 28.7 degrees C), while Delhi's range is about 18.9 degrees C (from 14.4 degrees C to 33.3 degrees C). The closer you are to the equator, the less variation in temperature across the year.

**2. Altitude**

Temperature decreases with increasing altitude — approximately 1 degree C for every 165 metres of ascent. India has mountains in the north with an average height of about 6,000 metres (the Himalayas) and coastal areas where the maximum elevation is about 30 metres. This enormous altitude difference creates vastly different climatic conditions.

Compare Leh (3,506 metres altitude) with Mumbai (11 metres altitude). Leh's January temperature is 8.5 degrees C and July is 17.2 degrees C, while Mumbai's January is 24.4 degrees C and May reaches 30 degrees C. The Himalayas also serve a critical function: they act as an effective barrier, preventing the bitterly cold winds from Central Asia from entering the subcontinent. This is why northern India experiences comparatively milder winters than locations at the same latitude in Central Asia.

**3. Pressure and Wind Systems**

The pressure and wind conditions over India are unique and are governed by three factors: pressure and surface winds, upper air circulation, and western cyclonic disturbances and tropical cyclones. India lies in the region of north-easterly winds that originate from the subtropical high-pressure belt of the northern hemisphere. These winds carry little moisture because they blow over land. Without the monsoon reversal, India would be an arid land.

However, the seasonal reversal of pressure systems changes everything. In winter, a high-pressure area north of the Himalayas drives cold, dry winds southward. In summer, intense low pressure develops over northwestern India and interior Asia, drawing moisture-laden winds from the Indian Ocean. This complete wind reversal is what makes India's climate monsoonal.

**4. Distance from the Sea (Continentality)**

The sea has a moderating influence on temperature because water heats and cools slowly compared to land. Coastal areas like Mumbai, Thiruvananthapuram, and Chennai experience smaller temperature variations throughout the year. In contrast, places far from the sea — like Delhi, Jodhpur, and the interior of the Deccan Plateau — experience extreme temperatures: very hot summers and very cold winters. This effect is called **continentality**.

The data makes this clear: Mumbai (coastal, 19 degrees N) has an annual range of only 5.6 degrees C (24.4 to 30.0 degrees C), while Delhi (interior, 29 degrees N) has a range of 18.9 degrees C (14.4 to 33.3 degrees C). Jodhpur, deep in the interior, has a range of 19.0 degrees C (14.9 to 33.9 degrees C).

**5. Ocean Currents**

Ocean currents, along with onshore winds, affect the climate of coastal areas. A coastal area with warm currents flowing past it will be warmed if the winds are onshore, while cold currents will have a cooling effect. Although the NCERT chapter mentions this factor briefly, it is relevant for understanding why certain coastal regions have different temperature profiles than expected from latitude alone.

**6. Relief Features**

High mountains act as barriers for cold or hot winds. They also cause **orographic rainfall** when they lie in the path of rain-bearing winds. The windward side of mountains receives heavy rainfall, while the leeward side remains relatively dry (the **rain shadow effect**). The Western Ghats are the best Indian example: the windward side (facing the Arabian Sea) receives over 250 cm of rainfall, while the Deccan Plateau on the leeward side receives much less. Similarly, the Himalayas block cold Central Asian winds, protecting the subcontinent from extreme cold.

> **MAP Locate on a map:** The Western Ghats — running roughly north-south along the western coast of India, parallel to the Arabian Sea coastline. The windward (western) side faces the sea; the leeward (eastern) side is the Deccan Plateau.

### 3.3 The Indian Monsoon Mechanism

The monsoon is the defining feature of India's climate, and understanding its mechanism is essential for both exams and general knowledge. The word "monsoon" comes from the Arabic word *mausim*, meaning season. It refers specifically to the seasonal reversal of wind direction. The complete mechanism operates in a chain of interconnected steps.

**What causes it**: Differential heating of land and water creates pressure differences that reverse the wind direction between summer and winter.

**Process Steps**:

1. **Differential heating of land and sea**: During summer (March-May), the landmass of northern India and the Tibetan Plateau heat up much faster than the surrounding oceans because land has a lower specific heat capacity than water. By May, a large area stretching from the Thar Desert to the Chotanagpur Plateau becomes intensely hot, with temperatures reaching 45 degrees C.

2. **Formation of intense low pressure over land**: The intense heating creates a strong low-pressure zone over northwestern India and interior Asia (particularly over the Tibetan Plateau). This low-pressure zone is so powerful that it attracts winds from thousands of kilometres away.

3. **Pressure gradient formation between ocean and land**: The Indian Ocean, which has heated up much less than the land, remains a region of relatively higher pressure. A steep pressure gradient develops between the high-pressure area over the southern Indian Ocean and the low-pressure area over the Indian subcontinent.

4. **Wind direction reversal — the trade winds respond**: The normal north-easterly trade winds (which blow from land to sea during winter) are replaced. Instead, the southern hemisphere's south-east trade winds are drawn northward toward the low-pressure zone over India. These winds cross the equator and enter the Indian peninsula.

5. **Deflection by the Coriolis force**: As the south-east trade winds cross the equator and enter the northern hemisphere, the **Coriolis force** deflects them to the right. This transforms them into **south-west monsoon winds**. This deflection is crucial — without it, the winds would continue moving in a straight line and would not bring rain to most of India.

6. **Branch splitting — Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal branches**: The south-west monsoon splits into two branches after reaching the Indian peninsula. The **Arabian Sea branch** moves northward along the Western Coast, delivering heavy rainfall to the windward side of the Western Ghats (over 250 cm) and then crossing to the Ganga plains. The **Bay of Bengal branch** moves northeastward, hitting the Myanmar coast and then being deflected westward by the Himalayas, bringing heavy rain to northeastern India (Mawsynram receives the world's highest average rainfall).

7. **Orographic effects and rainfall distribution**: When these moisture-laden winds encounter mountain barriers — the Western Ghats on the west coast or the Himalayas in the northeast — they are forced to ascend. As the air rises, it cools (adiabatic cooling), condenses, and produces heavy rainfall on the windward slopes. The leeward sides receive significantly less rain, creating rain shadow areas like the Deccan Plateau's interior and Rajasthan.

8. **Retreating monsoon**: By October-November, the low-pressure over northern plains weakens as the sun apparently moves southward. The south-west monsoon winds weaken and begin to withdraw. The low-pressure shifts to the Bay of Bengal, where cyclonic depressions form over the Andaman Sea. These bring heavy rain to the Coromandel Coast (Tamil Nadu), which receives the bulk of its annual rainfall during this retreating phase.

**Effects**:

- **On the landscape**: The monsoon transforms India's landscape seasonally — dry brown fields turn green within weeks of the first rains; rivers swell and flood; dry riverbeds fill with water. The rhythm of wet and dry seasons defines India's vegetation patterns, from tropical evergreen forests in the northeast to thorn scrub in Rajasthan.
- **On human life**: The Indian agricultural calendar revolves entirely around the monsoon. Over 60% of India's farmland is rain-fed. The timing of the monsoon determines when crops are sown (kharif crops with the advancing monsoon, rabi crops with winter rain). Festivals like Onam in Kerala and Bihu in Assam celebrate the monsoon's arrival. A failed monsoon leads to drought, farmer distress, and economic disruption.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** Climate change is altering the monsoon pattern. Recent years have seen more intense bursts of rainfall followed by longer dry spells — exactly the "breaks in monsoon" phenomenon but more extreme. In 2019, floods in Maharashtra and Bihar displaced millions, while parts of Tamil Nadu faced water shortages simultaneously.

> **THINK Think About It:** If the monsoon arrives two weeks late, what happens to a farmer who has already planted seeds based on the expected arrival date? How does this connect to the concept of food security you may have studied in Economics?

### 3.4 The Four Seasons of India

India experiences four distinct seasons, each with characteristic temperature, rainfall, and wind patterns. The seasonal contrast is most noticeable in the interior of the country, while coastal areas show less temperature variation.

#### The Cold Weather Season (December to February)

The cold weather season begins from mid-November in northern India and lasts till February. December and January are the coldest months in the northern part of the country. The temperature decreases from south to the north during this season. Chennai on the eastern coast has an average temperature of 24-25 degrees C, while the northern plains range between 10 and 15 degrees C. Days are generally warm and nights are cold — a significant diurnal (day-night) temperature range. Frost is common in the north, and the higher slopes of the Himalayas receive snowfall.

The **north-east trade winds** prevail during this season. They blow from land to sea, making it a dry season for most of the country. However, there is an important exception: the **Tamil Nadu coast** receives rainfall during winter because these winds blow from sea to land along that coast. In the northern plains, a feeble high-pressure region develops with light winds.

A characteristic feature is the inflow of **western cyclonic disturbances** from the Mediterranean Sea and western Asia. These low-pressure systems move into India along with the westerly flow and cause winter rain over the plains and snowfall in the mountains. This winter rainfall (locally called **mahawat**) is critically important for the cultivation of **rabi crops** like wheat, barley, and mustard.

The peninsular region does not have a well-defined cold season because the moderating influence of the sea prevents significant temperature drops.

#### The Hot Weather Season (March to May)

During March to May, the global heat belt shifts northward due to the apparent movement of the sun. By March, the highest temperature is about 38 degrees C on the Deccan Plateau. In April, temperatures in Gujarat and Madhya Pradesh reach around 42 degrees C. By May, 45 degrees C is common in northwestern India. Peninsular India remains cooler due to the moderating influence of the surrounding oceans.

Summer brings rising temperatures and falling air pressure in northern India. Towards the end of May, an elongated **low-pressure trough** develops from the Thar Desert to Patna and the Chotanagpur Plateau. This low-pressure zone is the trigger that will eventually draw the monsoon winds.

Key phenomena of this season:

- **Loo**: Strong, gusty, hot, dry winds in north and northwestern India that can be fatal with direct exposure
- **Dust storms**: Common in May across northern India; they bring temporary relief by lowering temperatures and sometimes bringing light rain
- **Kaal Baisakhi**: Violent localised thunderstorms in West Bengal with torrential rain and hail
- **Mango showers**: Pre-monsoon rains in Kerala and Karnataka that help ripen mangoes early

> **THINK Think About It:** Why does peninsular India escape the extreme heat that northern India experiences, even though cities like Chennai are closer to the equator? (Hint: think about what surrounds the peninsula on three sides.)

#### The Advancing Monsoon — The Rainy Season (June to September)

By early June, the low-pressure over the northern plains intensifies dramatically. This attracts the trade winds of the southern hemisphere, which cross the equator and enter India as the **south-west monsoon**. The winds blow at an average velocity of 30 km per hour and cover the country in about a month, except the extreme northwest.

The advancing monsoon transforms the weather completely. The windward side of the Western Ghats receives extremely heavy rainfall — over 250 cm. The northeastern region receives the maximum rainfall: **Mawsynram** in the southern ranges of the Khasi Hills receives the **highest average rainfall in the world**. Rainfall decreases from east to west in the Ganga valley. Rajasthan and parts of Gujarat receive scanty rainfall.

A critical feature is the tendency for **breaks in monsoon rainfall**. The monsoon does not rain continuously — it has wet spells and dry spells. These breaks are caused by the movement of the monsoon trough. When the trough axis lies over the plains, rainfall is good there. When it shifts closer to the Himalayas, the plains experience dry spells while the mountains receive heavy rain, causing devastating floods in the river plains. **Tropical depressions** that form at the head of the Bay of Bengal also influence rainfall — they cross the mainland and bring rain along the monsoon trough axis.

#### The Retreating Monsoon — Transition Season (October to November)

With the apparent southward movement of the sun, the low-pressure trough over the northern plains weakens and is replaced by a high-pressure system. The south-west monsoon winds weaken and start withdrawing. By early October, the monsoon withdraws from the Northern Plains.

This transition period is marked by clear skies, high daytime temperatures, and cool pleasant nights. Because the land is still moist from monsoon rains, high humidity combines with high temperature to create oppressive conditions known as **October heat**. By late October, temperatures begin falling rapidly in northern India.

The most significant weather event of this season is the formation of **tropical cyclones**. The low-pressure conditions shift from northwestern India to the Bay of Bengal by early November. Cyclonic depressions originate over the Andaman Sea and cross the eastern coast, causing heavy and widespread rainfall. The thickly populated deltas of the **Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri** rivers are frequently struck by these destructive cyclones. The **Coromandel Coast** (Tamil Nadu) receives the bulk of its annual rainfall from these retreating monsoon cyclones — this is why Chennai gets most of its rain in October-November rather than during the June-September monsoon.

> **MAP Locate on a map:** The Coromandel Coast — the eastern coastal strip of India along the Bay of Bengal, from Point Calimere (Tamil Nadu) in the south to the deltas of the Godavari and Krishna in Andhra Pradesh.

### 3.5 Distribution of Rainfall in India

India's rainfall distribution is highly uneven, both spatially and temporally. Understanding this pattern is essential for exams because map-based and data-interpretation questions frequently test this topic.

**Rainfall zones of India**:

- **Areas of very high rainfall (over 400 cm)**: Western coast (especially the windward side of the Western Ghats) and northeastern India (Meghalaya, Assam). These areas receive rain primarily from the Arabian Sea branch and Bay of Bengal branch of the monsoon respectively.
- **Areas of high rainfall (200-400 cm)**: Parts of the Western Ghats, the northeastern states, and the eastern coast.
- **Areas of moderate rainfall (100-200 cm)**: Most of peninsular India including Mumbai (183.4 cm), Kolkata (162.5 cm), and Thiruvananthapuram (181.2 cm).
- **Areas of low rainfall (50-100 cm)**: Interior of the Deccan Plateau, Delhi (67.0 cm), parts of the Ganga plains.
- **Areas of very low rainfall (less than 50 cm)**: Western Rajasthan, Ladakh, and the leeward side of the Western Ghats. Jodhpur receives only 36.6 cm and Leh receives just 8.5 cm annually.

**Seasonal distribution of rainfall is equally uneven.** Most parts of the country receive rainfall from June to September (the advancing monsoon). However, the Tamil Nadu coast receives a large portion of its rain from October to November (retreating monsoon). Winter rain in northern India from western disturbances is crucial for rabi crops but amounts to only a small fraction of annual rainfall.

**Annual variability is a major concern.** The monsoon is highly variable from year to year, especially in low-rainfall regions like Rajasthan, Gujarat, and the leeward side of the Western Ghats. Areas of high rainfall are flood-prone, while areas of low rainfall are drought-prone. This dual vulnerability is a fundamental challenge for Indian agriculture and water management.

### 3.6 Monsoon as a Unifying Bond

Despite the great regional variations in temperature and rainfall, the monsoon serves as a unifying force across the Indian subcontinent. This concept is important for understanding India's geographical identity and is a frequent exam topic.

The **Himalayas** protect the subcontinent from extremely cold Central Asian winds, enabling northern India to have uniformly higher temperatures compared to areas at similar latitudes. The **Peninsular Plateau**, surrounded by sea on three sides, maintains moderate temperatures. Both moderating influences are significant, yet there are still great variations — from Drass at minus 45 degrees C to Thiruvananthapuram at 22 degrees C in winter.

The unifying influence of the monsoon is perceptible in several ways:

- **Seasonal rhythm**: The seasonal alteration of wind systems provides a rhythmic cycle of seasons that all Indians experience, even if the intensity varies. Everyone, from Kashmir to Kanyakumari, awaits the monsoon.
- **Agricultural calendar**: The entire agricultural cycle — sowing, weeding, harvesting — revolves around the monsoon. Kharif crops are sown with the onset of monsoon; rabi crops depend on the winter rain. This shared agricultural rhythm binds diverse farming communities.
- **Cultural life**: Festivals, songs, dances, and food traditions are connected to seasonal changes. Onam in Kerala celebrates the harvest brought by the monsoon; Bihu in Assam marks the Assamese new year tied to the agricultural cycle.
- **River systems**: The monsoon-fed rivers — the Ganga, Brahmaputra, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri — unite different regions as parts of interconnected river valley systems.

Even the uncertainties of the monsoon — its uneven distribution, its breaks and variability — are shared experiences. Year after year, from north to south and east to west, people of India eagerly await the monsoon's arrival, knowing their livelihoods depend on it.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** The India Meteorological Department (IMD) now issues advanced monsoon forecasts using satellite data and computer models. Despite these advances, the monsoon remains unpredictable enough that farmers and economists watch its progress anxiously every June.

## Section 7: Exam-Oriented Summary

### Top 8 Things to Remember

1. ⭐ **Climate vs. Weather**: Climate is long-term (30+ years) and regional; weather is short-term and local. Both share the same elements: temperature, pressure, wind, humidity, precipitation.
2. ⭐ **Six factors affecting India's climate**: Latitude, Altitude, Pressure and wind systems, Distance from the sea (continentality), Ocean currents, Relief features. Remember the acronym **LAPDOR**.
3. ⭐ **Monsoon mechanism (6+ steps)**: Differential heating → Low pressure over land → Pressure gradient → Trade winds cross equator → Coriolis deflection → Branch splitting (Arabian Sea + Bay of Bengal) → Orographic rainfall → Retreating monsoon.
4. ⭐ **Mawsynram** in the Khasi Hills of Meghalaya receives the world's highest average rainfall — NOT the Western Ghats.
5. ⭐ **Tamil Nadu coast gets rain from the retreating monsoon** (October-November), not the advancing monsoon (June-September). This is because of cyclonic depressions from the Bay of Bengal.
6. **Western disturbances** bring winter rain (mahawat) important for rabi crops. They originate from the Mediterranean Sea and are NOT part of the monsoon.
7. **Breaks in monsoon** are caused by the movement of the monsoon trough. When the trough shifts toward the Himalayas, the plains get dry spells and the mountains get floods.
8. **Continentality**: Interior regions (Delhi, Jodhpur) have extreme temperature ranges; coastal regions (Mumbai, Thiruvananthapuram) have moderate ranges because of the sea's moderating influence.

### Model Answers

#### 1-Mark Questions

**Q:** What is meant by monsoon?
**A:** Monsoon refers to the seasonal reversal in wind direction during a year. The word is derived from the Arabic word *mausim*, meaning season.

**Q:** Name the wind that causes rainfall along the Malabar Coast.
**A:** The south-west monsoon wind causes rainfall along the Malabar Coast. The Arabian Sea branch of the monsoon brings heavy rainfall to the windward side of the Western Ghats.

**Q:** Why does India have an almost uniform pattern of temperature despite latitudinal extent?
**A:** The Himalayas protect the subcontinent from cold Central Asian winds and the peninsular plateau is surrounded by sea on three sides, both of which moderate temperatures across India.

#### 3-Mark Questions

**Q:** Why does India have a monsoon type of climate?
**A:** India has a monsoon type of climate due to seasonal reversal of wind direction.
1. During summer, intense low pressure develops over northwestern India, drawing moisture-laden south-west monsoon winds from the Indian Ocean, bringing widespread rainfall.
2. During winter, high pressure develops north of the Himalayas, causing dry north-east trade winds to blow from land to sea.
3. This seasonal reversal is caused by differential heating of land and sea, and it gives India its characteristic wet and dry seasons.

**Q:** Differentiate between the advancing monsoon and the retreating monsoon season.
**A:**
1. **Duration**: Advancing monsoon lasts from June to September; retreating monsoon lasts from October to November.
2. **Wind direction**: Advancing monsoon has south-west winds; retreating monsoon has weakening north-east winds.
3. **Rainfall distribution**: Advancing monsoon brings rain to most of India (except Tamil Nadu); retreating monsoon brings heavy rain mainly to the Coromandel Coast (Tamil Nadu) from cyclonic depressions.

#### 5-Mark Questions

**Q:** Describe the factors affecting the climate of India.
**A:** India's climate is influenced by six major factors that work together to create the country's diverse climatic conditions.
1. **Latitude**: The Tropic of Cancer passes through the middle of India. Areas south of it are tropical (less temperature variation), while areas north are subtropical (greater seasonal variation).
2. **Altitude**: Temperature decreases with height. Leh at 3,506 m has a very different climate from Mumbai at 11 m. The Himalayas also block cold winds from Central Asia.
3. **Pressure and wind systems**: Seasonal reversal of pressure conditions over land and sea drives the monsoon winds. The Coriolis force deflects winds, determining rainfall patterns.
4. **Distance from the sea**: Coastal areas have moderate temperatures (low continentality), while interior areas like Delhi and Jodhpur have extreme temperatures (high continentality).
5. **Relief features**: Mountains like the Western Ghats cause orographic rainfall on the windward side and create rain shadow areas on the leeward side. The Himalayas also trap monsoon winds and cause heavy rainfall in the northeast.

**Q:** Describe the mechanism of the Indian monsoon.
**A:** The Indian monsoon is driven by differential heating of land and sea, which reverses wind direction between seasons.
1. During summer, the Indian landmass heats up much faster than the surrounding oceans, creating an intense low-pressure zone over northwestern India.
2. This low pressure attracts the south-east trade winds from the southern hemisphere across the equator.
3. The Coriolis force deflects these winds to the right as they cross into the northern hemisphere, transforming them into south-west monsoon winds.
4. The monsoon splits into two branches — the Arabian Sea branch and the Bay of Bengal branch — each bringing rainfall to different parts of India.
5. The Arabian Sea branch causes heavy orographic rainfall on the Western Ghats; the Bay of Bengal branch brings heavy rain to northeastern India (Mawsynram).
6. In winter, the pattern reverses: high pressure north of the Himalayas causes dry north-east winds to blow from land to sea, while western disturbances bring some winter rain to the plains.

### 1-Mark Questions

**Q1.** What is the direction of the wind during the advancing monsoon season? ⭐
**A:** During the advancing monsoon season (June to September), the wind blows in the **south-westerly direction**. These are the south-west monsoon winds that bring rainfall to most of India.

**Q2.** Which instrument is used to measure rainfall?
**A:** Rainfall is measured using an instrument called a **rain gauge**. It collects and measures the amount of liquid precipitation over a set period.

**Q3.** Define continentality.
**A:** **Continentality** refers to the condition where places far from the sea experience extreme temperatures — very hot summers and very cold winters — because the sea's moderating influence decreases with increasing distance from the coast.

### 2-Mark Questions

**Q4.** Why does Thiruvananthapuram have an equable (moderate) climate?
**A:**
1. Thiruvananthapuram is located very close to the equator (8 degrees 29' N) and is situated on the coast, so it experiences the moderating influence of the sea from both the Arabian Sea and the Indian Ocean.
2. Its annual temperature range is only about 2.1 degrees C (26.2 to 28.7 degrees C) because coastal locations and equatorial proximity both reduce seasonal temperature variation.

**Q5.** Differentiate between loo and Kaal Baisakhi.
**A:**
1. **Loo** refers to strong, gusty, hot, dry winds that blow during the day over north and northwestern India during the hot weather season, and can even be fatal.
2. **Kaal Baisakhi** refers to violent localised thunderstorms in West Bengal during the hot weather season, characterised by torrential rain, violent winds, and often hail.

**Q6.** Why is the monsoon considered a unifying bond? Give any two reasons.
**A:**
1. The seasonal rhythm of the monsoon — the cycle of wet and dry seasons — is shared across the entire country, from Kashmir to Kanyakumari, binding all regions through a common climatic experience.
2. India's agricultural calendar, festivals, and cultural life all revolve around the monsoon, creating shared patterns of livelihood and celebration across diverse regions.

### 3-Mark Questions

**Q7.** Explain the mechanism of the south-west monsoon. ⭐
**A:**
The south-west monsoon is caused by the differential heating of land and sea during summer.
1. During summer, the landmass of north India heats up intensely, creating a strong low-pressure zone over the Thar Desert and surrounding regions.
2. This low pressure attracts the south-east trade winds from the southern hemisphere. These winds cross the equator and are deflected to the right by the Coriolis force, becoming south-west monsoon winds.
3. These moisture-laden winds split into two branches: the Arabian Sea branch brings heavy rainfall to the Western Ghats, while the Bay of Bengal branch brings heavy rain to northeastern India (Mawsynram).

**Q8.** Why does the Tamil Nadu coast receive winter rainfall while most of India remains dry?
**A:**
The Tamil Nadu coast receives winter rainfall due to the direction of the prevailing winds during the cold weather season.
1. During winter (December-February), the north-east trade winds blow from land to sea over most of India, making it a dry season.
2. However, along the Tamil Nadu coast, these winds blow from sea to land (the Bay of Bengal to the Coromandel Coast), picking up moisture and causing rainfall.
3. Additionally, during the retreating monsoon (October-November), cyclonic depressions from the Bay of Bengal strike the eastern coast, bringing the bulk of Tamil Nadu's annual rainfall.

**Q9.** Explain why rainfall decreases from east to west in the Northern Plains.
**A:**
Rainfall decreases from east to west in the Northern Plains due to the distance from the source of moisture and the progressive weakening of the monsoon.
1. The Bay of Bengal branch of the monsoon enters India from the east, bringing heavy rainfall to West Bengal and Assam (Kolkata receives 162.5 cm annually).
2. As these winds move westward over the plains, they progressively lose moisture. By the time they reach Delhi, rainfall drops significantly (Delhi receives only 67 cm).
3. In western Rajasthan, the winds are almost dry, resulting in scanty rainfall of less than 10 cm. The distance from the sea and the absence of orographic barriers further reduce rainfall in the west.

### 5-Mark Questions

**Q10.** Describe the regional variations in the climatic conditions of India with suitable examples. ⭐
**A:**
India exhibits remarkable regional climatic variations due to its vast latitudinal extent, altitude differences, and relief features.
1. **Temperature variations**: In summer, the mercury reaches 50 degrees C in Rajasthan's Thar Desert, while Pahalgam in Kashmir is around 20 degrees C. In winter, Drass in J&K drops to minus 45 degrees C, while Thiruvananthapuram stays at 22 degrees C. This is due to latitude and altitude differences.
2. **Coastal vs. interior**: Coastal areas like Mumbai (annual range: 24.4 to 30 degrees C) have moderate temperatures, while interior areas like Delhi (14.4 to 33.3 degrees C) and Jodhpur (14.9 to 33.9 degrees C) have extreme ranges due to continentality.
3. **Rainfall variations**: Annual rainfall ranges from over 400 cm in Meghalaya (Mawsynram — world's highest) to less than 10 cm in Ladakh and western Rajasthan, creating flood-prone and drought-prone regions respectively.
4. **Seasonal timing differences**: Most of India receives rain during June-September (advancing monsoon), but the Tamil Nadu coast receives its major rainfall during October-November (retreating monsoon) from cyclonic depressions.
5. **Altitude effects**: High-altitude stations like Leh (3,506 m) remain cool even in summer (maximum 17.2 degrees C) and receive very little rain (8.5 cm), while low-altitude coastal stations like Thiruvananthapuram are warm year-round and receive 181.2 cm of rain.

**Q11.** Give an account of the weather conditions and characteristics of the cold weather season in India.
**A:**
The cold weather season in India lasts from mid-November to February, with December and January being the coldest months.
1. **Temperature pattern**: Temperature decreases from south to north. Chennai averages 24-25 degrees C, while the northern plains range from 10-15 degrees C. Days are warm and nights are cold. Frost is common in the north, and higher Himalayan slopes receive snowfall.
2. **Wind pattern**: North-east trade winds prevail, blowing from land to sea, making most of the country dry. However, the Tamil Nadu coast receives rain as winds blow from sea to land there.
3. **Western disturbances**: A key feature of this season is the inflow of cyclonic disturbances from the Mediterranean Sea and western Asia, which bring winter rain to the northern plains (locally called mahawat) and snowfall in the mountains.
4. **Agricultural importance**: Though the total winter rainfall is small, it is extremely important for the cultivation of rabi crops such as wheat, barley, and mustard.
5. **Regional difference**: The peninsular region does not have a well-defined cold season due to the moderating influence of the sea, and there is hardly any noticeable seasonal change in temperature in coastal areas.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills)

**Q12.** A farmer in Rajasthan is planning to grow wheat. His neighbour suggests he should also install a rainwater harvesting system. Using your knowledge of India's climate, explain why both decisions make sense.
**A:** Both decisions are well-supported by the climatic conditions of Rajasthan.

Rajasthan lies in the interior of India, far from the sea, making it a region of high continentality with extreme temperatures and very low rainfall (less than 10 cm in western Rajasthan). Wheat is a rabi crop that is sown in winter and harvested in spring. During the cold weather season, western cyclonic disturbances bring some winter rain (mahawat) to the northern plains, including parts of Rajasthan. The farmer's decision to grow wheat aligns with the region's rainfall pattern.

A rainwater harvesting system would capture whatever rain falls — whether from western disturbances in winter or occasional monsoon showers — and store it for use during dry periods. This provides a buffer against drought years and reduces the farmer's dependence on the uncertain monsoon. In a region where climate data shows Jodhpur receives only 36.6 cm of rainfall annually (mostly concentrated in July-August), water conservation is not just advisable — it is essential for sustainable farming.
