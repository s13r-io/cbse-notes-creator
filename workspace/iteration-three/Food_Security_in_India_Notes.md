### What This Chapter Is About

Did you know that India produces over 315 million tonnes of foodgrains every year, yet millions of people still go to bed hungry? This chapter explores one of the most important challenges facing any nation: how to make sure every citizen has enough safe and nutritious food at all times. You will learn what **food security** actually means (it is not just about having food -- it is about being able to reach it and afford it), why some groups of people remain vulnerable to hunger despite India becoming self-sufficient in food production, and what the government does through systems like **buffer stocks** and the **Public Distribution System (PDS)** to protect its citizens from starvation.

## Section 3: Core Content

### 3.1 What is Food Security and Why Do We Need It?

**What it is**: Food security means that every person in a country has enough nutritious food to eat at all times -- not just during good seasons, but also during droughts, floods, pandemics, and economic crises. It is built on three pillars: **availability** (enough food is produced or imported), **accessibility** (food is within physical reach of every person), and **affordability** (people have enough money to buy food of acceptable quality).

**How it works**:
1. A country must produce or import sufficient foodgrains to feed its entire population.
2. The food must be distributed so that it reaches every region, including remote and backward areas.
3. Every individual must have the economic means to purchase or access that food.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Food-secure families have better health, children perform better in school, and adults are more productive at work.
- **On the economy/society**: A food-secure nation has a healthier workforce, lower healthcare costs, and greater social stability. Hunger, on the other hand, creates poverty traps -- malnourished children grow into less productive adults.

The need for food security becomes most visible during calamities. When a drought hits, total foodgrain production falls, creating a shortage in affected areas. This shortage pushes prices up. At higher prices, poor families cannot afford to buy food. If the calamity is widespread or prolonged, it leads to starvation and potentially a **famine**. India experienced one of the worst famines in modern history -- the **Bengal Famine of 1943** -- which killed approximately 30 lakh (3 million) people in the province of Bengal. Interestingly, Amartya Sen's research (cited in the NCERT textbook) showed that the Bengal famine was not primarily caused by a shortage of rice production, but rather by failures in distribution and people's inability to access food -- an idea known as the **exchange entitlement approach**. Sen argued that people starve not because there is no food, but because they lose the means to acquire food (through wages, trade, or production).

> **THINK Think About It:** During the COVID-19 lockdown in 2020, millions of migrant workers walked hundreds of kilometres to their villages because they lost their jobs in cities. Using Sen's framework, did these workers lose their "exchange entitlements"? How might this have affected their food security?

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated why food security systems matter even in non-famine times. Restrictions on movement disrupted economic activity, meaning people lost incomes and therefore their ability to afford food. India's existing PDS and additional free foodgrain distribution during the pandemic helped avert widespread hunger.

### 3.2 Who Are Food-Insecure in India?

**What it is**: Food insecurity does not affect all Indians equally. Certain social groups and regions are far more vulnerable because of low incomes, lack of land, seasonal employment, and social discrimination.

**How it works**:
1. In rural areas, the most food-insecure are **landless labourers**, **traditional artisans**, **petty self-employed workers**, and **destitutes** (including beggars).
2. In urban areas, food insecurity affects families whose working members are employed in **ill-paid occupations** and the **casual labour market**, especially those in seasonal work.
3. Socially, **SCs, STs, and some sections of OBCs** with poor land-base or low land productivity are disproportionately affected.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Food-insecure families like Ramu's (a casual agricultural labourer in Raipur) cannot ensure two square meals daily. Ramu is unemployed for 4 months each year due to the seasonal nature of agriculture, during which his children sometimes sleep without food. Milk and vegetables are not a regular part of their meals.
- **On the economy/society**: High food insecurity leads to malnutrition, which reduces workforce productivity and increases healthcare spending. According to the National Health and Family Survey (NHFS) 1998-99, approximately **11 crore women and children** (pregnant and nursing mothers and children under 5) were at risk of malnutrition.

The regions with the largest food-insecure populations are economically backward states with high poverty: **Uttar Pradesh** (eastern and south-eastern parts), **Bihar**, **Jharkhand**, **Orissa**, **West Bengal**, **Chhattisgarh**, parts of **Madhya Pradesh**, and **Maharashtra**. These states share common features: high poverty incidence, large tribal populations, remote areas, and vulnerability to natural disasters.

Hunger in India has two dimensions:

| Type | Definition | Who Suffers | Example |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Chronic Hunger** | Persistent inadequate diet due to very low income | Poor people with irregular or no employment | A landless labourer who can rarely afford a full meal |
| **Seasonal Hunger** | Related to cycles of food growing and harvesting | Rural agricultural labourers during lean seasons; urban casual workers during rainy season | Ramu faces hunger during 4 months of agricultural unemployment |

Data shows positive trends: the percentage of rural households experiencing hunger fell from 18.5% in 1983 to 3.3% in 1999-2000. Urban hunger fell from 6.4% to 0.9% over the same period. However, these figures are from older surveys, and the absolute numbers remain significant given India's large population.

If the government eliminated the PDS tomorrow, what would happen to families like Ahmad's? What does this tell us about the role of government in food security?

### 3.3 India's Food Security System: Buffer Stock and PDS

**What it is**: India's food security system has two main components: **buffer stock** (foodgrains stored by the government through the FCI) and the **Public Distribution System** (distribution of those foodgrains through ration shops at subsidised prices).

**How it works**:
1. The government, through the **Food Corporation of India (FCI)**, purchases wheat and rice from farmers in surplus-producing states.
2. Farmers are paid a pre-announced **Minimum Support Price (MSP)**, declared before each sowing season, which incentivises them to grow these crops.
3. The purchased foodgrains are stored in government granaries as **buffer stock**.
4. The buffer stock is distributed through **Fair Price Shops** (ration shops) to people at an **issue price** that is lower than the market price.
5. Any family with a ration card can buy a stipulated quantity of items (e.g., 35 kg grains, 5 litres kerosene, 5 kg sugar) every month.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: Families below the poverty line can buy food at half or less of market prices. Ahmad's family in Bangalore survives on PDS supplies of wheat, rice, sugar, and kerosene at subsidised rates.
- **On the economy/society**: The PDS has stabilised food prices across the country and averted famines by moving food from surplus to deficit regions. The MSP system has increased foodgrain production and provided income security to farmers.

There are three types of ration cards in India:
- **Antyodaya cards**: For the poorest of the poor (launched under AAY in 2000).
- **BPL cards**: For families below the poverty line (yellow cards).
- **APL cards**: For all others (above poverty line).

| Name of Scheme | Year | Target Group | Entitlement | Issue Price (per kg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PDS (Universal) | Up to 1992 | All citizens | Fixed quota | W: Rs 2.34, R: Rs 2.89 |
| RPDS | 1992 | Remote/backward blocks | 20 kg foodgrains | W: Rs 2.80, R: Rs 3.77 |
| TPDS | 1997/2000 | BPL and APL separately | 35 kg/month | BPL: W 4.15, R 5.65; APL: W 6.10, R 8.30 |
| AAY | 2000/2002 | Poorest of the poor | 35 kg/month/household | W: Rs 2.00, R: Rs 3.00 |
| APS | 2000 | Indigent senior citizens | 10 kg foodgrains | Free |
| NFSA | 2013 | 75% rural + 50% urban | 5 kg per person/month | W: Rs 2.00, R: Rs 3.00, Coarse grains: Rs 1.00 |

The evolution of the PDS shows India's move from universal coverage to targeted programmes. Initially (up to 1992), PDS was universal -- everyone got the same low prices. In 1992, **RPDS** was introduced for 1,700 backward blocks. In 1997, **TPDS** introduced differential pricing for BPL and APL families. In 2000, **AAY** and **Annapurna Scheme (APS)** were launched for the most vulnerable. Finally, the **National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013** consolidated these efforts by covering 75% of the rural and 50% of the urban population.

> **THINK Think About It:** When the PDS was universal, both rich and poor families bought from ration shops. After TPDS, APL families found that prices were almost equal to market prices, so they stopped buying. Is a universal system or a targeted system better for food security? What are the trade-offs?

### 3.4 Green Revolution and India's Self-Sufficiency in Foodgrains

**What it is**: After Independence, India adopted a new agricultural strategy called the **Green Revolution** that dramatically increased foodgrain production, especially wheat and rice, making India self-sufficient in foodgrains.

**How it works**:
1. Post-Independence policy-makers identified food shortage as a critical national challenge, especially after the Bengal Famine.
2. High-yielding variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilisers, and irrigation were introduced, initially for wheat.
3. **Indira Gandhi**, the then Prime Minister, released a special stamp titled "Wheat Revolution" in **July 1968** to mark the success.
4. The success of wheat was later replicated in rice production across the country.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: India's self-sufficiency means that food is available at stable prices in most parts of the country, reducing the risk of famine-like situations.
- **On the economy/society**: Total foodgrain production reached **310 million tonnes in 2020-21** and **315 million tonnes in 2021-22**, rising to **329 million tonnes** by 2023-24. The highest growth was in **Uttar Pradesh** (58 million tonnes) and **Madhya Pradesh** (33 million tonnes) in 2020-21.

| Year | Foodgrain Production (Million Tonnes) | Key Milestone |
|------|--------------------------------------|---------------|
| 2010-11 | 130 | Baseline year |
| 2013-14 | 196 | Crossed 190 MT |
| 2015-16 | 252 | Steady growth |
| 2019-20 | 277 | Pre-pandemic production |
| 2020-21 | 297 | Pandemic year production |
| 2021-22 | 310 | Record production |
| 2023-24 | 329 | Latest estimate |

However, the Green Revolution has both positives and negatives. On the positive side, it ended India's dependence on food imports and famine-like conditions. On the negative side, the intensive use of water for rice cultivation has led to **environmental degradation** and **falling water tables**, especially in Punjab, Haryana, and Western UP. The MSP system has encouraged farmers to shift from **coarse grains** (the staple food of the poor) to wheat and rice, reducing crop diversity.

**Leading foodgrain producing states**: Punjab, Haryana, Andhra Pradesh (procurement); Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh (highest production volumes); West Bengal and UP (significant rice production).

> **WORLD** In Today's World: The ongoing farmer protests in India (2020-2021 and 2024) were partly driven by demands for a legal guarantee on MSP. This connects directly to the food security system -- if farmers are assured fair prices, they will continue producing enough foodgrains for the country.

### 3.5 Problems with the Food Security System

**What it is**: Despite India's large foodgrain production and extensive PDS network, the food security system faces serious challenges including overflowing godowns, rotting grain, diversion of supplies, and regional imbalances in procurement.

**How it works**:
1. The FCI procures foodgrains from surplus states at MSP, but procurement is concentrated in a few prosperous regions: **Punjab, Haryana, Western UP, Andhra Pradesh**, and to a lesser extent **West Bengal**.
2. Procurement is mainly of two crops -- **wheat and rice** -- which encourages farmers to divert land from coarse grains (the poor person's food).
3. Rising MSP increases the government's procurement and storage costs. In 2022, the central pool had **159 LMT wheat and 104 LMT rice** -- far exceeding minimum buffer norms.
4. Excess stock leads to high carrying costs, wastage, and deterioration in grain quality. Some grain rots in godowns or is eaten by rats.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: PDS malpractices directly hurt the poor. Dealers divert grains to the open market for better margins, sell poor quality grains, and open shops irregularly. Families who depend on the PDS may find the shop closed or the grain of unusable quality.
- **On the economy/society**: The economic cost of maintaining excess buffer stocks is enormous. Storage, transportation, and wastage costs burden the exchequer. Meanwhile, per capita rice consumption has actually declined -- rural India's per person monthly rice consumption fell from 6.38 kg (2004-05) to 5.98 kg (2011-12).

Key problems with the PDS:

| Problem | Description | Impact |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **Diversion of grains** | Dealers sell PDS grains in the open market at higher prices | Poor families get less grain than their entitlement |
| **Poor quality grains** | Substandard grain sold at ration shops | Families cannot use the grain even at low prices |
| **Irregular shop opening** | Ration shops not open on scheduled days | Families must make multiple trips or go without |
| **Unsold stocks** | APL families have little incentive to buy since prices are near market rates | Grain piles up with FCI, leading to wastage |
| **Regional imbalance** | Procurement concentrated in few states; eastern states produce less | Food-insecure states may not benefit proportionally |
| **Environmental cost** | Intensive wheat/rice cultivation depletes water tables | Threatens long-term agricultural sustainability in Punjab, Haryana |

> **THINK Think About It:** FCI godowns are overflowing with grain while some families in India still go hungry. Why does this paradox exist? Is the problem one of production, distribution, or both?

### 3.6 Role of Cooperatives and NGOs in Food Security

**What it is**: Beyond government programmes, **cooperative societies** and **NGOs** play a significant role in ensuring food security, particularly in southern and western India.

**How it works**:
1. Cooperative societies set up shops to sell low-priced goods to poor people, supplementing the government PDS.
2. Some cooperatives specialise in specific commodities: milk (Amul, Mother Dairy), grain (ADS Grain Banks).
3. NGOs facilitate community-level food security interventions like grain banks and capacity building programmes.

**Effects**:
- **On individuals/households**: In Tamil Nadu, **94% of Fair Price Shops** are run by cooperatives, making the PDS highly effective in the state. The **Academy of Development Science (ADS)** in Maharashtra has set up grain banks that provide foodgrains to people during lean seasons.
- **On the economy/society**: Amul (Gujarat) brought about the **White Revolution** in milk production, making India the world's largest milk producer. Mother Dairy (Delhi) provides milk and vegetables at government-controlled rates. These cooperatives demonstrate that community-based models can complement government programmes effectively.

The **ADS Grain Bank programme** is acknowledged as a successful and innovative food security intervention. ADS facilitates a network of NGOs for setting up grain banks in different regions of Maharashtra, organises training and capacity building programmes on food security, and works to influence government policy.

| Cooperative / NGO | Location | Contribution to Food Security |
|---|---|---|
| Tamil Nadu Cooperatives | Tamil Nadu | Run 94% of Fair Price Shops in the state |
| Amul | Gujarat | White Revolution in milk; provides affordable milk products |
| Mother Dairy | Delhi | Supplies milk and vegetables at controlled government rates |
| ADS (Academy of Development Science) | Maharashtra | Runs grain banks through NGO network; trains NGOs on food security |

## Section 4: Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

| # | Misconception / Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|------------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Food security just means growing enough food for everyone." | This ignores the other two dimensions. India produces 315+ million tonnes of foodgrains, yet millions remain food-insecure. | Food security has **three** pillars: **availability** (enough food produced), **accessibility** (food within reach), and **affordability** (people can buy it). Production alone is not enough. |
| 2 | "The Bengal Famine of 1943 happened because there was not enough rice." | Data in Table 4.1 shows rice availability in Bengal was not the lowest in 1943 (79 lakh tonnes vs 70 lakh tonnes in 1941). The famine was caused by distribution failure and loss of entitlements. | Amartya Sen's **exchange entitlement approach** shows people starve when they lose the means to acquire food (jobs, trade, production), even if food exists in the market. |
| 3 | "Green Revolution solved India's food problem completely." | While the Green Revolution made India self-sufficient, it created new problems: water depletion, soil damage, inequality between regions, and shift away from coarse grains. | Green Revolution is a **mixed success** -- more food and less famine (positive), but environmental damage and regional inequality (negative). ## Section 5: How This Chapter Connects

### Within Economics
- **The Story of Village Palampur** -- This chapter introduced factors of production (land, labour, capital) and the Green Revolution's impact on farming. Food security builds on this by examining how agricultural surplus translates into food availability for the nation.
- **Poverty as a Challenge** -- The poverty line (2400 calories rural / 2100 calories urban) directly connects to food security. People below the poverty line are the primary target of food security programmes. Understanding poverty helps explain why food insecurity persists despite sufficient production.
- **People as Resource** -- Human capital (health, nutrition, education) depends on food security. Malnourished children cannot learn effectively, and undernourished adults cannot work productively. This chapter shows the practical consequences of investing in human capital.

### Across Subjects
- **Geography -- Climate** -- Natural calamities (droughts, floods) that affect food production are geographical phenomena. Climate change is making these more frequent, threatening food security.
- **Political Science -- Democratic Rights** -- The National Food Security Act, 2013 is a legal right to food. The right to food is part of the broader right to life under Article 21 of the Indian Constitution.
- **Science -- Life Processes / Why Do We Fall Ill** -- Nutrition, balanced diet, and the health consequences of malnutrition are science topics directly linked to food security.

### In the Real World
- **COVID-19 Pandemic (2020-2021)**: India's PDS became a critical lifeline during lockdowns. The government distributed free foodgrains under the Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY) to over 80 crore people, distributing over 1,000 LMT of foodgrains.
- **Global Food Crisis (2022-2023)**: The Russia-Ukraine war disrupted global wheat supplies, pushing food prices up worldwide. Countries with strong food security systems (like India) were better insulated, but even India imposed wheat export restrictions to protect domestic supply.

**Q:** What is buffer stock? Why is it maintained by the government?
**A:**
Buffer stock is the stock of foodgrains (wheat and rice) procured by the government through the Food Corporation of India.
1. It is maintained to distribute foodgrains in **deficit areas** and among the **poorer sections** of society at prices lower than the market price (issue price).
2. It helps resolve the problem of **shortage of food** during adverse weather conditions or during periods of calamity such as droughts and floods.
3. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic, buffer stocks were used to distribute free foodgrains to over 80 crore people under the PMGKAY scheme.

**Q:** Differentiate between chronic hunger and seasonal hunger.
**A:**
Hunger has two main dimensions:
1. **Chronic hunger** is a consequence of diets persistently inadequate in terms of quantity and/or quality. Poor people suffer from it because of their very low income and inability to buy food even for survival.
2. **Seasonal hunger** is related to cycles of food growing and harvesting. It is prevalent in rural areas due to the seasonal nature of agriculture (e.g., Ramu is unemployed for 4 months between sowing and harvesting) and in urban areas among casual construction labourers during the rainy season.

#### 5-Mark Questions

**Q:** Describe the role of the Public Distribution System (PDS) in ensuring food security in India. What are its main problems?
**A:**
The Public Distribution System is the most important instrument of government policy for ensuring food security in India.
1. The PDS distributes foodgrains procured by FCI through **5.5 lakh ration shops** at subsidised prices (issue price) lower than the market price, making food affordable for the poor.
2. It has been **instrumental in averting widespread hunger and famine** by supplying food from surplus regions to deficit ones, and stabilising food prices across the country.
3. The PDS evolved from a **universal system** to a **targeted system**: RPDS (1992) for backward blocks, TPDS (1997) with differential pricing, and AAY (2000) for the poorest of the poor -- covering 2 crore families.
4. The **National Food Security Act, 2013** further expanded coverage to 75% rural and 50% urban population, providing 5 kg per person per month at Rs 2-3/kg.
5. However, PDS faces serious problems: **diversion of grains** to open market, **poor quality grains**, **irregular opening** of shops, and **unsold stocks** because APL prices are near market rates, leading to wastage in FCI godowns.

**Q:** Explain how cooperatives have contributed to food security in India with examples.
**A:**
Cooperatives play an important role in food security, especially in southern and western India, by complementing government efforts.
1. In **Tamil Nadu**, around **94% of all Fair Price Shops** are run by cooperatives, making the PDS highly effective and widely accessible in the state.
2. **Amul** in Gujarat is a success story of cooperative milk production that brought about the **White Revolution**, making India the world's largest milk producer and ensuring availability of affordable milk products.
3. **Mother Dairy** in Delhi supplies milk and vegetables to consumers at controlled rates decided by the Government of Delhi, ensuring affordable nutrition for urban families.
4. The **Academy of Development Science (ADS)** in Maharashtra has set up **grain banks** through a network of NGOs, providing food security during lean seasons and training NGOs on food security practices.
5. These examples show that **community-based cooperative models** can effectively supplement government programmes in achieving food security.

### 2-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q4.** Differentiate between Minimum Support Price (MSP) and Issue Price. ⭐
**A:**
1. **MSP** is the pre-announced price paid by the government to **farmers** for their crops (wheat and rice) before the sowing season to incentivise production. **Issue Price** is the subsidised price at which foodgrains are sold to **consumers** through ration shops.
2. MSP is generally **higher** than market price (to benefit farmers), while Issue Price is **lower** than market price (to benefit consumers). The difference is covered by the government as a subsidy.

**Q5.** Name any two food-insecure states in India and one reason for their food insecurity.
**A:**
1. **Bihar** -- High incidence of poverty and frequent floods make it one of the most food-insecure states in India.
2. **Jharkhand** -- Large tribal population in remote areas with poor land productivity and limited access to PDS contribute to widespread food insecurity.

**Q6.** What are the two components of India's food security system?
**A:**
1. **Buffer Stock**: The stock of foodgrains (wheat and rice) procured by the government through the Food Corporation of India (FCI) and stored in granaries.
2. **Public Distribution System (PDS)**: The system through which procured foodgrains are distributed to poorer sections of society through government-regulated ration shops at subsidised prices.

### 3-Mark Questions (3 questions)

**Q7.** "Food security depends on both the Public Distribution System and government vigilance and action at times of threat." Explain this statement. ⭐
**A:**
Food security is ensured through a combination of systematic distribution and emergency response.
1. The **Public Distribution System** provides a continuous safety net by distributing subsidised foodgrains through 5.5 lakh ration shops across the country, ensuring that poor families can afford food on a regular basis.
2. **Buffer stock** maintained by the FCI allows the government to respond quickly during natural calamities (droughts, floods) or crises (pandemics) by releasing additional foodgrains to affected areas.
3. **Government vigilance** is needed because PDS dealers sometimes divert grains, sell poor quality items, or keep shops closed. Active monitoring and timely action (e.g., launching AAY for the poorest, NFSA 2013 for legal entitlements) are essential to protect the vulnerable.

**Q8.** Explain the problems faced by the Public Distribution System in India.
**A:**
Despite its successes, the PDS faces several significant challenges:
1. **Diversion of grains**: PDS dealers sometimes divert foodgrains to the open market to earn better margins, meaning intended beneficiaries do not receive their full entitlement.
2. **Poor quality and irregularity**: Ration shops often sell inferior quality grains and open irregularly, forcing poor families to make multiple trips or go without supplies.
3. **Unsold APL stocks**: Since APL prices are close to market prices, APL families have little incentive to buy from ration shops, leading to unsold stocks piling up with the FCI and resulting in wastage and rotting grain.

**Q9.** Describe the evolution of the Public Distribution System from a universal system to the National Food Security Act, 2013.
**A:**
The PDS has evolved significantly over the decades to become more targeted and effective:
1. **Universal PDS (up to 1992)**: Initially, PDS had universal coverage with no discrimination between poor and non-poor. Everyone received the same subsidised prices.
2. **RPDS (1992) and TPDS (1997)**: RPDS was introduced in 1,700 backward blocks. TPDS adopted differential pricing for the first time -- separate prices for BPL and APL families.
3. **AAY and APS (2000)**: Special schemes for the poorest of the poor (AAY -- 35 kg at Rs 2-3/kg) and indigent senior citizens (APS -- 10 kg free).
4. **NFSA 2013**: Consolidated all efforts by covering 75% rural and 50% urban population with a legal entitlement of 5 kg per person per month at Rs 2-3/kg, making food security a legal right.

### 5-Mark Questions (2 questions)

**Q10.** How has the Green Revolution contributed to food security in India? What are its negative consequences? ⭐
**A:**
The Green Revolution has been the foundation of India's food security system, though it has also created significant challenges.

1. **Self-sufficiency in foodgrains**: India adopted a new agricultural strategy post-Independence using high-yielding variety seeds, chemical fertilisers, and expanded irrigation. This made India self-sufficient, with production rising from 130 million tonnes (2010-11) to 329 million tonnes (2023-24).

2. **Regional leaders**: Uttar Pradesh produced 58 million tonnes and Madhya Pradesh produced 33 million tonnes in 2020-21. West Bengal and UP were significant rice producers (17 and 16 million tonnes respectively).

3. **Buffer stock creation**: The increased production enabled the government to build large buffer stocks through FCI, which are used to supply foodgrains through PDS at subsidised prices to poor families across India.

4. **Environmental degradation**: Intensive cultivation of wheat and rice, especially in Punjab and Haryana, has led to falling water tables, soil damage, and reduced sustainability of agriculture. Rice cultivation requires enormous amounts of water.

5. **Crop diversity loss**: MSP for wheat and rice incentivised farmers to shift land from coarse grains (staple food of the poor) to these two crops. Procurement is concentrated in a few prosperous states (Punjab, Haryana, Western UP, Andhra Pradesh), creating regional imbalance in agricultural development.

**Q11.** Who are the food-insecure people in India? Describe the factors that make them vulnerable to food insecurity.
**A:**
Food insecurity in India affects specific social groups and regions disproportionately, driven by economic, social, and geographic factors.

1. **Rural food-insecure groups**: Landless labourers with little or no land, traditional artisans, petty self-employed workers, and destitutes including beggars. These groups lack stable income sources and productive assets to ensure regular food supply.

2. **Urban food-insecure groups**: Families whose working members are employed in ill-paid occupations and the casual labour market. Workers like Ahmad (rickshaw puller in Bangalore) have fluctuating daily earnings and no job security.

3. **Socially vulnerable groups**: SCs, STs, and lower OBCs with poor land-base or low land productivity are disproportionately affected. According to NHFS 1998-99, approximately 11 crore women and children (pregnant/nursing mothers and children under 5) were at risk of malnutrition.

4. **Calamity-affected people**: People displaced by natural disasters (droughts, floods) who migrate to other areas in search of work lose their livelihoods and access to food. COVID-19 similarly disrupted incomes and food supply chains.

5. **Geographic concentration**: States like Uttar Pradesh (eastern/south-eastern), Bihar, Jharkhand, Orissa, West Bengal, Chhattisgarh, parts of MP and Maharashtra have the largest food-insecure populations due to high poverty, tribal areas, and vulnerability to natural disasters.

### HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills) (1 question)

**Q12.** Table 4.1 in the NCERT textbook shows that rice availability in Bengal was 70 lakh tonnes in 1941 but 79 lakh tonnes in 1943 (the famine year). Yet 30 lakh people died in 1943. How is this possible? What does this tell us about the nature of food security?
**A:**
This paradox is explained by Amartya Sen's **exchange entitlement approach** to understanding famines. Sen argued that famines are caused not merely by a decline in food availability, but by a failure in people's ability to acquire food through legitimate means (wages, trade, production, or entitlement). In 1943, Bengal was affected by World War II -- the British government diverted food supplies for military use, prices skyrocketed due to inflation, and people's wages did not keep pace. Even though food existed in the market, ordinary people (especially rural labourers and artisans) could no longer afford to buy it. Their "exchange entitlements" -- the goods they could command through their labour, trade, or production -- collapsed.

This tells us that food security is not just about production (availability); it is equally about **accessibility** (food being within physical reach) and **affordability** (people having the economic means to buy food). A country can have sufficient food in absolute terms and still experience famine if distribution fails or if people lose their ability to pay for food. This is why India's PDS is so important -- it directly addresses affordability by selling foodgrains at subsidised prices, and accessibility by maintaining ration shops in remote areas.

### Case-Based / Source-Based Questions (1 question, 4 marks)

**Q13.** Read the following passage and answer the questions that follow:

*Ramu works as a casual labourer in agriculture in Raipur village. His eldest son Somu who is 10 years old also works as a pali to look after cattle of the Sarpanch. Ramu has three more sons and two daughters but they are too young to work. His wife Sunhari works part-time as a house cleaner for livestock, earning half litre milk and some cooked food daily. Agriculture being a seasonal activity employs Ramu only during sowing, transplanting and harvesting. He remains unemployed for about 4 months in a year. During those months, his small kids sometimes have to sleep without food.*

**13a.** Identify the type of hunger Ramu's family experiences. (1 mark)

**13b.** Which government system could help Ramu's family during the 4 months of unemployment? Explain how. (1 mark)

**13c.** Analyse why Ramu's family is food-insecure despite multiple members working. What does this suggest about the relationship between employment and food security? (2 marks)

**A13a:** Ramu's family experiences **seasonal hunger**. This is because the hunger is not year-round but occurs specifically during the 4 months when Ramu is unemployed due to the seasonal nature of agricultural activity (between sowing and harvesting periods).

**A13b:** The **Public Distribution System (PDS)** could help Ramu's family during the lean months. If Ramu's family has a BPL ration card, they can buy subsidised foodgrains (wheat, rice, sugar, kerosene) from the nearest Fair Price Shop at prices much lower than the market, ensuring basic food supply even without income.

**A13c:**
Ramu's family is food-insecure despite multiple members working because:
1. **Low and irregular wages**: Ramu earns only enough for bare survival even during working months, and his wife's income is in-kind (milk and food) rather than cash. Their 10-year-old son's annual income is just Rs 1,000, showing extreme poverty.
2. **Seasonal nature of employment**: Despite having multiple working members, all their work (agriculture, cattle care, house cleaning) is linked to the agricultural cycle. When farming stops for 4 months, all income sources dry up simultaneously.

This suggests that **employment alone does not guarantee food security** -- what matters is stable, year-round employment with adequate wages. Food security requires both income security (regular employment) and a safety net (PDS) for when employment fails, as it does for Ramu's family.
