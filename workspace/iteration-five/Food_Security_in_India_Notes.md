## Core Content

### What is Food Security?

Food is as essential for living as air is for breathing. But **food security** means something more than getting two square meals. It has three dimensions:

**Availability of food** — Food production within the country, food imports, and previous years' stock stored in government granaries. India must grow or import enough to feed 1.4 billion people.

**Accessibility** — Food is within reach of every person. There should be no physical barriers — no remote village left out, no disaster-struck area cut off from supply.

**Affordability** — An individual has enough money to buy sufficient, safe and nutritious food to meet their dietary needs. A family may live near a market full of food but still go hungry if they can't afford it.

Food security is ensured only when all three conditions are met simultaneously: (1) enough food is available for all persons, (2) all persons have the capacity to buy food of acceptable quality, and (3) there is no barrier on access to food.

> **THINK Think About It:** India is among the world's largest producers of wheat and rice. Yet millions of Indians go hungry. How is this possible? What does this tell you about the relationship between production and food security?

### Why Food Security Matters

**The Calamity Chain**: Drought → Crop failure → Food shortage → Prices rise → Poor can't afford food → Starvation → Famine

The poorest section of society is food insecure most of the time. But even people above the poverty line can become food insecure during a national disaster — earthquake, drought, flood, tsunami, or widespread crop failure.

Here's how a calamity destroys food security step by step:
1. A natural calamity (say, drought) reduces total foodgrain production
2. The shortage of food pushes prices up in affected areas
3. At higher prices, poor families cannot afford to buy food
4. If the calamity is widespread or prolonged, it causes **starvation**
5. Massive starvation leads to **famine** — characterised by widespread deaths, epidemics from contaminated water or decaying food, and loss of body resistance

**The Bengal Famine of 1943** is the most devastating famine in India's recorded history — it killed **30 lakh (3 million) people** in the province of Bengal. Study Table 4.1 from the textbook: food availability actually dropped from 92 lakh tonnes in 1942 to 79 lakh tonnes in 1943, but the famine was caused not just by production decline — it was made worse by wartime policies, hoarding, and unequal distribution.

| Year | Production (Lakh tonnes) | Imports (Lakh tonnes) | Total Availability (Lakh tonnes) |
|------|--------------------------|-----------------------|----------------------------------|
| 1942 | 93 | 01 | 92 |
| 1943 | 76 | 03 | 79 |

*Source: Sen, A.K, 1981*

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** The COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21) disrupted food security globally. Lockdowns restricted movement of people, goods, and services — hitting daily-wage workers hardest. India's government expanded PDS to provide free foodgrains to 80 crore people under the Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana.

Nothing like the Bengal famine has happened in India since Independence, thanks to the food security system described in this chapter. However, famine-like conditions still exist in some parts of the country, leading to starvation deaths at times.

### Who Are Food-Insecure?

**The Most Vulnerable Groups**:

| Group | Why They're Vulnerable |
|-------|----------------------|
| Landless labourers | No land to grow food; depend on seasonal agricultural work |
| Traditional artisans | Declining demand for handmade goods; irregular income |
| Petty self-employed workers | Street vendors, domestic workers — low and unstable earnings |
| Destitutes (beggars) | No regular income source at all |
| Casual labourers (urban) | Employed in ill-paid, seasonal work; no job security |
| SC/ST and lower OBCs | Poor land-base or very low land productivity |
| Pregnant/nursing mothers & children under 5 | High nutritional needs but low access — ~11 crore affected (NHFS 1998-99) |
| Disaster-affected migrants | Forced to leave homes; lose livelihood and social networks |

**Rural vs Urban Food Insecurity**:

| Aspect | Rural Food Insecure | Urban Food Insecure |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Primary cause | Seasonal agriculture — no work during non-harvest months | Ill-paid casual labour — no job security |
| Employment type | Agricultural labour, brick laying, construction | Rickshaw pulling, domestic work, daily-wage construction |
| Supplement | Some families grow their own food or receive payment in kind | No option to grow food; entirely dependent on cash income |
| States most affected | UP (eastern/SE), Bihar, Jharkhand, Orissa, West Bengal, Chhattisgarh, parts of MP and Maharashtra | Same states — food insecurity follows poverty |

**Social composition matters**: SCs, STs, and lower OBCs are disproportionately affected because they have either poor land-base or very low land productivity. Women face higher malnutrition — a serious concern because it puts even unborn babies at risk of malnutrition.

### Hunger: Chronic vs Seasonal

Hunger is not just an expression of poverty — it **brings about** poverty. A hungry worker is less productive, earns less, and stays poor. This creates a cycle: poverty → hunger → lower productivity → less income → more poverty.

**Chronic Hunger**: A consequence of diets persistently inadequate in quantity and/or quality. Poor people suffer from chronic hunger because of their very low income and inability to buy food even for survival. This is a permanent, year-round condition.

**Seasonal Hunger**: Related to cycles of food growing and harvesting. In rural areas, it occurs because agricultural work is seasonal (sowing, transplanting, harvesting — then months of unemployment). In urban areas, casual construction labourers face it during the rainy season when outdoor work stops.

| Year | Seasonal (Rural) | Chronic (Rural) | Total (Rural) | Seasonal (Urban) | Chronic (Urban) | Total (Urban) |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|--------------|-----------------|----------------|--------------|
| 1983 | 16.2% | 2.3% | 18.5% | 5.6% | 0.8% | 6.4% |
| 1993-94 | 4.2% | 0.9% | 5.1% | 1.1% | 0.5% | 1.6% |
| 1999-2000 | 2.6% | 0.7% | 3.3% | 0.6% | 0.3% | 0.9% |

*Source: Sagar (2004)*

**What this data tells us**: Both seasonal and chronic hunger have declined significantly in India. Rural seasonal hunger dropped from 16.2% to 2.6% between 1983 and 2000. Urban hunger is consistently lower than rural. This improvement is largely due to the Green Revolution increasing food production and government schemes like PDS and mid-day meals.

### The Green Revolution — India's Path to Self-Sufficiency

**Why it Started**: After Independence, India was not self-sufficient in foodgrains and depended on imports and aid (PL-480 from the US). Famine-like conditions were common. Policy-makers adopted all measures to achieve self-sufficiency.

**Sequence of Events**:
1. **1960s**: India adopted a new agricultural strategy — high-yielding variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilisers, and irrigation
2. **1968**: Prime Minister Indira Gandhi released a special stamp entitled "Wheat Revolution" to mark impressive strides in wheat production
3. **1970s-80s**: Success in wheat was replicated in rice; foodgrain production increased steadily
4. **2010-2023**: Foodgrain production grew from 130 million tonnes (2010-11) to 329 million tonnes (2023-24)

| Year | Production (Million Tonnes) |
|------|---------------------------|
| 2010-11 | 130 |
| 2015-16 | 252 |
| 2020-21 | 297 |
| 2021-22 | 310 |
| 2023-24 | 329 |

*Source: Department of Agriculture & Farmers Welfare, Economic Survey 2023-24*

**Before vs After the Green Revolution**:

| Aspect | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Foodgrain production | ~50 million tonnes (1950s) | 329 million tonnes (2023-24) |
| Food imports | Depended on US PL-480 aid | Net exporter of foodgrains |
| Famine risk | Regular famine-like conditions | No famine since Independence |
| Crop focus | Mixed (coarse grains included) | Wheat and rice dominant |
| Regional benefit | Uniformly low | Concentrated in Punjab, Haryana, Western UP |

**Problems and Limitations**:
- The increase was **disproportionate** — wheat and rice benefited most, while coarse grains (staple food of the poor) were neglected
- Procurement is concentrated in a few prosperous regions (Punjab, Haryana, Western UP, Andhra Pradesh)
- MSP incentivised farmers to shift from coarse grains to rice and wheat — reducing crop diversity
- Intensive water use for rice led to **environmental degradation** and falling water tables, threatening long-term agricultural sustainability

> **THINK Think About It:** The Green Revolution made India self-sufficient in foodgrains, but it also created new problems — water depletion, soil damage, and inequality. Was the trade-off worth it? Can you think of an alternative path India could have taken?

### Buffer Stock and the Public Distribution System

India's food security system has two main components:

#### Buffer Stock

**What it is**: The stock of foodgrains (wheat and rice) procured by the government through the **Food Corporation of India (FCI)**.

**How it works**:
1. FCI purchases wheat and rice from farmers in states with surplus production
2. Farmers are paid the **Minimum Support Price (MSP)** — a pre-announced price declared before the sowing season
3. The purchased foodgrains are stored in government granaries
4. During shortages or calamities, grain is distributed to deficit areas at **Issue Price** (lower than market price)

**Why it matters**: Buffer stock resolves the mismatch between where food is produced (surplus states like Punjab, Haryana) and where it's needed (deficit and disaster-affected areas). It also stabilises prices — when market prices rise during shortages, the government releases buffer stock to bring prices down.

> **⚠️ Watch Out:** Students often confuse MSP with Issue Price. MSP is what the government **pays farmers** (high, to incentivise production). Issue Price is what consumers **pay at ration shops** (low, to make food affordable). The difference is covered by the government as a subsidy.

#### Public Distribution System (PDS)

**What it is**: The system through which FCI-procured foodgrains are distributed to the poorer sections of society through government-regulated ration shops (also called **Fair Price Shops**).

**Key facts**:
- About **5.5 lakh** ration shops across India
- Items sold: foodgrains, sugar, kerosene
- Three types of ration cards determine eligibility and prices:

| Card Type | Target Group | Price Level |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Antyodaya** (AAY) | Poorest of the poor | Lowest — wheat Rs 2/kg, rice Rs 3/kg |
| **BPL** (Below Poverty Line) | Below poverty line families | Subsidised — wheat Rs 4.15/kg, rice Rs 5.65/kg |
| **APL** (Above Poverty Line) | All other families | Near market price — wheat Rs 6.10/kg, rice Rs 8.30/kg |

> **THINK Think About It:** Ahmad has a BPL (yellow) card and gets wheat and rice at half the market price. Without it, his irregular rickshaw earnings wouldn't feed his large family. Imagine if PDS didn't exist — what would happen to families like Ahmad's?

### Evolution of the Public Distribution System

The PDS has evolved through several phases, each expanding or restructuring the system:

| Scheme | Year | Target Group | Key Feature | Issue Price |
|--------|------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| PDS | Up to 1992 | Universal | No discrimination — same price for all | W-2.34, R-2.89/kg |
| RPDS | 1992 | Backward blocks | Targeted 1,700 backward blocks | W-2.80, R-3.77/kg |
| TPDS | 1997 (launched 2000) | BPL and APL | Differential pricing for poor vs non-poor | BPL: W-4.15, R-5.65; APL: W-6.10, R-8.30 |
| AAY | 2000 | Poorest of the poor | 35 kg at Rs 2-3/kg | W-2.00, R-3.00/kg |
| APS | 2000 | Indigent senior citizens | 10 kg free | Free |
| NFSA | 2013 | 75% rural + 50% urban | 5 kg/person/month as legal right | W-2.00, R-3.00, Coarse grains-1.00 |

**Additional food security programmes**:
- **Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS)** — 1975: nutrition for children under 6 and pregnant/nursing mothers
- **Mid-Day Meal Scheme** — free meals in government schools to improve nutrition and attendance
- **Food-for-Work (FFW)** — 1977-78: employment programmes that provide food as wages
- **Various Poverty Alleviation Programmes (PAPs)** — mostly rural, with food components

### Problems with PDS and the Role of Cooperatives

**Problems with PDS**:

Despite its success, the PDS faces several serious problems:

1. **Diversion of grains**: PDS dealers sometimes divert subsidised grains to the open market for higher profit margins
2. **Poor quality grains**: Ration shops regularly stock low-quality foodgrains that people don't want to buy
3. **Irregular opening**: Shops don't open on schedule, forcing families to make multiple trips
4. **Unsold stock accumulation**: When families don't buy from ration shops (APL price is near market price), grain piles up with the FCI — leading to wastage and rotting
5. **Excess procurement**: FCI godowns overflow because procurement is concentrated in few states (Punjab, Haryana, AP) for mainly wheat and rice
6. **Environmental cost**: MSP-driven rice cultivation uses intensive irrigation, causing water table depletion and soil degradation in Punjab and Haryana
7. **High carrying cost**: Storage of massive food stocks costs the government heavily in transportation, warehousing, and wastage

**What the data shows**: Despite producing 310+ million tonnes of foodgrains (2020-21), per capita consumption of rice has actually **declined** — from 6.38 kg/person/month (2004-05) to 5.98 kg (2011-12) in rural India. This means people are eating less rice even as the country produces more — suggesting distribution problems, not production problems.

**Role of Cooperatives in Food Security**:

Cooperatives play an important role, especially in southern and western India:

| Cooperative | Region | Contribution |
|------------|--------|-------------|
| Tamil Nadu cooperatives | Tamil Nadu | Run 94% of all Fair Price Shops in the state |
| Mother Dairy | Delhi | Provides milk and vegetables at government-controlled prices |
| Amul | Gujarat | Cooperative dairy that led India's **White Revolution** in milk production |
| ADS (Academy of Development Science) | Maharashtra | Runs grain banks through NGO network — successful food security innovation |

**Why cooperatives matter**: They cut out middlemen, reduce costs, and are more accountable to local communities than government-run shops. The success of Amul and Tamil Nadu's cooperative ration shops shows that locally-managed food distribution can be more effective than top-down government systems.

> **[WORLD] In Today's World:** India runs the world's largest food subsidy programme. In 2020, under the Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana, the government provided free foodgrains to approximately 80 crore people for several months during COVID-19 — a scale unmatched by any other country.

## Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

| # | Misconception / Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---|------------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Food security means growing enough food" | Production is only one of three dimensions | Food security = Availability + Accessibility + Affordability. India produces enough food but millions still go hungry — that's an affordability and distribution problem, not a production problem |
| 2 | "Only poor people are food insecure" | Calamities can make anyone food insecure | A middle-class family in a flood-affected area can become food insecure overnight. Poverty makes you chronically insecure; disasters make anyone temporarily insecure |
| 3 | "Buffer stock and PDS are the same thing" | They serve different functions | Buffer stock = storage (FCI buys and stores grain). PDS = distribution (ration shops sell grain to people). Buffer stock is the supply side; PDS is the demand side |
| 4 | "MSP is the price people pay at ration shops" | MSP is what farmers receive, not what consumers pay | MSP (Minimum Support Price) = what government pays farmers. Issue Price = what consumers pay at ration shops. The difference is the subsidy |
| 5 | "The Green Revolution solved India's food problem completely" | It increased production but created new problems | Green Revolution made India self-sufficient in wheat and rice. But it ignored coarse grains (poor people's staple), caused environmental damage (water depletion, soil degradation), and increased regional inequality |
| 6 | "Chronic hunger and seasonal hunger are the same" | They have completely different causes and durations | Chronic hunger = permanent, year-round, caused by poverty. Seasonal hunger = temporary, cyclical, caused by unemployment during non-harvest months. Different causes → different solutions |

### 3-Mark Questions

**Q18.** Explain how a natural calamity can lead to famine.
**A:**
A natural calamity (like drought) can lead to famine through a chain:
1. Calamity reduces total foodgrain production, creating a shortage in affected areas.
2. The shortage pushes prices up, making food unaffordable for the poor.
3. If the calamity is widespread or prolonged, people face starvation. Massive starvation leads to famine — characterised by widespread deaths and epidemics from contaminated food and water.
4. The Bengal Famine of 1943 is an example where reduced food availability, wartime policies, and unequal distribution combined to kill 30 lakh people.

**Q19.** What are the problems faced by the Public Distribution System?
**A:**
1. **Diversion of grains**: PDS dealers divert subsidised grains to the open market for better margins.
2. **Poor quality**: Ration shops stock low-quality grains that people don't want to buy.
3. **Irregular opening**: Shops don't open on schedule, forcing repeated trips.
4. **Unsold stock accumulation**: High APL prices mean fewer purchases, leading to grain piling up with FCI and wastage.
5. **Environmental cost**: MSP-driven procurement of rice and wheat has caused water depletion and soil degradation in surplus states.

**Q20.** "Cooperatives have played a significant role in food security in India." Support this statement with examples.
**A:**
1. **Tamil Nadu**: 94% of Fair Price Shops are run by cooperatives, ensuring efficient and reliable distribution.
2. **Mother Dairy (Delhi)**: Provides milk and vegetables at controlled prices, making these affordable for urban consumers.
3. **Amul (Gujarat)**: A cooperative dairy that led India's White Revolution, making India the world's largest milk producer.

### 5-Mark Questions

**Q21.** How is food security ensured in India? Describe the role of buffer stock and PDS.
**A:**
India ensures food security through a carefully designed system with two main components:

**Buffer Stock**:
1. The Food Corporation of India (FCI) procures wheat and rice from farmers in surplus states at Minimum Support Price (MSP).
2. This grain is stored in government granaries.
3. During shortages or calamities, it is distributed to deficit areas at Issue Price (lower than market price).
4. Buffer stock stabilises prices and ensures availability during adverse conditions.

**Public Distribution System (PDS)**:
1. FCI-procured foodgrains are distributed through 5.5 lakh ration shops (Fair Price Shops).
2. Three types of ration cards — Antyodaya (poorest), BPL, and APL — determine eligibility and prices.
3. Items sold include foodgrains, sugar, and kerosene at subsidised rates.
4. The PDS has evolved from universal (1940s) to targeted (TPDS, 1997) to a legal right (NFSA, 2013).

**Conclusion**: Together, buffer stock ensures availability, while PDS ensures accessibility and affordability — the three pillars of food security.

**Q22.** Describe the impact of the Green Revolution on food security in India. What were its positive and negative effects?
**A:**

**Why it started**: After Independence, India was not self-sufficient in foodgrains and depended on imports. Policy-makers needed a strategy to feed a growing population.

**Positive effects**:
1. India became self-sufficient in wheat and rice — foodgrain production grew from 130 million tonnes (2010-11) to 329 million tonnes (2023-24).
2. States like Uttar Pradesh (36 million tonnes of wheat) and Madhya Pradesh (18 million tonnes) became major producers.
3. Hunger declined — seasonal hunger in rural areas dropped from 16.2% (1983) to 2.6% (2000).

**Negative effects**:
1. **Crop imbalance**: Focus on wheat and rice led to neglect of coarse grains (staple food of the poor).
2. **Regional inequality**: Benefits concentrated in Punjab, Haryana, Western UP — surplus states. Other regions were left behind.
3. **Environmental damage**: Intensive irrigation for rice caused water table depletion and soil degradation in Punjab and Haryana.
4. **Excess procurement**: FCI godowns overflow with wheat and rice, leading to wastage and high carrying costs.

### Case-Based Question

**Q23.** Read the following passage and answer the questions:

*Ramu works as a casual labourer in Raipur village in Chhattisgarh. His family of 7 depends on his seasonal agricultural earnings. He is unemployed for about 4 months every year. His wife Sunhari supplements the income by working as a house cleaner. During unemployment, his children sometimes sleep without food. Milk and vegetables are not a regular part of their meals. Ahmad, a rickshaw puller in Bangalore, has a BPL ration card that provides wheat, rice, sugar, and kerosene at half the market price. His family of 8 survives on his fluctuating daily earnings plus PDS supplies.*

**23a.** Identify the type of hunger Ramu's family experiences. (1 mark)
**23b.** Why is Ahmad's family better off than Ramu's despite similar low incomes? (1 mark)
**23c.** What government interventions could help Ramu's family achieve food security? Explain with reference to two specific schemes. (2 marks)

**A23a:** Ramu's family experiences **seasonal hunger** — hunger that occurs during specific months when Ramu cannot find agricultural work. This is different from chronic hunger (which is year-round) because Ramu can feed his family during harvest season.

**A23b:** Ahmad's family is better off because Ahmad has a **BPL ration card** that provides subsidised foodgrains (wheat, rice, sugar, kerosene) at half the market price through the PDS. This means even on days when Ahmad earns very little from rickshaw-pulling, his family can still access basic food supplies from the ration shop. Ramu has no such safety net.

**A23c:**
1. **Antyodaya Anna Yojana (AAY)**: Ramu's family, being among the poorest with seasonal unemployment, would qualify for the AAY card, providing 35 kg of foodgrains per month at Rs 2/kg (wheat) and Rs 3/kg (rice). This would ensure a minimum food supply even during unemployment months.
2. **MGNREGA (Employment Guarantee)**: This scheme guarantees 100 days of wage employment per year to rural households. During the 4 months when Ramu is unemployed, MGNREGA would provide him work and income, reducing the period of food insecurity. It directly addresses the root cause — seasonal unemployment.

### Higher-Order Analysis

**Q24.** "India produces enough foodgrains to feed its population, yet hunger persists." Analyse this paradox by identifying at least three reasons why production alone does not ensure food security.
**A:**
This paradox exists because food security depends on three dimensions, not just production:

1. **Affordability gap**: Even when food is available, the poorest households cannot afford it. A daily-wage labourer earning Rs 200/day may not have enough to buy sufficient nutritious food for a family of 6, even though the local market is well-stocked.

2. **Distribution failure**: Food is concentrated in surplus states (Punjab, Haryana, AP) while hunger is worst in states like Bihar, Jharkhand, and MP. The PDS is supposed to bridge this gap but faces problems — diversion of grains, poor quality, irregular shop openings — that prevent food from reaching the neediest.

3. **Wrong crops produced**: MSP incentivises wheat and rice, but the poor depend on cheaper coarse grains. When farmers shift from coarse grains to rice/wheat, the poor's staple food becomes less available and more expensive.

4. **Seasonal employment**: Agricultural labourers like Ramu get work only during sowing and harvesting months. For 4 months, they have no income and cannot buy food — regardless of how much the country produced that year.

**Conclusion**: Food security requires production PLUS distribution PLUS purchasing power. India has solved the first but struggles with the second and third.

**Q25.** A section of people in India are still without food. Explain why this happens despite India being self-sufficient in foodgrain production.
**A:**
Despite India producing 329 million tonnes of foodgrains (2023-24) and being self-sufficient, food insecurity persists because:

1. **Unequal distribution**: Food is produced in surplus states (Punjab, Haryana, Western UP, AP) but the hungry live in backward states (Bihar, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, MP). The PDS, which should bridge this gap, suffers from diversion, poor quality, and irregular operations.

2. **Affordability**: Producing enough food nationally doesn't mean every household can afford it. The poorest — landless labourers, casual workers, SC/ST families — earn too little to buy sufficient food even at subsidised prices.

3. **Targeting issues**: The shift from universal to targeted PDS means APL families (marginally above poverty line) get food at near-market prices, leaving them with little incentive to buy from ration shops. This creates a gap between BPL and APL families.

4. **Coarse grain neglect**: MSP-driven production focuses on wheat and rice, but the poor traditionally depend on cheaper coarse grains. As coarse grain production declines, the staple food of the poor becomes less available.

5. **Seasonal unemployment**: Even with adequate national production, families dependent on seasonal agricultural work go hungry during non-harvest months because they have no income — not because food is unavailable.
